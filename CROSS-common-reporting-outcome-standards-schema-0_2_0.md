# CROSS: Common Reporting Outcome Standards Schema

Version 0.5.1 | 2026-06-08 | CC0

---

## Purpose

CROSS specifies what funded interventions are obligated to demonstrate. It provides grants ecosystems with a portable obligation schema that accommodates multiple obligation modes, calibrates evidentiary pressure to program context, and generates structured reporting data that is comparable across funders, rounds, and grantees.

CROSS recognizes three distinct obligation modes: build obligation, change obligation, and retroactive obligation. The Theory of Build is a failure mode only within a change-obligation round; it is a correct and complete specification within a build-obligation round. Evaluation is structured through a gate architecture with four named gate types whose evidence requirements are configured by the funder and published before any round opens. A program-level continuation gate determines progression across rounds and stages, supporting the staged program designs that major public goods funders use.

CROSS is not a process standard. It does not specify how rounds are structured, how funds are distributed, or how funding decisions are made. CROSS specifies the content of obligation: what applicants must demonstrate, what funders must evaluate, what grantees must report, and how evidentiary pressure is structured across the funding lifecycle.

Funder obligation maturation is trackable through CROSS. A funder operating within a conformant program records their gate configuration for each round. Funders who maintain a versioned round record can track gate configurations across rounds, making funder maturation visible to the field over time.

---

## Scope

CROSS applies to any grants program that makes funding decisions based on expected or demonstrated contribution. It is designed for adoption by public goods funders in the web3 ecosystem (Octant, Gitcoin, Stellar Development Foundation, and similar programs) and is portable to grant-making contexts outside web3. Any organization that funds interventions and expects grantees to be obligated to demonstrate results can adopt CROSS independently of any other standard or protocol.

Runbooks providing pre-built configurations for common program types are companion documents to this standard. A funder adopting CROSS may select a runbook as a starting configuration or build a custom configuration from the gate architecture specification in Part IV.

CROSS imposes obligations on concurrent funding attribution as well as on applicants and reviewers. Where concurrent funding is disclosed under Part VI, scope attribution and outcome credit obligations are specified in Part VI-A.

CROSS imposes obligations on funders as well as on applicants, grantees, and reviewers. Funder obligations and redress mechanisms are specified in Part XI.

## Part I: Independence, CRAFT Inheritance, and Suite References

CROSS is an independent standard. It is not a document of any specific grants program or protocol. Any grants ecosystem may adopt CROSS in whole or in part without adopting any other standard it references.

**Format agnosticism.** CROSS specifies what must be done, not how it must be done. Content requirements are stated throughout this standard. Format requirements are not. Any tool, platform, infrastructure, or data format that satisfies the content requirements is conformant with this standard. CROSS is designed to be compatible with many formats and systems, not to require any specific one. Where this standard references a specific format, platform, or protocol as an example of how a content requirement can be satisfied, that reference is illustrative, not prescriptive. A program that satisfies the same content requirement through a different format, platform, or protocol is equally conformant. The compatibility statements published alongside this standard show how CROSS content maps to specific formats and systems for programs that use them; those compatibility mappings do not obligate any program to adopt those formats or systems.

CROSS is a domain application of CRAFT (Chains Reveal Attested Falsifiable Truth), the meta-standard for evaluation chain legibility. CRAFT specifies what any evaluation chain requires to be relied on for decisions: six conditions running from decision context through feedback, and a construction grammar for the standards that specialize it to a domain. CROSS is the first such specialization, derived from inside analysis of grant evaluation failure modes. It inherits CRAFT's six conditions and adds the grant-domain requirements stated throughout this standard; where CROSS is stricter than CRAFT, the stricter requirement prevails, and CROSS introduces nothing that contradicts a CRAFT requirement.

This inheritance is structural, not an adoption burden placed on programs. A program adopts CROSS and thereby satisfies CRAFT's conditions for its grant evaluations, with no separate need to implement CRAFT. The full inheritance, which CRAFT conditions CROSS realizes and how, which Coordination Structural Integrity Suite standards it rests on, and the Frame Language vocabulary discipline it follows, is declared in the CRAFT inheritance receipt in Part XIII.

CROSS incorporates requirements from the following Coordination Structural Integrity Suite standards by reference. Adopters of CROSS inherit the requirements of these standards in the contexts specified below. They do not need to implement the full Suite to use CROSS.

The Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard applies to adverse signal disclosure. Applicants must surface signals that contradict their self-presentation: prior grant rejections from similar programs, contradictory technical findings, negative due diligence results from other funders. Reviewers must not suppress adverse signals surfaced during evaluation.

The Information Asymmetry Classification Standard applies to concurrent funding disclosure. Undisclosed concurrent funding is an omission asymmetry under the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard. The disclosure requirement in Part VI of this standard instantiates the omission class in the grant application context.

The Precision-First Design Standard applies to indicator sourcing and the double-negation invariant. Documentation requirements must not be so light that obligation is absent, and must not be so heavy that genuine small-team applicants are excluded. The proportionality requirements in Part IV are a Precision-First Design Standard corollary.

The Coordination Scaling Standard determines which evidence tier applies based on the scale and public impact claims of the application. Small technical teams with clear diagnoses do not require the same obligation machinery as large institutional applications. The Coordination Scaling Standard provides the calibration.

The Regenerative Obligation Standard and CROSS are structurally paired. The Regenerative Obligation Standard handles direction: how internal extraction and return mechanics operate within a funding mechanism. CROSS handles destination: what funded interventions must produce. A funding mechanism can satisfy the Regenerative Obligation Standard and fail CROSS, or satisfy CROSS and fail the Regenerative Obligation Standard. These are independent evaluations.

CROSS was developed using Frame Language as a conceptual tool. Frame Language is not a formal dependency of this standard. Adopters of CROSS are not required to use, cite, or license Frame Language.

## Part II: Independent Obligation Dimensions

CROSS specifies multiple obligation dimensions. Each dimension has its own triggering logic and its own tier structure. Dimensions do not scale together. A small grant does not automatically receive relaxed standards on all dimensions. A large grant does not automatically receive heavier requirements on all dimensions. Each dimension is assessed independently based on its own trigger.

The following dimensions are specified in this standard. Additional dimensions may be activated by funder configuration through any conformant configuration tool.

**Outcome documentation rigor.** Triggered by: funding amount and the scale of the public impact claim. The Coordination Scaling Standard provides the calibration. Small-team applications with specific diagnoses and modest claims require less formal documentation than large institutional applications with broad population impact claims.

**Institutional capacity.** Triggered by: the scope of the proposed work relative to the applying organization's demonstrated track record. A team proposing a \$150,000 multi-year infrastructure project with no prior grant history faces a higher institutional capacity bar than a team with ten years of verified outputs proposing a \$20,000 continuation. Capacity assessment is not a disqualifier; it calibrates the disbursement conditions tier.

**Conflict of interest.** Triggered by: the nature of relationships between decision-makers and applicants, and between applicants and committee members. Three tiers apply: categorical bar (no waiver), qualified waiver, and disclosure-encouraged. Specified in full in Part VII.

**Concurrent funding disclosure.** Triggered by: whether the applicant has active grants, investments, or revenue sources related to the scope of the application in the prior 24 months. Required field for all applications, across all obligation modes. Non-disclosure is a disqualifier; disclosed concurrent funding is not.

**Adverse signal disclosure.** Triggered by: whether any adverse signals exist relating to the application. Applicants must disclose prior rejections from similar programs, contradictory technical findings, and negative due diligence results from other funders. Reviewers must not suppress adverse signals found during evaluation.

**Open source and intellectual property.** Triggered by: whether the funded work produces code, data, research, or other artifacts, and what the public goods claim rests on. For work claiming a public goods benefit through open access to its outputs, those outputs must be demonstrably open at the time of reporting, not only at the time of application.

Where the funded work produces research outputs, models, datasets, or novel technical artifacts for which intellectual property rights are material, the applicant must submit an IP ownership declaration specifying: who holds rights in the outputs, under what terms those rights are held, and what the public's rights of access and use are. The declaration must be specific enough to answer whether a third party could use, fork, or build upon the outputs without seeking permission from any rights holder. An applicant who holds exclusive rights over outputs claimed as public goods must reconcile those rights with the public goods claim in the additionality declaration (Part VI-A). For programs funding decentralized science, AI model development, or research producing novel patentable outputs, the program's round configuration activates the IP ownership declaration as an explicit gate criterion rather than a supplementary disclosure.

**Public benefit mechanism and access condition.** Triggered by: any application whose eligibility is assessed against a public goods claim, regardless of obligation mode. This dimension is not optional for public goods grant programs: it is required at all entry specification gates in programs where the eligibility criteria include any public goods basis.

Active requirements: The applicant must declare (1) which of the four mechanism types their public benefit claim rests on (output production, access provision, condition change, or ecosystem shift), (2) the access conditions under which that mechanism holds, specified with enough precision that an independent reviewer could confirm them without contacting the applicant, and (3) whether the access conditions are current at the time of application, will be achieved at the time of the completion gate, or both. An applicant who claims output production access but has not yet applied the declared license to their outputs must specify when the license will be applied and confirm this at the completion gate.

The mechanism type declared at entry constrains the evidence scope required at the completion gate. An applicant declaring an output production mechanism commits to output evidence at minimum. An applicant declaring an access provision mechanism commits to usage evidence. An applicant declaring a condition change mechanism commits to outcome evidence. An applicant declaring an ecosystem shift mechanism commits to a documented impact contribution argument. A declared mechanism that cannot be supported by the evidence scope available at the completion gate is a misrepresentation in the entry specification.

The access condition for output production mechanism must include the SPDX license identifier and the location of the license file. A copyright notice that permits free viewing without granting modification or redistribution rights does not satisfy the output production access condition. A "source available" or "fair code" license that restricts commercial use does not satisfy the output production access condition unless the program's published round configuration explicitly accepts such licenses and states the accepted SPDX identifiers. The access condition must be present in the artifact at the time of the completion gate; a future licensing commitment without a current license does not satisfy the access condition at the entry gate.

For programs declaring the DPG Standard as an external reference, the output production access condition maps to Indicator 2 of the DPG Standard. The conformance threshold for Indicator 2 must specify: the list of accepted SPDX identifiers, whether all OSI-approved licenses are accepted or only a subset, whether copyleft licenses are accepted alongside permissive ones, and whether the license must be present in the repository root at the time of submission or at the time of completion. Leaving these questions to reviewer interpretation is a gate criterion specification failure under Part IV.

**Revenue architecture.** Triggered by: all applications regardless of obligation mode. This dimension is universal: every applicant must declare their revenue architecture type (grant-only, fee-for-service, commercial, or hybrid). For grant-only applicants, the declaration confirms that no commercial revenue model exists and additionality requires no further delineation. For fee-for-service, commercial, and hybrid applicants, the declaration activates the additionality delineation requirement in Part VI-A: the applicant must specifically identify which portion of their work or budget the commercial revenue does not cover, and the grant must be scoped to that non-commercial portion.

The revenue architecture declaration is a required element of the sufficiency architecture declaration in Part IV. It appears there rather than as a standalone gate condition because the revenue architecture and the sufficiency position are the two parameters that together determine whether an additionality argument is coherent.

**Continuity Capacity.** Triggered by: grants above a funder-defined size threshold, grants producing ongoing infrastructure with continuing operational requirements, and any application where the applying entity has a single named primary contributor and no documented backup capacity. For programs that fund infrastructure maintenance or sustained service delivery, continuity capacity is always active regardless of threshold.

Active requirements: The applicant must declare one of three continuity capacity states (single, partial, or resilient) with supporting evidence. For single-point-of-failure continuity, the declaration must name the person on whom continuation depends and state what would happen to active grants and funded deliverables if that person became unavailable. The disclosure of single-point-of-failure continuity is not a disqualifier but is a material fact that affects the sustainability assessment at the continuation gate and the funder's configuration of completion gate conditions. A funder may configure the completion gate to require evidence of partial or resilient continuity as a condition of final disbursement for grants above the threshold.

**Development stage.** Triggered by: all applications. Universal. The applicant declares one of five development stages with evidence from sources outside their control. The declared stage constrains which obligation modes are coherent and which evidence scopes are achievable at the completion gate. Funders declare in their round configuration which stages are within scope for the round.

Active requirements: Applicant declares their development stage and provides evidence confirming that stage. Stage 1 proof-of-concept applications must provide output evidence of the existing artifact. Stage 2 early adoption applications must provide usage evidence from at least one named external user. Stage 3 growth applications must provide independently verifiable adoption data with a trend. Stage 4 established infrastructure applications must provide 12 months of sustained usage data from sources outside the team. Stage 5 retroactive recognition applications provide prior contribution evidence in the entry submission. A declared stage that is not supported by the provided evidence is a material discrepancy requiring Block J investigation.

Funder round configuration must declare which stages are within scope. A round that accepts all stages must state this explicitly; a round targeting specific stages must name them and state why other stages are excluded. Stage targeting is a program design decision, not an evaluation judgment; it must be published before any applicant submits.

**Beneficiary engagement and beneficiary validation.** Triggered by: whether the application claims to address a problem experienced by a defined population. In change-obligation rounds, the claimed FROM state must be validated by parties outside the applicant's control. In build-obligation rounds, the claimed need for the deliverable must be validated by at least one party outside the applicant's organization who would use or benefit from it.

The parties who bear loss when a CROSS round's evaluation misjudges are the applicant whose work may be misclassified, the funder whose resources may be misdirected, and the served population. The people a funded intervention is intended to serve bear loss when the round funds work that does not serve them, or excludes work that would have served them. The served population is a risk-bearer of the round's evaluation, named here and carried through every gate, not validated only at entry.

Where a round's eligibility rests on serving a defined population, each gate determination accounts for that population's exposure, not only the applicant's claim about it. The beneficiary engagement and beneficiary validation dimension already performs part of this at entry, validating the claimed need from outside the applicant's control. At the completion verification gate, the determination and the unintended outcomes disclosure additionally consider whether the served population bears exposure the round has not surfaced: work recorded as delivered that does not reach them, or effects on them that the declared indicators do not capture. This is a named consideration each gate is checked against; the determination record must show that the consideration was applied, not merely that the population was named at entry. It does not, on its own, require a standing channel through which the served population raises concerns during the grant period; such a channel is a separate configuration a funder may add.

**Privacy and data sensitivity.** Triggered by: whether the affected population faces elevated risk from standard outcome measurement. For interventions where standard measurement would compromise the safety of the affected population, privacy-preserving measurement methodologies are required in lieu of standard analytics. The accommodation is not an exemption from obligation; it specifies which obligation mechanisms are appropriate to the context.

**Environmental and social impact.** Optional dimension. Activated by funder configuration when the funded work may produce significant, diverse, potentially irreversible, or unprecedented impacts on communities, ecosystems, or cultural heritage. If activated, follows the A/B/C classification structure derived from International Finance Corporation Performance Standards and Green Climate Fund Environmental and Social Policy.

**Gender equity.** Optional dimension. Activated by funder configuration when the funded work affects populations where gender-disaggregated outcomes are material to the public goods claim.

**Procurement integrity.** Optional dimension. Activated by funder configuration when the funded work involves sub-contracting, sub-granting, or re-granting to third parties exceeding 20% of the total award.

## Part III: Obligation Modes

Every CROSS-conformant round operates in one of three obligation modes. The mode is declared by the funder in the program's round configuration before the round opens and published as part of the round specification. Applicants are entitled to know which mode applies before they submit.

The mode determines what the entry specification gate asks, what the completion verification gate requires, and what failure modes are relevant at each gate. A finding that would disqualify an application in one mode may be correct and complete in another.

**Build obligation.** The funded work must produce a specified deliverable. The obligation object is the deliverable itself: does it exist, does it meet the stated completion criteria, and is it publicly accessible? The entry specification asks whether the applicant can name a falsifiable deliverable with independently verifiable completion criteria. The Theory of Build is not a failure mode in a build-obligation round; it is the correct and complete form of specification. An application that describes what it will build, with enough specificity that a reviewer who did not commission the work could verify whether it was completed, passes the entry specification.

Appropriate for: early-stage innovation grants, infrastructure development, tooling and library development, any program where the funder's primary goal is the existence of a public good artifact rather than a measured change in a population's condition.

**Change obligation.** The funded work must produce a measurable change in a defined population. The obligation object is the change itself: did the condition named in the FROM state improve toward the target named in the TO state, as measured by the specified indicator? The entry specification asks whether the applicant can name the FROM state as a specific measurable condition in a defined population. This was the sole gate in version 0.1.0 of this standard; in version 0.2.0 it is correctly scoped to change-obligation rounds.

The Theory of Build is a failure mode specifically in change-obligation rounds. An application that describes what it will build but cannot name the FROM state it addresses has not performed a problem diagnosis. It is not disqualified because it describes building; it is disqualified because it cannot answer the entry specification question for change-obligation: what specific measurable condition, in what defined population, does this intervention address?

Appropriate for: programs seeking measured population-level outcomes, continuation grants where prior epochs established a measurable baseline, programs aligned with institutional impact standards such as development agencies or foundations.

**Retroactive obligation.** The funded work has already produced value, and the funding rewards demonstrated past contribution. The obligation object is the prior contribution itself, assessed against the program's published criteria. The entry specification asks what evidence of prior contribution the applicant presents. No prospective commitment to future deliverables or outcomes is required, though the program may configure a forward commitment as a condition of the award.

Appropriate for: retroactive public goods funding programs, recognition grants for sustained infrastructure maintenance, programs where measuring prospective impact is less reliable than rewarding demonstrated past contribution.

**Application timing is not the diagnostic.** Across all three modes, when a team began their work is not a gate criterion. A team that built something before a grant opportunity appeared and presents it during an application window may be applying for build-obligation recognition of completed work, or may be presenting a baseline for a change-obligation continuation. The diagnostic is whether the entry specification question for the declared mode can be answered, not when the work began.

**Mixed and staged modes.** A multi-stage program may configure different obligation modes at different stages. An early stage may use build obligation; a later stage, after the deliverable exists and is in use, may require change obligation. The continuation gate between stages is the instrument that effects the transition. Conformant configuration tools support mode assignment per stage.

## Part IV: Gate Architecture

Every CROSS-conformant round publishes a gate configuration before the round opens. The gate configuration specifies, for each active gate: the evidence scope requirement, the evidence strength requirement, and the infrastructure declaration confirming how the funder will operate the gate.

Gate configurations are not retrospective. A funder may not apply a higher evidence requirement than was published at round opening. A funder may not waive a published requirement without a documented rationale submitted to a named authority before the gate is reached.

**Pre-round publication requirements.** Before any applicant submits an entry gate submission, the funder must publish a plain-language applicant-facing summary of the round's requirements. The summary is not a substitute for the full gate configuration; it is an applicant-facing translation of that configuration into language an applicant who has not read the CROSS standard can act on. The summary must state: which obligation mode applies and what the entry specification requires in plain terms, what evidence will be assessed at the completion gate and at what strength level, the conformance threshold for each declared external standard in language sufficient for an applicant to assess whether they can satisfy it, and what the organizational identity declaration and sufficiency architecture declaration require. The plain-language summary must be published before the round opens and must remain publicly accessible for the full duration of the round. An applicant who cannot determine from pre-publication materials whether they can satisfy the program's entry requirements has not been given adequate information to make an informed decision about whether to invest time in an application. Failure to publish the plain-language summary before the round opens is a funder obligation violation under Part XI.

The funder must also publish the **full application question list** before the round opens. The question list must be publicly accessible to any potential applicant without creating an account, starting a submission, or contacting the funder. It must show every question the applicant will encounter, in the order they will encounter it, with the response form type for each question stated (single-select, multi-select, text, numeric, URL, file upload). For each question, the criterion intent must be visible alongside the question label: the one-sentence statement of what the question measures. This is not a preview of the form; it is the specification of the form. An applicant who reads the question list before beginning an application has all the information they need to decide whether to apply and to prepare their answers before entering any form system. Programs that publish the question list before opening receive more targeted applications because self-selection happens before submission rather than during review. The question list publication requirement applies regardless of which form tool the program uses to collect applications; it is a publication obligation on the funder, not a feature of the form tool.

**The four gate types.**

*Entry specification gate.* What an applicant must demonstrate to enter the round. Content depends on the declared obligation mode. In build-obligation rounds: a falsifiable deliverable specification, meaning a description of the artifact to be produced that includes completion criteria specific enough that an independent reviewer could verify whether they were met. In change-obligation rounds: a FROM state specification, meaning a specific measurable condition in a defined population with a named data source. In retroactive rounds: evidence of prior contribution meeting the program's published criteria. The entry specification gate is always present; what it asks depends on the mode.

All entry specification gates, regardless of obligation mode, require an **organizational identity declaration** from the applicant. (The four fields below are derived from the Entity Boundary primitive, CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 2. The applying entity, affiliated entity, and contributing entity states of the Entity Boundary primitive each correspond to a required field.) The organizational identity declaration collects four required fields:

The **legal entity name and jurisdiction**: the name of the natural person or registered organization exactly as it appears in the relevant jurisdiction of registration, and the jurisdiction itself. A project name, display name, or GitHub organization name does not satisfy this field unless it is also the legal name. Where no legal entity exists, the applicant must state this explicitly and name the individual who is personally bound to the obligation.

The **parent organization declaration**: whether the applicant operates under or is affiliated with a larger organization, network, or chapter structure. Where a parent organization exists, its name must be stated. Where the applicant is a chapter, working group, or sub-entity of a known organization, the relationship must be described: does the parent organization know about this application, does it share the applicant's brand or personnel, and is it separately applying to this or concurrent rounds of the same program? Silence on parent organization is not acceptable where an affiliation is publicly documented or where the applicant's name, brand, or personnel overlaps with a known larger organization.

The **affiliated entity disclosure**: any other organization, project, or entity that shares the applicant's name, brand, domain, key personnel, or codebase, whether or not a formal legal relationship exists. The purpose of this field is to surface name collisions (a new applicant using a name associated with an established org), internal fragmentation (a sub-entity applying separately from a parent that may also apply), and personnel overlap (key team members holding funded roles in other organizations applying to the same round). Disclosure is not a disqualifier; non-disclosure of a publicly documentable affiliation is.

The **prior round history**: whether the applicant or any substantially overlapping entity (same personnel, same codebase, same project name, or same legal entity under a different display name) has applied to this program in prior rounds, under what name, and with what outcome. This field catches serial rebranding: applicants who received a prior rejection and return under a different name claiming no prior history with the program.

The **disbursement authority**: the named person or persons with legal authority to receive grant funds on behalf of the applying entity and approve their deployment. Three states are recognized: individual (one named person with full authority), collective (shared authority through a named mechanism with current key holders named and quorum threshold stated), and delegated (authority by role with current holder named and transfer mechanism stated). For DAO applicants without a legal wrapper, the disbursement authority declaration must name the controlling wallet address and the decision-standing mechanism, with current token holders above the materiality threshold named where possible. An entry gate submission that does not include a disbursement authority declaration is incomplete.

The **on-chain identity anchor**: the canonical wallet address by which the applying entity is identifiable across blockchain programs and grant databases. For entities that have applied to or received funding from any blockchain-based grant program, this is the address registered in those programs. For entities without prior blockchain grant history, this is the address that will be used as the identity root for this program's records. The on-chain identity anchor is used by the funder to perform cross-program identity matching and Attestation Corpus queries; it is not a fund receipt address unless it also serves as the Disbursement Authority. Where the on-chain identity anchor and the disbursement authority are the same address, the applicant must state this explicitly. Non-disclosure of an on-chain address that is publicly associated with the applicant's identity in any grant database is a conformance failure equivalent to non-disclosure in the affiliated entity field. (Derived from the On-chain Identity Anchor primitive, CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 2.)

The organizational identity declaration is a pre-condition for gate assessment, not a scored criterion. An entry gate submission that does not include the organizational identity declaration is incomplete and is not assessed until the declaration is provided.

All entry specification gates also require a **sufficiency architecture declaration** alongside the organizational identity declaration. The sufficiency architecture declaration collects five elements (the four listed below plus the revenue architecture declaration, which is required for all applicants as specified in Part II):

A **scope declaration**: the specific program, sub-entity, or aspect of the applying entity's work that this application covers, stated clearly enough that an independent reviewer could determine what falls within it and what falls outside it. Where the application covers the applying entity's entire operation, this must be stated explicitly. Most applications cover a specific scope within a larger entity; declaring that scope is what allows evaluators to assess additionality, attribute outcomes, and understand how this grant fits within the entity's broader funding architecture.

A **sufficiency position**: the applying entity's current resource position at the declared scope. One of four declared positions: critical gap (resources cover less than half of full scope requirements and the program's viability depends on closing this gap), partial (resources cover more than half but the program operates below intended capacity), approaching (resources are sufficient for current operations but not for the next intended scope expansion), or surplus (resources exceed current scope requirements in the current period). Sufficiency is declared at the declared scope, not at the entity level overall. An entity with multiple programs may be approaching sufficiency on one program and at critical gap on another; each application declares its own position.

A **portfolio context statement**: how other funding sources in the applying entity's funding architecture address other scopes or aspects of the entity's work. This is not the same as concurrent funding disclosure, which asks about other funding for the same scope. This asks how the entity has organized its pursuit of sufficiency across all its programs, so this application can be evaluated as part of a coherent architecture rather than in isolation. The presence of other funding sources for other scopes is expected and is not a disqualifier. Silence about other funding sources in an entity with multiple programs requires explicit explanation.

A **grant contribution statement**: what specific gap at the declared scope this grant would close, and what the sufficiency position at that scope would be after the grant's contribution. This is a declaration of where in the sufficiency architecture the grant fits, not a promise about what will be achieved.

The sufficiency position declaration requires at least one piece of corroborating evidence from a source outside the applicant's control. Acceptable corroborating evidence includes: publicly accessible treasury data (on-chain balance, Open Collective balance, or equivalent) confirming the scale of current resources at the declared scope; grant history records from a named external database (KarmaGAP, Gitcoin Explorer, RPGF recipient lists, or equivalent) confirming the number and scale of active grants; or disclosed revenue figures from a named independent source. A sufficiency position declaration with no corroborating evidence is accepted at self-report evidence strength only. For programs that configure the sufficiency architecture declaration at independent review evidence strength or above, corroborating evidence is required before gate assessment proceeds. The corroborating evidence does not need to precisely match the declared sufficiency position; it provides the factual basis against which the declaration can be assessed. Where no publicly accessible corroborating evidence exists (for example, a recently formed entity with no prior funding history), the applicant must state this explicitly and explain why the declared sufficiency position is reasonable given the absence of verifiable evidence.

Where co-funding is documented in a publicly verifiable coordination record before any work begins, that record constitutes independently verifiable evidence of the portfolio context declaration. A coordination record satisfies this condition if it allows an independent party to confirm that the named co-funders committed to the named complementary scopes before any disbursement from any of the coordinating programs occurred. The timestamp of the commitment distinguishes declared co-funding, which is asserted in the application without independent verification, from confirmed co-funding, which is verifiable from a record outside any single program's control. Programs may treat confirmed co-funding coordination records as stronger evidence of portfolio context than self-reported portfolio context statements, and are encouraged to distinguish these two evidence types in their gate configuration.

**Public benefit mechanism and access condition declaration.**

Where the public benefit mechanism dimension is active (which it is in all programs where eligibility includes a public goods claim), the entry specification gate must also include a public benefit mechanism and access condition declaration from the applicant. This declaration is a pre-condition for gate assessment alongside the organizational identity declaration and the sufficiency architecture declaration.

The declaration collects two required elements.

A **mechanism type statement**: which of the four mechanism types the public goods claim rests on, in the applicant's own terms but using the declared vocabulary. If a program's round configuration accepts only certain mechanism types (for example, a program that funds only output production and access provision, not condition change claims), the mechanism type field must specify which types are accepted and what evidence scope they require.

An **access condition statement**: the specific terms under which the declared mechanism delivers benefit to the stated population. The required content of the access condition statement is determined by the mechanism type: for output production, an SPDX license identifier and license file location; for access provision, an access mechanism, cost structure, and non-excludability mechanism; for condition change, a FROM state and population definition; for ecosystem shift, a named causal pathway and co-factor list.

An entry gate submission that includes a public goods claim in the eligibility section but does not include a public benefit mechanism and access condition declaration is incomplete and must not be assessed until the declaration is provided.

**Obligation Fulfillment Record.**

Where any of the following conditions apply, the entry gate submission must include an obligation fulfillment record before gate assessment proceeds: the applicant has received prior grants from this funder in any prior round; the applicant cites prior work as evidence of capability at the entry gate; the applicant's prior round history includes unresolved KarmaGAP milestones or open gate conditions.

The obligation fulfillment record documents, for each prior grant from this funder: what was committed at the entry gate of that prior round (the deliverable specification, the FROM-TO state, or the contribution evidence, as appropriate to the obligation mode of that round); what was produced during the grant period (the artifact, the measured change, or the verified contribution, with a publicly accessible link); and whether the commitment was fulfilled, partially fulfilled, or unfulfilled, with the applicant's explanation where fulfillment was not complete.

An unfulfilled prior obligation is not automatically disqualifying. It is a disclosed fact that affects the track record assessment at the continuation gate and may be addressed through a written explanation of what prevented fulfillment and what has changed since. An unfulfilled prior obligation that is not disclosed in the obligation fulfillment record but is discovered through research is a material discrepancy under Block J: non-disclosure of a prior failure is treated more seriously than the failure itself.

**Attestation Corpus Query (Funder-Side).**

For each application that provides an on-chain identity anchor, the reviewing program queries the Attestation Corpus for that address across named public sources before gate assessment is complete. The Attestation Corpus query is a funder-side procedure, not an applicant submission requirement. (Derived from the Attestation Corpus primitive, CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 4, and the On-chain Identity Anchor primitive, Layer 2.)

Required query sources: on-chain attestation registries (EAS deployments on Ethereum mainnet, Optimism, Base, and Arbitrum at minimum), the KarmaGAP grants and milestone API, and any program-specific grant databases the round configuration specifies. The query returns: total non-revoked attestations, attestations by known schema (KarmaGAP, RPGF, Gitcoin, and other named programs), endorsement count and endorser identities where resolvable.

The Attestation Corpus query result is recorded in the evaluation record alongside the Obligation Fulfillment Record. Where the Attestation Corpus reveals prior grants, milestones, or obligations not disclosed in the applicant's Obligation Fulfillment Record, this constitutes non-disclosure of a prior obligation and is treated as a material discrepancy under the adverse signal provisions of this Part.

**Development stage declaration.**

Where the development stage dimension is active (which it is in all rounds), the entry specification gate also requires a development stage declaration from the applicant. The declaration includes: the declared stage number (1 through 5), the applicant's description of why their work is at that stage rather than adjacent stages, and at least one piece of evidence from a source outside the applicant's control confirming the declared stage.

The declared development stage must be consistent with the evidence provided elsewhere in the entry submission. A Stage 1 declaration paired with usage evidence from independent users is either a misrepresentation of stage or an understatement that the reviewer should investigate. A Stage 3 or higher declaration paired with no independently verifiable adoption data is a discrepancy requiring Block J investigation.

The declared development stage constrains the obligation mode selection. A Stage 1 applicant committing to a change obligation must provide a FROM state and a population baseline at entry; this is coherent only if the Stage 1 artifact is specifically designed to measure against an existing condition. A Stage 4 applicant selecting a build obligation must specify what new functionality they are adding beyond sustained operation; declaring Stage 4 and a build obligation for work that amounts to continued maintenance of existing function is a framing misrepresentation.

A declared mechanism type that contradicts the applicant's own description of their work is a material discrepancy requiring Block J investigation under a conformant evaluation process. The most common contradiction pattern: an applicant describes work produced under a standard all-rights-reserved copyright while claiming an output production mechanism. The access condition for output production requires an open license; all-rights-reserved copyright is the opposite. This contradiction cannot be resolved by reviewer judgment; it must be surfaced as a discrepancy and the applicant must address it before gate assessment proceeds.

*Progress verification gates.* What a grantee must demonstrate to trigger mid-funding disbursement tranches. Each tranche disbursement is contingent on verification of progress against the milestone committed at the prior stage. The minimum acceptable evidence at a progress verification gate is a publicly linkable artifact demonstrating work toward the specified deliverable or indicator. Funders may configure higher evidence requirements; they may not configure lower ones. Where no mid-funding disbursement is planned, progress verification gates are not required, and the full obligation weight rests on the completion verification gate.

*Completion verification gate.* What a grantee must demonstrate to receive final payment. This gate cannot be informal. The funder must have a named, published mechanism for determining whether completion criteria have been met. Grantee self-report alone does not satisfy the completion verification gate at any evidence strength level. The funder must review the grantee's submission against the published completion criteria and make a documented determination. For build-obligation rounds, the completion gate must additionally confirm that the specified deliverable is publicly accessible before final payment is released. Final payment may not be released without this confirmation.

The completion gate submission must include an unintended outcomes disclosure alongside the primary outcome evidence. The unintended outcomes disclosure asks the grantee to identify any effects, positive or negative, that occurred during the grant period that were not specified in the entry gate and that are plausibly connected to the funded work. This is a disclosure requirement, not a disqualifier. An unintended positive outcome (adoption by a population not specified at entry, a secondary use of a built tool) is recorded as a discovery. An unintended negative outcome (displacement of existing community practices, reduction in activity from competing approaches, adverse effects on populations adjacent to the target population) is recorded as an adverse signal and triggers the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle for the continuation gate assessment. Grantees who state that no unintended outcomes occurred must state this explicitly; silence on the field does not satisfy the disclosure requirement. The absence of unintended outcomes in a complex intervention context warrants documentation of why that determination was reached.

*Continuation specification gate.* What a project must demonstrate to advance from one stage to the next in a multi-stage program, or to receive funding from the same funder in a subsequent round. This gate sits above the individual round and is configured at the program level, not the round level. It is the primary mechanism for escalating evidentiary pressure as a project matures. Not all programs require a continuation gate; it is activated by funder configuration. Where configured, the continuation gate must be published before any round in the program opens, so applicants understand the progression requirements before committing to the program.

**Prior Work Attribution Statement**

Where an applicant cites prior work as evidence of capability, track record, or past contribution in any entry gate submission, a prior work attribution statement is required. (Derived from the contributing entity state of the Entity Boundary primitive, CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 2.) This requirement activates for all three obligation modes: build-obligation applicants who cite prior deliverables as evidence of capacity; change-obligation applicants who cite prior outcomes as evidence of a measurable baseline or established methodology; and retroactive-obligation applicants whose entire entry submission consists of prior work.

The prior work attribution statement must address three questions for each piece of prior work cited:

**Entity of performance**: under which legal entity or organizational context was the cited work performed? If the work was performed as an employee, contractor, or contributor to an organization that is not the applying entity, that organization must be named. If the work was performed under the applying entity's own resources and control, that must be stated explicitly.

**Applicant's relationship at the time**: what was the applicant's role in the entity under which the work was performed, and what is the current status of that relationship? An individual who founded a project within an organization and has since departed is in a materially different position from an individual who founded an independent project they have controlled throughout. A contributor who wrote 30% of a codebase held by another entity is in a materially different position from the entity that owns and maintains that codebase. The relationship at the time of creation and the relationship at the time of application must both be stated.

**Current ownership and access**: who currently holds the intellectual property rights to the cited work, who controls the deployment or codebase, and who has the user relationship with any users cited as evidence of impact? If the applying entity does not hold the IP, does not control the deployment, or does not have the relationship with the cited users, this must be stated. A claim of prior contribution to a project does not confer the right to represent that project's user base or codebase as the applicant's own in a continuation or retroactive funding application.

The prior work attribution statement is an adverse signal disclosure requirement. An applicant who cannot truthfully complete the attribution statement for their cited prior work has surfaced a misrepresentation in the core evidential basis of their application. This is not a procedural technicality: an application whose track record is built on work performed under a different entity, without that entity's knowledge or endorsement of the application, is claiming to be answerable for outcomes the applicant did not control and cannot warrant continuing.

**Gate Record Legibility and Cross-Program Track Record**

Where a program expresses gate determinations as publicly verifiable records signed by the funder, those records constitute independently verifiable history of the grantee's obligation fulfillment. A funder-signed gate record is evidence, queryable by any independent party, that a specific entity demonstrated specific obligations to a specific published standard in a specific round.

This affects two provisions directly. For the organizational identity declaration's prior round history field: where a prior funder's gate determination exists as a publicly verifiable signed record, an applicant may cite it as corroboration, and the reviewing program may query it as independent verification of the claimed prior history, in lieu of or alongside self-reported documentation. A funder-signed gate record from a prior round is stronger corroboration than an applicant's written account of the same outcome, because it reflects the prior funder's determination rather than the applicant's characterization.

For the continuation gate's track record assessment: a funder-signed completion record from a prior stage provides a more reliable basis for track record claims than self-reported history for the same reason. Programs configuring continuation gates should specify whether they will accept prior-funder gate records as satisfying the track record evidence requirement, and in what form those records must be presented.

The format of gate records is determined by the program's infrastructure and institutional context, as specified in Part I's format agnosticism principle. On-chain attestations, signed institutional documents, and comparable mechanisms are all conformant. Content requirements for gate records are specified in the Gate Criterion Specification subsection and in the evidence scope and strength requirements of this Part.

**Gate function: developmental vs. summative.** Progress verification gates are developmental in character: their purpose is to surface what needs to change before the next gate, not to render a final judgment on the work. A progress gate that results in a hold or revision request is functioning correctly; it is catching a problem while it is still correctable. The appropriate funder posture at a progress gate is formative, and grantees retain iteration rights between progress gates without those iterations constituting a gate failure. Completion verification gates are summative in character: their purpose is to render a documented determination of whether the obligation was met as specified. A completion gate that results in a failure is a final judgment; it does not trigger iteration rights, though it does trigger redress rights under Part XI. The evidence requirements, reviewer posture, and grantee rights differ by gate function. Funders who treat completion gates as formative (negotiating outcomes after the gate is reached) and progress gates as summative (treating partial progress as failure) are violating the gate architecture regardless of conformance with evidence scope requirements.

**Pre-Award Indicator Confirmation**

After the entry specification gate is passed and before first disbursement, funders may configure a pre-award indicator confirmation window. During this window, the funder reviews the indicator specification submitted at entry (Part V) and issues a written confirmation, a revision request, or an escalation to structured clarification. The window must be time-bounded and must not delay first disbursement beyond the published window period.

The purpose of the pre-award confirmation window is to surface indicator specification problems before work begins, when revision is costless, rather than at the completion gate, when it becomes a dispute about whether the work satisfied obligations the funder considers binding. A grantee who receives written confirmation of their indicator specification before work begins has an explicit record of what the funder accepted as sufficient. A funder who confirms an indicator specification and subsequently claims it was insufficient at the completion gate must document the specific discrepancy and the basis on which the confirmed specification was found wanting.

Where a pre-award confirmation window is configured, the following apply:

- The funder must respond within the published window period. Silence at the window's close constitutes confirmation; no additional notification is required.
- Written confirmation or revision request is the required output. Verbal or informal communication does not satisfy this requirement.
- Grantee revisions requested during the window do not affect funding status. Revision requests issued after the window closes are subject to the mid-grant amendment procedure.
- The confirmed indicator specification binds all subsequent gate assessments unless the grant is formally amended.

Where no pre-award confirmation window is configured, the indicator specification submitted at the entry gate binds all subsequent gate assessments, and the funder may not impose requirements at the completion gate that were not visible in the published gate configuration.

**Evidence scope.** The scope of evidence accepted at any gate falls into one of four levels, in ascending order of rigor.

Output evidence: the specified deliverable exists, or a measurable artifact demonstrates progress toward it. Applicable to build-obligation gates. A shipped feature, a deployed contract, a published dataset, a recorded demonstration meeting the specified completion criteria are all output evidence.

Usage evidence: the deliverable is being used by parties outside the applicant's control, at a scale and in a manner that an independent reviewer can verify from public sources. Not all build-obligation programs require usage evidence, but continuation gates in build-obligation programs should require it to confirm that the deliverable has public uptake.

Outcome evidence: a measurable change has occurred in the specified population, documented against the baseline established at entry. Required for completion and continuation gates in change-obligation rounds.

Impact evidence: a supportable causal link is established between the funded work and the measured change, through methodology sufficient to support causal inference. Not required in most public goods grant contexts, but may be configured as a continuation gate requirement for large-scale programs with institutional funders.

For on-chain contract and protocol deliverables, output evidence includes not only the existence of the deployed contract but also the existence of a verification artifact demonstrating that the contract satisfies the specified invariant set. Where the obligation object is a behavioral property rather than a discrete output, the verification artifact is part of the output evidence, not only supporting documentation.

**Outcome Verification with Counterfactual Reference**

The default completion verification gate for change-obligation rounds asks whether a measurable change occurred against the baseline established at entry. This is a completion gate: it asks whether the indicator moved in the documented direction by the documented amount. It is not a causal gate: it does not ask whether the intervention produced the change rather than confounding factors.

For programs where causal attribution is material to the funding decision, funders may configure the completion verification gate or the continuation specification gate at counterfactual reference level. A gate configured at counterfactual reference level asks: did the funded intervention produce a change in the specified condition above the level expected in the absence of the intervention?

A grantee submitting evidence at counterfactual reference level must include all of the following as required contract elements:

- **Counterfactual baseline.** A documented estimate of what the indicator value would have been during the grant period without the intervention, derived from one of: historical trend extrapolation from pre-intervention data, a matched comparison group not exposed to the intervention, or a modeled counterfactual with stated assumptions and sensitivity ranges.
- **Attribution argument.** A written account of why the observed change is attributable to the intervention rather than to confounding factors, naming the main alternative explanations and explaining why they are insufficient to account for the observed change.
- **Comparison period or group.** A defined period or population that serves as the counterfactual reference, described specifically enough that an independent reviewer can assess whether it is an appropriate comparison for the intervention context.
- **Independent attestation.** Confirmation from a party outside the grantee organization that the counterfactual method and comparison group are reasonable given the intervention context and available data.

Where a comparison group cannot be defined because the intervention affects an entire ecosystem or operates at a protocol level with universal effects, the grantee must document why a comparison group approach is not feasible and provide a modeled counterfactual with stated assumptions. Absence of a comparison group is not itself a gate failure; absence of a counterfactual estimate and attribution argument is.

Counterfactual reference requirements apply only when explicitly configured by the funder in the gate configuration and declared in the infrastructure declaration. Funders activating counterfactual reference requirements must specify the acceptable counterfactual methods before the round opens. Default gate configurations do not require counterfactual attribution. The requirement is not implied by selecting outcome evidence scope; it is an additional layer that must be activated explicitly.

**Attribution vs. contribution as causality stance.** Counterfactual attribution is one end of a causality spectrum. The other end is contribution analysis: a documented account of how an intervention plausibly contributed to an observed outcome, acknowledging co-factors and without claiming exclusive causation. Most grants programs operate somewhere between these two positions without declaring which they are in. CROSS requires the causality stance to be declared explicitly in the gate configuration.

A gate configured at **attribution stance** requires evidence that the intervention caused the observed change. Counterfactual reference (above) is the standard methodology for attribution stance claims. Attribution stance is appropriate where the funder needs to defend a causal claim to an institutional body (a board, a regulator, a co-funder requiring proof of impact), where the intervention is the primary or only active change effort in the target population, or where future funding decisions depend on causal proof rather than plausible contribution.

A gate configured at **contribution stance** requires evidence that the intervention plausibly contributed to the observed outcome, with the co-factors named and the mechanism of contribution stated. Contribution stance does not require a counterfactual. It requires: a named causal pathway from the funded activity to the observed change; a list of other interventions, conditions, or actors that also contributed; and a written account of why the funded intervention's contribution was material rather than incidental. Contribution stance is appropriate for most public goods and Web3 grants contexts, where multiple funders, tools, and community actors are contributing to the same ecosystem outcomes and exclusive attribution is neither warranted nor necessary.

The causality stance is a gate-level configuration, not a program-level one. A program may configure progress gates at contribution stance and continuation gates at attribution stance as evidence accumulates across rounds. Where a program-level ToC declaration is present, the causality stance declared for each pathway should match the gate configuration for rounds advancing that pathway.

**Evidence strength.** The verification mechanism at any gate falls into one of four levels, in ascending order of rigor.

Self-report with documentation: the grantee provides a narrative account with supporting links, files, or on-chain data references, reviewed by funder staff against the published criteria. Appropriate for small grants and early-stage progress verification gates.

Third-party verifiable: the evidence is independently accessible to any reviewer with internet access, from sources outside the applicant's control. On-chain data from named public contracts, public repository activity, published results with open data. Appropriate as the minimum for completion verification gates above small grant thresholds.

Independent review: a named party outside the funder-grantee relationship confirms that the submitted evidence meets the completion criteria. The reviewer may be a domain expert, a named committee member not involved in the original award decision, or another funder who has reviewed the same work. Appropriate for continuation gates and for grants above a funder-defined size threshold.

For contract-centric interventions, independent review and independent evaluation may be satisfied by a named external security auditor, formal verification provider, or equivalent qualified reviewer who attests that the submitted verification artifacts and on-chain behavior meet the published completion criteria.

Independent evaluation: a qualified evaluator with relevant domain expertise conducts a structured assessment of whether the funded work produced the claimed output, usage, or outcome, using methods proportional to the evidence scope required. Appropriate for Stage 2 and Stage 3 grants in graduated programs, and for programs where causal attribution is material to the funding decision.

**Infrastructure declaration.** Funders configuring any gate at independent review level or above must declare, before the round opens, the specific mechanism by which that verification will be conducted: who will conduct it, what their qualification is, what process they will follow, and what the timeline for completion is. Declaring a gate at a verification level the funder cannot actually operate is a conformance violation. The infrastructure declaration is part of the published round specification and may not be changed after the round opens without documented justification and notification to applicants.

**Reviewer calibration.** For any gate configured at independent review level or above, the funder must conduct a reviewer calibration exercise before the review window opens, completed before any reviewer assesses any live application.

The calibration exercise must present reviewers with the published gate criteria, their criterion intents, and the conformance threshold for each criterion, and must include at least two worked examples: one application that clearly satisfies the gate criteria and one that clearly does not, with documented assessments for each. Where a review panel includes reviewers from multiple institutions or contexts, calibration must include a check for inter-rater consistency on the worked examples before live applications are assessed.

The calibration record must name the reviewers present, the date, the gate criteria reviewed, and confirm that calibration was completed. It is retained as part of the gate's evidence record. A gate assessment conducted by a reviewer who did not complete calibration does not satisfy the infrastructure declaration for that gate. Programs configuring gates at independent review level or above must include reviewer calibration in their infrastructure declaration.

**Proportionality.** Minimum gate requirements scale with funding amount and program stage. The Coordination Scaling Standard provides the calibration logic. As a baseline: grants below a funder-defined small grant threshold may configure all gates at self-report with documentation level. Grants above the threshold must configure the completion verification gate at no lower than third-party verifiable level. Continuation gates for programs at their second stage or beyond must configure the evidence scope at usage level or higher.

**Cost-effectiveness for continuation.** Where a continuation gate is configured for a change-obligation program, the entry specification for the next stage includes a cost-effectiveness consideration: does the evidence from the prior stage indicate that further investment in this intervention produces meaningful change relative to available alternatives? This is not a disqualifying criterion at first-stage entry; it is a continuation criterion. A conformant configuration tool may configure cost-effectiveness as a required or advisory element of the continuation gate.

**Sustainability assessment at continuation.** The OECD DAC Sustainability criterion asks whether the benefits produced by an intervention will continue after the intervention ends. CROSS's continuation gate asks whether to fund another round; it must also ask whether the outcomes produced by prior rounds are self-sustaining or dependent on continued intervention. These are different questions. A program can pass the cost-effectiveness test (further investment is worthwhile) while failing the sustainability test (prior outcomes will not persist without continued funding). Both questions are relevant to a continuation decision and must not be collapsed into one.

Where a continuation gate is configured, the grantee's continuation submission must include a sustainability stance on outcomes produced in prior rounds: are the documented outcomes continuing under their own momentum, or do they require ongoing intervention to persist? The sustainability stance takes one of three declared positions. Sustained: the outcomes are self-sustaining and do not require additional intervention to continue. Conditional: the outcomes persist under specified conditions that are independent of this program's funding. Dependent: the outcomes will not persist without continued intervention, and the continuation request is in part a request to sustain existing outcomes rather than solely to advance new ones. A dependent sustainability stance is not a disqualifier; it is a disclosure. Funders who continue programs with dependent sustainability stances are making an explicit choice to sustain outcomes rather than only to advance them, and that choice should be visible in the continuation record.

**External Standard References**

External standard references in a CROSS-conformant gate must follow the WALKRI External Standard Reference Protocol (WALKRI-standard-0_1_0.md, Part VI), including URL, version anchor, scope, and conformance threshold specification. A reference without a conformance threshold delegates interpretation to reviewers and is a gate criterion specification failure.

**Gate Criterion Specification**

Gate criteria in a CROSS-conformant round must meet WALKRI criterion specification requirements at Standard certification level or above (WALKRI-standard-0_1_0.md, Part III). A gate criterion that does not pass WALKRI audit before the round opens is a funder-side conformance failure. The five required elements are: criterion intent, operational definition with examples, response form with justification, evidence form, and conformance threshold for any external standard reference.

**Round Specification Pre-commitment**

Programs may publish a verifiable commitment to the round specification before the application window opens. A round specification pre-commitment is a publicly queryable record, derived from the complete published round specification before any applicant sees the form, that an independent party can use to confirm that the specification existed at a specific point in time before submissions were received.

The pre-commitment may take any form that satisfies two conditions: it must be derivable from the specification document so that any party can verify the match between the commitment and the specification in force, and it must carry a timestamp from a source outside the program's own control. On-chain transactions, signed timestamps from recognized timestamping authorities, and equivalent mechanisms all satisfy these conditions.

Where a pre-commitment is published, it constitutes independently verifiable evidence that the gate criteria were specified before applications were received. This is the evidentiary basis for applicant redress rights under the summative gate character provisions: an applicant asserting that criteria changed after submission can demonstrate the discrepancy by comparing the committed specification to the criteria as applied. Without a pre-commitment, such a claim is an assertion; with one, it is a documentable discrepancy. Programs whose gate architecture includes summative completion gates with declared redress rights are strongly advised to publish a pre-commitment before the application window opens.

Programs using the pre-commitment mechanism must include the commitment reference in the published round specification, so that any party can verify the match between the committed document and the specification applied.

## Part V: Indicator Specification

Applicants self-specify their outcome indicators across all obligation modes. CROSS does not pre-specify what outcomes or deliverables are valid for a given funding domain. The funder specifies eligibility criteria. The applicant specifies what their intervention produces within that domain. CROSS specifies the fields that self-specified indicators must contain and the quality standards those fields must satisfy.

Each claimed indicator requires the following fields.

**Indicator name.** A short descriptive label. The name is not the operational definition; two funders may use the same indicator name with different operational definitions.

**Rationale for indicator.** Why this indicator was selected over available alternatives. Documents the diagnostic thinking that preceded tool selection. Required across all obligation modes. An applicant who cannot articulate why they chose this particular indicator, deliverable definition, or evidence form over obvious alternatives has not engaged seriously with whether their obligation instrument is valid.

**Measurement form and evidence classification.** A plain-language description of what a result looks like and in what form it will be reported. This field replaces the prior constrained list of four data types, which was calibrated only for quantitative change-obligation indicators.

The applicant names the measurement form in plain language: a count of unique addresses, a shipped library version, a published dataset with a named license, a binary yes/no against defined completion criteria, a scored assessment on a defined scale. Reviewers classify the stated form against three axes for data quality assessment purposes.

Source type: on-chain verifiable (accessible from public blockchain data with a named contract or address), off-chain verifiable (accessible from public sources outside the blockchain, such as a public code repository, a published report, or a named application programming interface), or qualitative/narrative (documented through structured narrative, interview data, or artifact review requiring a human evaluator). Each source type carries different minimum verification requirements at the data quality assessment stage.

Measurement form: quantitative (expressed as a number, proportion, rate, currency amount, or duration), ordinal (expressed as a position on a defined ordered scale), binary (expressed as met or not met against named completion criteria), or qualitative (expressed as a documented condition, verified artifact, or narrative record assessed against named criteria).

Aggregation type: cumulative (values sum correctly across reporting periods) or non-cumulative (values describe a state at a point in time; summing across periods is a construction error). Reviewers must flag a non-cumulative indicator presented with a cumulative target at the application stage, not the reporting stage.

For build-obligation indicators: the measurement form is typically binary (the deliverable either meets the completion criteria or does not) or qualitative (the deliverable is a documented artifact assessed against named criteria). The measurement form declaration specifies what done looks like with enough precision that a reviewer who did not commission the work could verify it independently.

For change-obligation indicators: the measurement form is typically quantitative or ordinal. The declaration must include the unit of measurement and the direction of desirable change.

**On-Chain Execution and Verification Instruments.** For interventions where the primary deliverable is a smart contract, protocol, or on-chain system, the obligation object may be the contract's behavior under specified conditions rather than discrete artifacts or counts. In these cases, the indicator's measurement form and evidence classification must explicitly name the execution and verification instruments that will be used to confirm completion.

An execution and verification instrument is any mechanism that provides structured evidence about contract behavior or protocol properties, including but not limited to:

- Formal verification artifacts (machine-checkable proofs and their specifications).
- Independent security audits and review reports.
- On-chain invariant monitors or watchdog contracts.
- Canonical transaction patterns or state transitions on named contracts, observable from public blockchain data.

Where the obligation object is a contract's behavior, the indicator specification must additionally contain:

- **Invariant specification.** A narrative description of the invariants or properties the contract must satisfy (for example, conservation of balances, absence of re-entrancy on named functions, bounded slippage for a specific operation). This description is the human-readable counterpart of the formal specification used in verification tools.
- **Verification method.** A statement of whether the invariant will be assessed by formal verification, independent audit, manual review of on-chain behavior, continuous monitoring, or a combination. If formal verification is used, the named tool or approach must be specified (for example, model checking, theorem proving, or a named framework).
- **Verification artifact.** The concrete artifact that constitutes completion evidence: a proof object, an audit report from a named firm, or a named contract and query that a reviewer can use to reproduce the behavioral check from public chain data.
- **Failure surface.** At least one example of behavior that would constitute a failure to meet the invariant, and how such a failure would be detected by the chosen instrument.

For build-obligation indicators in contract-centric projects, the measurement form may be binary ("the contract satisfies the specified invariant set as demonstrated by the named verification artifact") rather than a count of outputs. The operational definition and construction methodology fields must still enable an independent reviewer to reproduce the verification procedure or validate the artifact from public sources.

Indicators that rely solely on applicant-controlled test suites or private monitoring dashboards, without any independent verification instrument, fail the data quality standard. They may appear as supplementary evidence but do not satisfy the minimum verification requirement at the completion gate for contract-centric deliverables.

**Operational definition.** A narrative specification of what counts and what does not count as one unit of the claimed result. Must include: inclusion criteria (what qualifies as one instance), exclusion criteria (what the indicator explicitly does not count), unit of analysis (person, wallet address, transaction, repository, site, or other named unit), and edge case determination (at least one example of how an ambiguous instance is handled). Without an operational definition, the claimed indicator is a name, not a measurement instrument. The operational definition must meet WALKRI requirements for inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, unit of analysis, and edge case determination (WALKRI-standard-0_1_0.md, Part III, Criterion 2).

**Construction and aggregation methodology.** The calculation rule in sufficient detail that an independent reviewer with access to the stated data source could replicate the result. Must include the formula or counting rule, how partial data is handled, and how values from sub-units are aggregated. For build-obligation indicators, the construction methodology specifies how completion is determined: what constitutes a passing demonstration, who performs the verification, and by what process. For change-obligation indicators, this is the standard data construction methodology. Self-report of a result without a stated construction methodology does not satisfy this field.

**Cumulative or non-cumulative.** Whether the indicator is cumulative (can be summed across reporting periods; life-of-grant target is achieved by addition) or non-cumulative (measures a status at a point in time; life-of-grant target is the target status at grant end, not a sum of period targets).

**Disaggregation.** The categories by which indicator values will be reported. Disaggregation categories committed to in the application may be supplemented in subsequent reporting periods but may not be removed without committee approval and documented rationale. This constraint protects longitudinal comparability.

**Data source and collection method.** The named source and the collection method. The source must be accessible to an independent reviewer. Applicant-controlled sources without named independent corroboration do not satisfy the data quality standard. For build-obligation indicators, the data source for completion verification is the named artifact itself plus the named party who will confirm it meets the completion criteria.

**Data cost estimation.** Attestation that data collection is operationally feasible within the project budget, with the approximate cost and source of funding for collection. An indicator that cannot be collected within the project budget has not been operationalized.

**Baseline (FROM state).** For change-obligation indicators: the documented state of the condition before the intervention begins, with a named data source. Required. For build-obligation indicators: the documented current absence or inadequacy of the deliverable, with named evidence of the gap it addresses. Required where the funder has activated the beneficiary engagement dimension. For retroactive indicators: the documented state of the contribution at the time the award period begins.

**Target (TO state).** The expected state of the indicator at the end of the grant period or stage. For change-obligation indicators: expressed in the same units as the baseline. For build-obligation indicators: expressed as the completion criteria the deliverable must meet. For retroactive indicators: may describe a forward commitment the awardee makes as a condition of the award, if configured by the funder.

**Sustainability and transition plan.** Optional field, activated by funder configuration. Required for grants above a funder-defined size threshold or grants producing infrastructure, software, or services with ongoing operational requirements beyond the grant period. The sustainability and transition plan specifies:

- What happens to the funded work at the end of the grant period: who maintains it, under what conditions it remains publicly accessible, and what operational costs are required to sustain it.
- How those costs will be covered after the grant ends, naming the expected source (self-funding, successor grant, revenue, community contribution, or protocol treasury) with enough specificity that a reviewer can assess plausibility.
- Where the funded work will be handed to another organization, community, or protocol: the identity of the receiving party and the conditions of the transition.
- Where the funded work reaches a defined end state after which maintenance is not required: a specification of that end state and the conditions under which it will be declared reached.

A grant producing infrastructure with ongoing operational requirements that provides no sustainability plan has not addressed whether the public benefit it claims will persist beyond the grant period. The sustainability and transition plan is not a guarantee; it is a documented position on continuation that the funder may use in subsequent continuation gate assessments.

## Part VI: Concurrent Funding Disclosure

All active grants, investments, or revenue sources from the prior 24 months that are related to the scope of this application must be disclosed. Required across all obligation modes. Required fields:

- Source name (investor, foundation, decentralized autonomous organization, protocol, or other)
- Approximate amount
- Relationship to this application's scope
- Whether ongoing reporting or milestone obligations to the source exist
- Whether any investor or funder has board, coordinating, or decision-making rights over the funded work

Non-disclosure of concurrent funding is a disqualifier. Disclosure is not. An applicant with venture capital backing can receive public goods grant funding if the relationship between commercial financing and the public goods ask is clear and the public goods ask is specifically scoped to work the commercial funding does not cover.

Token generation events, planned or executed within the prior 24 months, are required disclosure items alongside grants and investments. A token generation event is any mechanism by which the applying entity or its principals generate, allocate, or vest tokens to themselves, investors, or the public in a manner that represents or may represent economic value. This includes initial decentralized exchange offerings (IDOs), token generation events (TGEs), initial coin offerings (ICOs), liquidity bootstrapping pool launches, and continuous founder or contributor vesting schedules releasing tokens during the disclosure window. The disclosed amount is the approximate USD value at time of event, or for pending events, the planned allocation value based on the most recent publicly stated or implied valuation. A token that has not yet launched but for which a whitepaper, tokenomics document, or allocation schedule exists is a pending token generation event and must be disclosed. The existence of a token or planned token is not a disqualifier; non-disclosure of a material token event is treated as a concurrent funding omission under the same rules as undisclosed grants or investments.

## Part VI-A: Scope Attribution and Outcome Credit

CROSS distinguishes between disclosure of concurrent funding (Part VI) and attribution of results to specific funding sources. This Part specifies how scope attribution and outcome credit are handled when concurrent funding is present. It applies across all obligation modes and all gate types where outcome or usage evidence is required.

Scope attribution and outcome credit obligations are triggered when the concurrent funding disclosure requirement in Part VI is triggered. Where no concurrent funding exists, this Part does not apply.

### 1. Additionality Declaration (Budget Scope)

Where concurrent funding is disclosed at application stage, the applicant must submit an additionality declaration for this award. The additionality declaration specifies what this grant funds that concurrent sources do not.

The additionality declaration is a required field in the entry specification for any application with concurrent funding, across build, change, and retroactive obligation modes. Failure to submit an additionality declaration when concurrent funding is disclosed is a gate failure at entry.

The additionality declaration must contain:

- **Scope boundary.** A narrative specification of the work, deliverables, or costs funded by this grant that are not funded by any concurrently disclosed source. The boundary must be specific enough that a reviewer can determine whether a proposed activity lies inside or outside this grant's scope.
- **Overlap statement.** A brief statement of where overlap exists between this grant and concurrent sources, if any, and how double funding of the same input is avoided in practice.
- **Assurance of non-duplication.** An attestation that the applicant will not knowingly charge the same cost to multiple funders, and that any material changes in concurrent funding affecting this assurance will be reported at the next gate.

For build-obligation applications, the additionality declaration specifies which parts of the deliverable or which development stages this grant covers relative to other funders.

For change-obligation applications, the additionality declaration specifies which portion of the intervention pathway (activities, geography, population segment, or time period) this grant is funding relative to other funders.

For retroactive applications, the additionality declaration specifies whether the prior contribution has already received retroactive awards from other programs and what forward-facing obligations, if any, this award uniquely creates.

Programs may adopt the Impact Management Project (IMP) Contribution dimension vocabulary for the additionality argument within the additionality declaration. Under this vocabulary, the additionality argument takes one of three positions: the grant causes significant improvement in outcomes that would not otherwise occur; the grant prevents significant deterioration in outcomes that would otherwise occur without continued support; or the grant helps avoid worse outcomes compared to a named counterfactual scenario. This three-position vocabulary is more analytically precise than an open narrative additionality argument and is recommended for programs where institutional funders require structured contribution logic. A program that declares IMP Contribution vocabulary as its additionality standard must specify which of the three positions applies in the additionality declaration and must name the counterfactual against which the contribution claim is made. The three positions are not mutually exclusive; an applicant may claim both significant improvement in a new scope and prevention of deterioration in an existing scope within the same application, provided each claim is separately delineated against the declared scope boundary.

### 2. Outcome Credit Attribution (Reporting Stage)

Where concurrent funding is disclosed and outcome or usage evidence is required at a completion verification or continuation gate, the applicant must submit an outcome credit attribution statement alongside their results. This requirement applies to any gate configured at usage, outcome, or impact evidence scope in Part IV.

The outcome credit attribution statement must contain:

- **Attribution fractions.** A declared percentage allocation of the reported result across all funders that materially contributed to it, including this program. Percentages must sum to 100 percent across named funders and, where relevant, a residual category for un-funded or self-funded contribution.
- **Attribution rationale.** A short narrative explaining how the attribution fractions were determined. The rationale must name the main factors used (relative funding amounts, timing, specific work packages funded, or other relevant distinctions) and acknowledge material uncertainties.
- **Change tracking.** If attribution fractions change between reporting periods for the same indicator, the applicant must document the reason for the change and its implications for comparability of prior periods.

The attribution requirement is a documentation obligation, not a causal proof requirement. The failure mode is absence of a declared attribution position where concurrent funding exists, not imprecision in the fractions themselves. Reviewers assess whether an attribution statement is present and coherent with the additionality declaration and the indicator specification.

### 3. Gate-Level Consistency

Reviewers must assess consistency between:

- The concurrent funding disclosure (Part VI)
- The additionality declaration (Section 1 of this Part)
- The indicator specification (Part V)
- The outcome credit attribution statement (Section 2 of this Part)

Inconsistencies that must be flagged at the rigor tier include:

- An additionality declaration that asserts unique funding for work that, by the applicant's own description, is also fully funded by another source.
- An outcome credit attribution statement that assigns 100 percent of outcome credit to this funder where multiple concurrent funders are disclosed for the same scope and time period.
- Material changes in attribution fractions across periods without documented rationale.

These are rigor-tier failures, not automatic gate failures. Review committees may request clarification or revision before making a final determination.

### 4. Program-Level Continuation Gate Option

Programs that configure a continuation specification gate in Part IV may activate a scope attribution check as part of the continuation criteria. Where activated, the continuation gate must specify:

- Whether a public prior-claims registry or equivalent record will be consulted to determine whether the same outcomes have been previously claimed in other programs.
- How inconsistent or conflicting attribution statements across funders will be handled in continuation decisions.
- Whether a minimum standard for attribution coherence (for example, stable fractions over time for the same indicator absent documented changes in the funding mix) is required for advancement to the next stage.

Activation of the scope attribution check at continuation is optional at Stage 1 and required for programs whose round configuration specifies Stage 2 or above, consistent with the cost-effectiveness and maturation logic in Part IV and the Appendix.

### 5. Relationship to Concurrent Funding Disclosure

Concurrent funding disclosure in Part VI is a public documentation requirement. The additionality declaration and outcome credit attribution requirements in this Part are obligation requirements. Disclosure alone is not sufficient for CROSS conformance where concurrent funding exists.

Non-disclosure of concurrent funding remains a disqualifying disclosure failure under Part VI. Submission of a materially incomplete or incoherent additionality declaration or attribution statement is a rigor-tier failure under this Part, subject to remediation where feasible.

## Part VII: Conflict of Interest Framework

The conflict of interest framework applies independently from the outcome documentation rigor tier and independently from the obligation mode. A small-grant build-obligation round does not receive relaxed conflict of interest requirements. Relationship type, not funding amount or mode, determines conflict of interest tier assignment.

**Coverage.** Conflict of interest obligations run in both directions. Reviewers must disclose relationships to applicants. Applicants must disclose relationships to committee members and reviewers.

**Applicable standard.** The reasonable third party standard applies: would an informed observer with knowledge of the relevant facts conclude that the person cannot maintain independence and is therefore not capable of exercising objective and impartial judgment? The checklist below illustrates the standard; it does not exhaust it. Novel relationship types not on the checklist that meet the reasonable third party standard require disclosure.

**Tier 1: Categorical bar. No waiver.**

- Direct financial interest in the applicant entity, including equity, revenue share, or token holdings above the materiality threshold (default: greater than 1% of circulating supply or greater than \$5,000 in value at time of review)
- Family relationship with any principal, director, officer, or owner of the applicant project
- Prior receipt of funding from this funder in the same program area where the reviewer's funded work is directly related to the applicant's scope
- Being named as a team member, advisor, or contributor on the applicant project's public materials, whether compensated or not

**Tier 2: Disclosure required. Qualified waiver possible.**

A qualified waiver requires: (a) a written justification submitted to a named authority other than the conflicted party, (b) approval granted before participation, not after, and (c) documentation in the review record. Waiver is not available where the relationship creates a financial interest.

- Employment with the applicant organization in the past 36 months
- Co-authorship or active project collaboration with applicant principals in the past 48 months
- Mentor or student relationship with applicant principals (no expiration)
- Governance token voting power exercised on proposals affecting the applicant project
- Decentralized autonomous organization co-membership in coordinating or committee roles within the same decentralized autonomous organization as the applicant
- Having received an honorarium, award, or gift from the applicant entity in the past 12 months
- Active job negotiations or arrangements for future employment with the applicant organization
- Organizational conflict: the reviewer's employer has a material financial relationship with the applicant
- Organizational role conflict: holds a paid or unpaid leadership, advisory, or operational role in another organization applying to this round, or that received awards from this funder in the most recent prior round in the same program area. This disclosure is required even when no direct financial relationship exists between the reviewer and the applicant under review; the structural position across multiple funding relationships is itself the conflict

**Cohort Position Assessment (Round-Level Procedure).**

Before final gate determinations are issued for a round, the program performs a Cohort Position assessment across all applicants. (Derived from the Cohort Position primitive, CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 7.) The assessment checks: (1) personnel overlap, whether any named team member, contact, or key contributor in one application also appears in another application in the same round under a different entity name; (2) wallet overlap, whether any on-chain identity anchor or disbursement authority address in one application appears as a recipient, controller, or named address in another application in the same round; (3) endorser concentration, whether the same identifiable party has endorsed or attested to a disproportionate share of applicants in the same round using non-standard schemas.

Personnel overlap does not disqualify either application; it triggers a Tier 2 conflict of interest disclosure requirement for the overlapping individual. Wallet overlap requires explanation: the same address appearing across multiple distinct legal entities applying separately is a material discrepancy requiring reconciliation before disbursement. Endorser concentration is informational and is recorded in the round record without disqualifying effect.

**Tier 3: Disclosure encouraged. Reasonable third party standard applies.**

- Social and community relationships (conference co-speakers, forum co-membership, shared group chats)
- Prior interactions across grant rounds in different program areas
- Informal mentorship or advisory conversations without ongoing engagement

**Funder-side consulting conflicts.**

Where an evaluation participant is under a paid consulting or service contract with the funder at the time of evaluation, that relationship must be disclosed to the receiving function regardless of its connection to any specific applicant. This disclosure is required for any paid engagement covering the same program area as the applications under review, including review contracts, evaluation support, research commissions, and advisory retainers.

Where the consulting arrangement covers the same program area as the applications under review, a Tier 2 qualified waiver applies: written justification submitted to a named authority other than the conflicted party, approval granted before participation, and documentation in the review record.

Where the consulting arrangement constitutes the primary mechanism through which the reviewer receives income from the ecosystem being evaluated, a categorical bar applies. The categorical bar does not require the reviewer to have a direct financial relationship with any specific applicant; structural income dependency on the funder's continued operation and on favorable award decisions is itself the conflict.

**Attestation independence.**

A party who holds a Tier 1 conflict with an applicant may not serve as the independent reviewer or sign an attestation confirming that applicant's gate evidence, regardless of the attestation format used. A Tier 1-conflicted party who signs an on-chain attestation, issues a signed document, or issues a credential confirming milestone completion has not satisfied the independent review requirement; the attestation does not meet the evidence strength standard regardless of its format.

A party who holds a Tier 2 conflict with an applicant requires a qualified waiver before serving as attestor for that applicant, using the same procedure as the reviewer waiver requirement. Programs must verify reviewer and attestor conflict status before accepting any attestation as satisfying independent review evidence strength.

This provision applies to all mechanisms by which gate evidence is independently confirmed at any gate configured at independent review level or above. The conflict of interest framework runs in both directions: it applies to reviewers assessing applications and to attestors confirming completion evidence.

**Web3-specific categories.**

The following relationship types require the same three-tier assessment but are not present in standard conflict of interest frameworks developed outside web3: token holdings (see Tier 1 materiality threshold), governance token voting on applicant proposals, core contributor or unpaid advisory status on applicant projects, having received investment from the same named investor backing the applicant, and decentralized autonomous organization co-governance roles.

**Process.**

Before review: each reviewer and each applicant completes a conflict of interest declaration covering all three tiers. The declaration is submitted to a named receiving function that is separate from the person making the funding decision. In programs using the CROSS Reviewers Dashboard, the conflict of interest declaration operates as a gate: a reviewer who has not completed their declaration for a specific application cannot open that application.

After review: each reviewer completes a post-participation certification confirming that no new conflicts arose during the review process and that the declaration remained accurate.

Non-disclosure of a Tier 1 conflict is a disqualifying violation. Non-disclosure of a Tier 2 conflict is a process violation requiring remediation. Remediation options are: retroactive recusal, review restart with a non-conflicted panel, or award cancellation, depending on the degree of influence the conflicted party had.

## Part IX: Implementation Architecture

CROSS specifies what is required. Implementation tools make the standard usable without requiring funders, grantees, or reviewers to interpret the full specification directly. CROSS conformance does not require any specific tool. Programs may implement CROSS through three pathways: directly from the standard using internal systems or existing grants infrastructure; through any third-party grants management platform or form builder that produces conformant round specifications and field definitions; or through a third-party tool suite that implements the standard. All three pathways produce equally conformant output. The tools described below are reference implementations; any tool producing output that satisfies the requirements stated in this standard is a conformant implementation.

**CROSS Grant Configurator** (CROSS-grant-configurator-0_2_0.md): the funder onboarding instrument. Any tool that produces a complete, published round specification meeting the requirements of this Part satisfies this function. A conformant configurator generates a round-specific obligation specification from the funder's declared obligation mode, gate configuration, evidence requirements, and infrastructure declarations. It inherits all field definitions, tier logic, and conflict of interest requirements from this standard. The configurator also supports program-level configuration: the continuation gate structure and stage progression requirements are specified here, separately from individual round configuration. Runbook configurations may be selected as a starting point and customized.

A conformant round configuration tool records the following and publishes them before the round opens: whether concurrent funding is present and the resulting additionality requirements; external standard references for each gate; the program's funder-side obligation configuration including clarification and appeals mechanisms; and the field clarity gate outcome for each application field. Any system that records and publishes these elements satisfies the configurator function. Programs using any tool must satisfy the same content requirements through whatever mechanism their system provides.

Concurrent funding presence activates the additionality declaration and outcome credit attribution requirements specified in Part VI-A. External standard references include the identifier, version anchor, scope, and conformance threshold. The funder-side obligation configuration covers the internal clarification mechanism, structured appeals procedure, and independent redress mechanism status. The field clarity gate requires that each application field carry a criterion specification meeting Part IV requirements before the round opens; fields that do not are flagged and must be resolved or overridden with documented justification before publication.

**Post-award obligation interface** (reference implementation: CROSS Grantee Dashboard, CROSS-grantee-dashboard-0_2_0.md): converts the published round specification into a live record for each funded project, tracking gate evidence submissions and producing structured records of the obligation lifecycle. Any system satisfying this function must: carry the round specification's indicator schema, evidence requirements, gate configuration, disaggregation requirements, and disbursement conditions; track gate evidence submissions against published criteria; and produce a record of each gate determination. The format of that record is determined by the program's context, as stated in Part I.

**Review interface** (reference implementation: CROSS Reviewers Dashboard, CROSS-reviewers-dashboard-0_1_0.md): the stage-gated evaluation interface. Any system satisfying this function must: require conflict of interest declarations before any application is assessed; maintain assessment isolation between reviewers until submission; provide role-differentiated access aligned with reviewer roles; and load the appropriate rubric based on the configured obligation mode. The review record produced is the final artifact; any supporting tools (including AI-assisted evaluation) are aids to the human reviewer's determination, not substitutes for it.

**CROSS skill** (claude-skills/claude-skill-CROSS-0_2_0.md): machine-readable encoding of the standard for AI-assisted implementation across all three tools. Covers three obligation modes, the four-gate architecture, open measurement form with three-axis classification, and gate evidence assessment procedures.

**Runbooks.** Pre-built, documented configuration packages for common program types. Each runbook specifies a complete gate configuration, obligation mode assignment, evidence scope and strength at each gate, infrastructure declaration requirements, and a recommended evolution path to a more mature configuration. The initial runbook library covers: discovery sprint, staged development, community allocation with build obligation, retroactive impact, graduated evidence, and institutional conformance configurations. Runbooks may optionally declare a measurement framework alignment (see Part IX-A). Runbooks are companion documents to this standard.

**Measurement framework declarations.** Programs may declare alignment with an institutional measurement framework as a round-level configuration. When declared, the framework name and version are published alongside the gate configuration, and the external standards section of the configurator is pre-populated with the framework's canonical reference and a template conformance threshold. Current recognized declarations: IRIS+ Core Metrics Set (with sub-selection of thematic set), OECD DAC criteria (DCD/DAC(2019)58/FINAL), Logic Model (W.K. Kellogg Foundation), Grant Maturity Index (Biedermann and Gibrel, arXiv:2410.19828), DAOIP-5 (DAOstar/Metagov), and custom. A measurement framework declaration does not modify CROSS's gate or indicator requirements; it adds a named vocabulary expectation that the configurator exposes to applicants and reviewers. Programs may declare multiple frameworks.

## Part IX-A: Ecosystem Interoperability

CROSS round configurations, gate evidence, and grantee reports are structured to be expressible in any grants data format a program's ecosystem requires. This Part states the structural principle and lists compatibility examples. None of the formats listed are required; all are illustrative of how CROSS content can be expressed in systems programs may already use.

**Interoperability principle.** CROSS data has a defined content structure: round configurations carry obligation mode, gate requirements, external standard references, and funder identity. Gate evidence carries grantee identity, evidence artifacts, gate type, and evidence scope and strength. Grantee reports carry indicator values, baselines, targets, and provenance. Any system that accepts structured grants data with these elements can consume CROSS-conformant output. The format in which that output is expressed is determined by the program's context, its downstream consumers, and its existing infrastructure.

**Examples of compatible formats.** For programs operating within the DAOstar/Metagov Web3 ecosystem, CROSS round configurations map to DAOIP-5 GrantPool objects and gate evidence maps to DAOIP-5 Attestation objects. For programs operating within the international development sector, CROSS data maps to IATI activity records. For programs using KarmaGAP, CROSS milestones map to KarmaGAP milestone records. For programs using Hypercerts, CROSS completion gate evidence can reference a Hypercert as the primary evidence artifact. For institutional programs not operating in any of these ecosystems, CROSS data can be expressed as structured JSON, XML, CSV, or any tabular format appropriate to the funder's reporting infrastructure. The compatibility statements published alongside this standard provide detailed mappings for specific formats. Adopting those formats is not required for CROSS conformance.

**Gate attestation content.** A CROSS gate attestation must carry: grantee identity, round and gate identifier, evidence references with stable pointers, funder determination (pass, conditional, or fail), funder attribution, and timestamp. Any format, system, or mechanism that carries these elements satisfies the gate attestation requirement. This includes cryptographically signed digital records, institutional sign-off documents, notarized statements, and audit certificates.

**Identity infrastructure.** Where grantees have existing identity infrastructure, CROSS is compatible with it. For DAO grantees, DAOIP-2 / ERC-4824 identity records can serve as the identity layer to which CROSS gate evidence is attributed. For institutional grantees, organizational registration numbers and legal entity identifiers serve the same function. CROSS does not require any specific identity infrastructure.

**Coherence disclosure.** The OECD DAC Coherence criterion (DCD/DAC(2019)58/FINAL, Criterion 2) asks how an intervention relates to other interventions in the same problem area, and whether those relationships are complementary, duplicative, or in tension. CROSS inherits from the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle (ASEP) for adverse signal disclosure. The coherence dimension extends this: applicants are asked to name known interventions in the same problem area and characterize their relationship to the current application (complementary, parallel without coordination, duplicative, or in tension). This is a disclosure requirement, not a disqualifier. The absence of any known related interventions must be stated explicitly; "not applicable" is not an acceptable response where other interventions in the same area are publicly documented. Funders may configure whether coherence disclosure is required at the entry gate, the progress gate, or both. Where OECD DAC alignment has been declared as the measurement framework, coherence disclosure is required at both gates.

## Part IX-B: Program-Level Theory of Change Architecture

CROSS round configurations operate at the round and project level. Rounds specify what funded interventions are obligated to produce; gates verify that those obligations are met. This is sufficient for single-round obligation verification. It is insufficient at the portfolio level, where an institutional funder or grants data analyst needs to see how a portfolio of rounds and projects advances a shared causal theory about what changes and why.

Part IX-B introduces the program-level Theory of Change (ToC) declaration: an optional but recommended structural layer that situates CROSS rounds within a named causal architecture. Where declared, the ToC layer enables portfolio analysis, cross-program dependency mapping, and the kind of structural efficiency analysis that institutional funders and evaluation teams require to assess whether a portfolio of funded interventions is advancing its stated goals, and how.

The reference hierarchy for this Part is the Compact Logic structure used by the Millennium Challenge Corporation and compatible with USAID Results Framework requirements. (The ToC hierarchy, pathway, theory layer, specification directionality, sustainability stance, and portfolio analysis primitives used throughout this Part are specified in the CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layers 6 and 7.) That hierarchy is: Process, Outputs, Short-term Outcomes, Intermediate Outcomes, Long-term Outcomes, Goal. This hierarchy is adopted because it has the granularity required to distinguish output-level interventions from outcome-level ones, and because it is the structure institutional funders operating in international development already use as their reporting vocabulary. Programs in other sectors may substitute a comparable hierarchy if they declare it explicitly and map their substituted levels to this standard's reference levels.

---

#### 1. Program Identity Declaration

A program-level ToC declaration begins with a program identity block that is distinct from any individual round configuration. The program identity block contains:

A **program name and identifier**, stable across all rounds within the program. This identifier is the anchor to which round records and pathway records are linked.

A **domain declaration**: one or more sector or thematic tags identifying the problem area the program operates in. These tags are freeform but should use controlled vocabulary from a declared framework (IRIS+ thematic sets, OECD DAC sector codes, or a custom vocabulary declared in the measurement framework section) where possible.

A **geographic scope** declaration specifying the community, region, or jurisdiction the program's causal theory operates within. Where the program operates across multiple geographies, the scope declaration identifies them and notes whether the ToC is shared across all or specific to each.

A **program time horizon**: the declared period across which the program's ToC is expected to operate, covering all planned rounds and stages from first disbursement through expected impact realization. This is not a completion date; it is the window within which the causal chain from Process to Goal is expected to unfold.

---

#### 2. Goal and Outcome Architecture

The program-level ToC declaration specifies the causal chain the program is designed to advance. Following the Compact Logic hierarchy, this requires:

A **Goal statement**: a single declarative statement of the ultimate change the program is designed to contribute to. The goal is at the top of the causal chain; it is what all intermediate outcomes, short-term outcomes, and outputs ultimately serve. Goals are typically at the population or system level and operate over the full program time horizon.

A **long-term outcomes register**: the 1-4 long-term outcomes the program contributes to, each with a unique identifier within the program's pathway registry, a plain-language description, and its relationship to the Goal statement. Long-term outcomes are conditions that persist after the program's active intervention period ends. They are typically not directly measurable at any single project completion gate; instead, they accumulate contributions from multiple projects and rounds over the program time horizon. Attribution of any single project to a long-term outcome is therefore an argued contribution, not a directly measured one. Evidence at long-term outcome level is appropriate for program-level evaluation and institutional funder reporting, not for individual project completion verification.

An **intermediate outcomes register**: the intermediate outcomes the program's rounds and projects are designed to advance. Intermediate outcomes are conditions produced by the convergence of short-term outcomes; they are the primary reporting level for most institutional funders operating within a multi-year program. Each intermediate outcome carries a unique identifier, a plain-language description, and its declared relationship to one or more long-term outcomes. Intermediate outcomes are typically measurable within the program time horizon but not within a single project cycle. They are the level at which Mike's dependency analysis identified the $1.5 billion in portfolio efficiency: the convergence of multiple pathway contributions at a single intermediate outcome node is where coordinated investment is most productive and where uncoordinated investment most often duplicates effort.

A **short-term outcomes register**: the conditions produced directly by the program's outputs within the program's active period. Short-term outcomes are the primary verification level for individual project completion gates in CROSS change-obligation rounds. Each short-term outcome carries a unique identifier and its declared relationship to one or more intermediate outcomes. Short-term outcomes are directly measurable at the project level within a single grant cycle. They are the highest outcome level at which a completion gate can reasonably require outcome evidence rather than output evidence. A project claiming to produce an intermediate or long-term outcome at completion, without a declared short-term outcome as the measurable step in that direction, is making an attribution claim that exceeds what a single-cycle completion gate can verify.

The distinction between short-term, intermediate, and long-term outcomes is not merely taxonomic. It determines what evidence is appropriate at each gate, what attribution arguments are warranted, and what the portfolio analysis can aggregate without misrepresenting what individual projects contributed. Both short-term and long-term outcomes are technically "outcomes," but they require different evidence standards, different gate configurations, and different analytical treatments. Conflating them produces outcome claims that appear more robust than the evidence supports.

---

#### 3. Pathway Registry

The pathway registry is the structural core of the program-level ToC declaration. It is the element that makes portfolio analysis possible.

A pathway is a numbered causal claim: a declared relationship between a source node at one ToC level and a target node at the next level up. Source nodes are Processes or Outputs; target nodes are Short-term Outcomes, Intermediate Outcomes, or Long-term Outcomes. Each pathway carries:

A **pathway identifier**: a unique number or code within the program's pathway registry. Pathway identifiers are stable across rounds; they do not change when a round is updated or closed.

A **source node identifier** and its ToC level.

A **target node identifier** and its ToC level.

A **causal mechanism statement**: a written "if-then" statement declaring how the source produces or contributes to the target. This statement documents the program's causal theory at the pathway level. It is not an empirical claim; it is a stated hypothesis that the program's evaluation will test.

A **critical assumptions list**: the conditions that must hold for the causal mechanism to operate as claimed. Following USAID ADS 201 requirements, critical assumptions are conditions external to the program's direct control that the causal claim depends on. They are stated explicitly rather than implied.

An **external risk list**: conditions that could disrupt the pathway that are external to the funder's control. Risk listing does not disqualify a pathway; it makes the program's situational awareness visible to institutional funders and analysts.

**Dependency declarations**: a list of other pathway identifiers that this pathway depends on. Where pathway 14 (for example) cannot advance unless pathway 1 and pathway 10 have produced their claimed outputs, those dependencies are declared explicitly. Dependency declarations are what make cross-program portfolio analysis structurally possible: a portfolio analysis tool can aggregate dependency declarations across programs to identify convergence points (multiple pathways sharing a downstream outcome), gaps (outcome nodes with no active pathways contributing), and leverage points (single outputs contributing to multiple downstream pathways).

A **Theory of Build tag**: a declaration of whether this pathway operates primarily in the build layer (Inputs to Outputs, producing artifacts, infrastructure, or capacity) or the change layer (Outputs to Outcomes, producing measurable shifts in conditions). The tag takes one of two values: `build` or `change`. A pathway tagged `build` corresponds to a CROSS build-obligation round: the causal claim is that a specified deliverable will be produced and made accessible. A pathway tagged `change` corresponds to a CROSS change-obligation round: the causal claim is that a specified measurable condition will shift from a declared FROM state to a declared TO state. A staged program may have pathways at both layers; the tag identifies each pathway's layer rather than the program's overall orientation. This tag makes explicit the distinction, often obscured in grant portfolios, between investments that produce capacity and investments that use capacity to produce change. Both are valid; conflating them produces incommensurable portfolio data.

For pathways tagged `build`, an optional **intended outcome level** field declares which outcome level the built artifact is designed to enable: `short-term`, `intermediate`, or `long-term`. A tool built to enable short-term outcomes (for example, a monitoring dashboard showing monthly conditions data) has a different obligation relationship to the portfolio than infrastructure built to enable long-term systemic change (for example, institutional decision-making capacity). Both are build-obligation investments, but the intended outcome level determines how the build pathway connects to the change pathways that depend on it, and what the portfolio analysis should expect to see downstream. A build pathway with no declared intended outcome level is a build without a declared purpose in the ToC chain.

A **causality stance tag**: a declaration of whether this pathway's causal claims are made at attribution stance or contribution stance, as defined in Part IV. Attribution stance pathways require counterfactual or exogeneity evidence at the completion gate. Contribution stance pathways require a named causal pathway, acknowledged co-factors, and a materiality argument. The tag takes one of two values: `attribution` or `contribution`. Where a pathway is tagged `attribution`, the rounds advancing it should configure their completion gates at counterfactual reference level. Where tagged `contribution`, rounds advancing it should configure completion gates to require the contribution analysis elements. A mismatch between a pathway's causality stance tag and its associated rounds' gate configurations is a conformance flag that any audit of the pathway registry should surface.

A **specification directionality tag**: a declaration of whether this pathway was specified in planning mode or delivery mode. Planning mode means the pathway was defined starting from the desired target node (outcome) and working backward to identify the required source node (output or process) and the causal mechanism that connects them. Delivery mode means the pathway was defined starting from the available source node (what the program can produce) and working forward to claim a target node. The distinction matters for portfolio analysis: a portfolio in which all pathways are delivery-mode specified may lack coverage of intermediate outcomes that the program's goal requires, because delivery-mode specification naturally clusters around what programs can already do rather than what the goal requires. The directionality tag does not disqualify a pathway; it makes the portfolio's planning coherence visible to institutional funders and analysts. Programs may specify pathways in either mode. Programs declaring OECD DAC alignment are encouraged to include at least one planning-mode pathway connecting their program's activities to the Effectiveness or Impact criteria, since those criteria require outcome-level evidence that delivery-mode specification alone may not produce.

The directionality distinction maps predictably onto outcome levels. Delivery-mode pathways naturally cluster at the short-term outcome level because they start from what a program can already produce and claim forward to the nearest visible change. Planning-mode pathways are required to reach intermediate and long-term outcomes, because only by starting from the desired long-term condition and working backward can a program identify what outputs and short-term outcomes are actually necessary to reach it. A portfolio in which all pathways are delivery-mode specified will therefore tend to show strong short-term outcome coverage and structural gaps at the intermediate and long-term levels, not because programs do not care about those outcomes, but because delivery-mode specification does not reach them. This is the most common form of portfolio incoherence that ToC-level portfolio analysis surfaces.

---

#### 4. Round Linkage

Each CROSS round is linked to the program-level ToC declaration through a round linkage record. The round linkage record contains:

The **primary pathway identifiers** the round's projects are designed to advance. A round may advance multiple pathways; the primary pathways are the ones against which round-level evidence is primarily assessed.

The **primary ToC level** the round operates at. For build-obligation rounds, the primary level is typically Outputs. For change-obligation rounds, the primary level is typically Short-term Outcomes or Intermediate Outcomes. This linkage makes the CROSS obligation mode and ToC level explicitly connected, enabling analysts to aggregate build-obligation evidence as output evidence and change-obligation evidence as outcome evidence without manual interpretation.

A **portfolio position declaration**: whether this round is an initiating round (establishing a new process or output with no prior CROSS round in the same pathway), a continuation round (advancing an established pathway), or a convergence round (designed to produce an output or outcome that depends on pathways established by other programs or other funders).

---

#### 5. Field-Level ToC Tagging (WALKRI Extension)

Where a CROSS-conformant program also adopts WALKRI for field specification quality, each application field may carry an optional ToC component tag within the WALKRI field specification. The tag specifies which ToC level the field's evidence is designed to capture (Process, Output, Short-term Outcome, Intermediate Outcome, Long-term Outcome) and, where a pathway identifier is applicable, which pathway identifier the field contributes evidence for.

Field-level ToC tagging enables data aggregation by ToC component across all applicants in a round, and by pathway identifier across all rounds in a program. A grants data analyst or institutional funder can then query: what is the aggregate evidence from all projects advancing pathway 3 across all rounds? This query is only structurally possible when the tagging is present at the field level; without it, the aggregation requires manual interpretation of each project's evidence.

Field-level ToC tagging is not required for CROSS or WALKRI conformance. It is the mechanism by which the program-level ToC declaration becomes analytically productive rather than declarative.

---

#### 6. Portfolio Analysis Enablement

The program-level ToC declaration, the pathway registry, the dependency declarations, and the field-level ToC tags together constitute the data layer required for the portfolio analysis that institutional funders and grants strategists require. Specifically, they enable:

**Convergence analysis**: identifying where multiple projects or programs are contributing to the same intermediate or long-term outcome node, and whether their contributions are complementary, parallel, or in tension.

**Gap analysis**: identifying intermediate outcome nodes that are declared as target nodes in the pathway registry but have no active rounds or projects currently contributing evidence toward them.

**Leverage point identification**: identifying output nodes or short-term outcome nodes that contribute to multiple downstream pathways, where investment concentration produces disproportionate portfolio-level effect.

**Sequencing analysis**: where dependency declarations are present, identifying whether the rounds that produce upstream pathway outputs are sequenced to complete before the rounds that depend on them begin.

**Efficiency analysis**: where multiple programs in the same portfolio declare dependencies on the same intermediate outcome, identifying whether the portfolio's total investment in those programs is proportionate to the convergence point's position in the overall causal chain.

This analysis, previously produced manually by experienced evaluators over months-long engagements, is structurally enabled by the data layer this Part specifies. A portfolio analysis tool built on CROSS+WALKRI-conformant data with program-level ToC declarations can produce the dependency visualization programmatically from round submission data, refreshed at each gate.

---

#### 7. Conformance

The program-level ToC declaration is recommended but not required for CROSS conformance at the round level. A round can be fully CROSS-conformant without a program-level ToC declaration.

Where a program-level ToC declaration is present, the following are required for that declaration to be CROSS-conformant: a goal statement, at least one intermediate outcomes register entry, at least one pathway in the pathway registry with a causal mechanism statement and a critical assumptions list, and a round linkage record for each active CROSS round within the program.

Dependency declarations, external risk lists, field-level ToC tags, and portfolio position declarations are optional elements that increase the analytical value of the declaration without affecting conformance status.

## Part XI: Funder Obligations and Redress

CROSS specifies what funded interventions are obligated to demonstrate and how reviewers must assess the evidence. It also imposes obligations on funders: to publish gate configurations, declare verification infrastructure, and apply gate criteria as published. This Part specifies the minimum mechanisms by which funders are held to their published obligations, and how applicants and grantees may seek redress when they believe those obligations have been violated.

This Part applies to all CROSS-conformant programs. Configurable elements are tiered by program scale.

---

#### 1. Funder Obligation Requirements

In addition to the obligations stated elsewhere in this standard, a CROSS-conformant funder must:

- Maintain a record of gate configurations, infrastructure declarations, and documented waivers for each round and stage.
- Apply gate criteria consistently with the published configuration for that round.
- Document any deviation from the published configuration or infrastructure declaration, including justification and the party under whose authority the deviation was made.

Failure to meet these obligations is a funder obligation violation. It does not automatically invalidate individual awards, but it does create an obligation to remediate where affected applicants or grantees are identified.

**Structured dataset publication.** At the close of each round, a CROSS-conformant program must publish a machine-readable structured dataset containing, for each funded grant: the indicator specification (all fields specified in Part V), the obligation mode, the gate configuration applied, the evidence scope and strength at each gate, the causality stance declared, the sufficiency position declared, the sustainability stance at continuation where applicable, and the unintended outcomes disclosed at completion. The dataset must not include personally identifying information beyond what applicants have consented to make public.

The dataset must be published in a format that can be ingested by a data analysis pipeline without manual transformation, with field-level schema documentation and each field's operational definition available as part of the dataset metadata. Programs that have adopted WALKRI automatically satisfy this requirement because WALKRI-conformant fields carry the required metadata. Programs that have not adopted WALKRI must supply equivalent metadata describing the construction of each indicator field. A minimum retention period of three years from the round close is recommended.

This obligation serves analysts and portfolio strategists who need cross-program comparable data, and also provides the raw data underlying the institutional funder portfolio summary below. The structured dataset and the portfolio summary address different audiences and are not interchangeable.

**Institutional funder portfolio-level output.** Where a CROSS-conformant program has a declared institutional funder of the program (cohort 5: an entity that funds the grants program itself, such as a foundation board, government appropriation, DAO token holders, or comparable authority), the program must produce a portfolio-level summary at the close of each round. The summary must be accessible to the institutional funder without requiring them to read individual grantee records. It must contain: the number of applications received and the number funded, the total amount disbursed, the distribution of funded projects across obligation modes and ToC hierarchy levels, a summary of sufficiency positions declared across funded grantees (how many were at critical gap, partial, approaching, or surplus at the declared scope), the unintended outcomes disclosed at completion gates across the cohort, and the sustainability stance distribution (how many funded projects reported sustained, conditional, or dependent outcomes at the continuation gate where applicable). Where a program-level ToC declaration is present, the portfolio summary must additionally include a pathway coverage report: which declared pathways had active funded projects, which had none, and whether any convergence or gap findings from the portfolio analysis are material to the institutional funder's obligations. The portfolio-level summary is not a separate reporting burden; it is an aggregation of data the program has already collected through its gate architecture. Programs with more than ten funded projects per round should produce this summary in a machine-readable format exportable to the institutional funder's reporting infrastructure.

---

#### 2. Internal Review and Clarification

Before formal redress is sought, applicants and grantees must have access to an internal clarification mechanism. At minimum, this mechanism must provide:

- A named point of contact to whom applicants and grantees can submit questions about how gate decisions were applied in their specific case.
- A requirement that the funder provide a written response explaining how the decision was consistent with the published gate configuration, the applicable rubric, and the evidence on record.
- A time bound for response that is shorter than the round's typical cycle time, so that clarifications can inform subsequent applications.

The internal clarification mechanism is not an appeals body. It is a structured means of obtaining a record of the funder's reasoning and ensuring that gate decisions are anchored to the published configuration.

---

#### 3. Structured Appeals on Procedural Grounds

CROSS recognizes appeals on procedural grounds: claims that the funder did not follow its own published gate configuration, infrastructure declaration, or conflict of interest framework in a way that materially affected an applicant or grantee.

Where a round specification pre-commitment was published before the application window opened (Part IV, Round Specification Pre-commitment), that commitment record is the primary evidentiary basis for procedural appeals alleging that gate criteria changed after submission. An applicant with access to the committed specification and the applied criteria can demonstrate a discrepancy without relying on the funder's own records. Programs that have not published a pre-commitment place a higher burden of proof on applicants making criteria-change claims, and a correspondingly higher obligation on the appeals body to investigate the claim through alternative evidence.

A CROSS-conformant program must:

- Publish an appeals procedure that specifies:
    - What decisions are appealable (for example, entry gate rejections, completion verification failures, continuation denials).
    - The grounds on which appeals may be made (procedural non-conformance, conflict of interest violations, undisclosed changes to gate configuration).
    - The time window within which an appeal must be filed after the decision is communicated.
- Designate an appeals body that is structurally separate from the original decision-making panel. In small programs, this may be a board member or external advisor who did not participate in the original decision.
- Commit to issuing a written determination on each appeal that states:
    - Whether a funder obligation violation occurred.
    - Whether the original decision stands, is modified, or is reversed.
    - What remediation, if any, will be offered.

Appeals under this Part assess whether the funder followed CROSS-conformant procedures. They do not reopen substantive scoring absent a documented procedural error.

---

#### 4. Independent Redress Mechanism (Optional)

Larger programs and multi-round ecosystems may configure an independent redress mechanism analogous to the Independent Redress Mechanism operated by institutional funders such as the Green Climate Fund and the World Bank. Where configured, the mechanism:

- Accepts complaints from applicants, grantees, or affected parties who allege that the funder has violated CROSS or its own published policies.
- Operates independently from the program's grant decision-making structure.
- May review documentation, interview parties, and issue findings and recommendations.

For CROSS conformance, the independent mechanism is optional. Where a program chooses to operate without one, the funder must state this explicitly in the published round configuration and publish the decision as part of the program-level specification.

---

#### 5. Linkage to Failure Record Obligation

Where a funder obligation violation has materially affected the status of a grant (for example, premature termination, unjustified completion failure, or denial of continuation funding), the case must be recorded in the funder's failure record, alongside the remediation offered. Future versions of this standard may make the failure record a conformance requirement for all CROSS-conformant programs.

## Appendix: Open Design Questions (v0.2.0)

**Single-outcome constraint.** Whether CROSS should require one primary indicator per application or permit multiple. Current position: permit multiple, require one designated primary indicator. The primary indicator is the one against which the entry specification, progress verification, and completion verification gates are primarily assessed.

**Reporting frequency.** Whether reporting frequency requirements belong in the standard or the guidance. Current position: guidance, calibrated by the Coordination Scaling Standard.

**Cost-effectiveness as a required field.** Whether cost-effectiveness assessment should be a required field at the continuation gate for all change-obligation programs or remain advisory. Current position: required for continuation in programs whose round configuration specifies Stage 2 or above; advisory elsewhere.

**Round-level common indicators.** Whether funders may designate indicators common to all applications in a round, in addition to applicant-specified indicators. Current position: yes, a valid configurator option. Common indicators do not change the applicant-specification principle for all other indicators.

**Failure record obligation.** Whether CROSS-conformant funders are required to maintain a public record of terminated grants, missed milestones, and declined continuation applications. Current position: strongly indicated by the research but not yet specified as a conformance requirement. To be addressed in v0.3.0.

**DAOIP-5 relationship.** CROSS references DAOIP-5 (DAOstar/Metagov) as a companion coordinating instrument specifying round metadata structure and ecosystem interoperability. DAOIP-5 handles how round data is described and exchanged between systems. CROSS handles what funded interventions must demonstrate. The formal relationship is specified in Part IX-A.

## Part XII: Institutional Framework Alignment

CROSS is designed for adoption by any grants program operating under any institutional or ecosystem framework. This Part documents how CROSS's obligation architecture relates to established frameworks, so that organizations already operating under those frameworks understand what CROSS conformance produces.

### OECD DAC Evaluation Criteria (DCD/DAC(2019)58/FINAL)

The six OECD DAC criteria (Relevance, Coherence, Effectiveness, Efficiency, Impact, Sustainability) each map onto CROSS's gate architecture.

Relevance is addressed at the entry specification gate: does this intervention respond to the named FROM state in the defined population? The entry gate requires the applicant to name a specific, measurable condition in a defined population with a sourced baseline, which is the structural expression of Relevance.

Coherence is addressed through the coherence disclosure requirement in Part IX-A: how does this intervention relate to other known interventions in the same problem area? For programs that declare OECD DAC alignment, coherence disclosure is required at both the entry and progress gates.

Effectiveness is addressed at the progress verification gate: is the intervention producing evidence of movement from the FROM state toward the TO state?

Efficiency is addressed through the data cost estimation field in the indicator specification (Part V) and, where activated, the sustainability plan requirement: are resources deployed in a way that produces the claimed change within the operational budget?

Impact is addressed at the completion verification gate: does the completion evidence demonstrate that the TO state was reached as specified?

Sustainability is addressed through the continuation specification gate and the sustainability plan: will the change persist beyond the grant period, and under what conditions?

A CROSS-conformant program with OECD DAC alignment declared satisfies all six DAC criteria structurally through its gate architecture. The criteria are not a separate procedural checklist; they are embodied in the gate design.

### IRIS+ (Global Impact Investing Network, v5.3c)

CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification (Part V) is a strict superset of IRIS+'s indicator metadata requirements. IRIS+ requires: indicator name, definition, data type, unit, calculation methodology, data collection instructions, evidence rationale, and SDG linkage. CROSS requires all of these through its eleven fields, plus cumulative or non-cumulative designation, disaggregation categories with ratchet protection, the FROM state baseline, and the TO state target.

A CROSS-conformant indicator specification automatically satisfies IRIS+ metadata requirements. Programs that declare an IRIS+ Core Metrics Set as their measurement framework will find that CROSS's indicator specification fields carry all required IRIS+ metadata without duplication. IRIS+ indicators referenced in CROSS fields should use the IRIS+ indicator identifier type defined in WALKRI Part VI Section 6.2.

### USAID PIRS (Performance Indicator Reference Sheet)

USAID Automated Directives System (ADS) 201 requires each performance indicator in a program's Performance Management Plan to be documented in a Performance Indicator Reference Sheet (PIRS). A PIRS requires nine documentation elements per indicator.

CROSS Part V's eleven-field indicator specification is a strict superset of the nine PIRS required elements. The mapping is as follows. Indicator name (Field 1) maps to PIRS Indicator name. Rationale for indicator (Field 2) maps to PIRS Rationale and relationship to project/activity. Measurement form and evidence classification (Field 3) combined with Operational definition (Field 4) maps to PIRS Indicator definition and unit of measure; CROSS separates these into two fields for greater specificity. Construction and aggregation methodology (Field 5) maps to PIRS Notes on indicator limitations, required by CROSS as a construction rule rather than a caveat. Disaggregation (Field 7) maps to PIRS Disaggregation, with the additional CROSS requirement of a disaggregation ratchet: reported categories cannot be dropped in subsequent periods. Data source and collection method (Field 8) combined with Data cost estimation (Field 9) maps to PIRS Data acquisition. Baseline / FROM state (Field 10) maps to PIRS Baseline value and data source. Target / TO state (Field 11) maps to PIRS Targets.

The data quality assessment element of PIRS (the ninth element) is addressed by WALKRI's five data quality standards: Validity, Integrity, Precision, Reliability, and Timeliness. These five standards are structurally identical to USAID's five data quality criteria specified in ADS 201 and the Performance Management and Evaluation Policy (2016). A program using WALKRI-conformant fields satisfies the PIRS data quality assessment requirement because the quality is built into the instrument design rather than assessed retrospectively.

CROSS Part IX-B's program-level Theory of Change declaration maps to the USAID Results Framework as follows: Goal corresponds to Development Objective; Long-term Outcome or Intermediate Outcome corresponds to Intermediate Result; Short-term Outcome corresponds to Sub-Intermediate Result; Output corresponds to Output.

The result is that a CROSS+WALKRI-conformant program satisfies USAID PIRS documentation requirements as a structural consequence of conformance. No additional documentation work is required beyond what CROSS and WALKRI already specify.

The full mapping is documented in the published USAID-PIRS-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### Pluralistic Grants Program Framework (Brennan, ETH Denver, February 2025)

The pluralistic grants program framework identifies eight components of a mature grants program. Five of these are structurally embodied in CROSS.

Clear goals and social impact alignment is the entry specification gate: the applicant must name a specific, measurable condition in a defined population, which is the structural form of goal specificity.

Trust-based relationships with grantees is addressed through the Bidirectional Precision Principle inherited from WALKRI: the funder must specify gate criteria with the same precision they require of applicants specifying indicators. The precision obligation runs in both directions because the power runs asymmetrically.

Monitoring and evaluation is the progress and completion verification gate architecture.

Responsible pluralism is CROSS's three-mode obligation architecture: different kinds of work warrant different obligation structures (build, change, retroactive), and the funder makes an explicit choice about which structures are appropriate for which kinds of work, rather than imposing a single theory of change on all applicants.

Long-term perspective is the continuation specification gate: what a project must demonstrate to advance to a next stage or epoch, separate from the completion of any single grant.

The unrestricted funding component of the pluralistic framework is explicitly outside CROSS's scope. CROSS does not cover unrestricted grants. A pluralistic program may use CROSS for its structured grants and operate unrestricted grants in parallel; CROSS does not require that all funding be structured.

### Logic Model Framework (W.K. Kellogg Foundation)

The logic model's five columns (inputs, activities, outputs, outcomes, impact) map onto CROSS's gate and indicator architecture.

Inputs are addressed through the concurrent funding disclosure requirement (Part VI): what resources, including other funding, are entering the intervention?

Activities and outputs are addressed in the build obligation mode: the deliverable specification with falsifiable completion criteria is the build obligation's equivalent of the activities and outputs columns.

Outcomes are the change obligation mode's FROM-TO state architecture: the FROM state is the pre-intervention baseline, the TO state is the expected outcome, and the eleven-field indicator specification is the operationalization of the outcome column.

Impact is the long-term condition that continuation specifications address: what does sustained change in the TO state direction produce at the population level over multiple grant cycles?

### DAOIP-5 and the Metagov Grants Ecosystem

CROSS round configurations are expressible as DAOIP-5 GrantPool objects. Gate evidence and grantee reports are expressible as DAOIP-5 Attestation objects. CROSS gate attestations are cryptographically attributed, publicly verifiable records; the specific format used (W3C Verifiable Credentials, EAS attestations, DAOIP-3 VCs for DAOIP ecosystem programs, or other verifiable mechanisms) is determined by the program's infrastructure and audience. The complete interoperability specification is in Part IX-A.

The combined effect: a CROSS-conformant program simultaneously satisfies institutional reporting requirements (OECD DAC, IRIS+, Logic Model) and Web3 ecosystem interoperability requirements (DAOIP-5 and compatible attestation infrastructure), making its data and obligation records consumable by both institutional and decentralized infrastructure without separate configuration.

### World Bank Results Framework

The World Bank Results Framework (RF) covers all Trust Fund grants and Investment Project Financing grants under Bank Policy. Its two indicator levels, PDO (Programme Development Objective) and IR (Intermediate Results), map to CROSS's outcome and output indicator levels in the Theory of Change architecture. The nine RF documentation elements per indicator (name, definition, unit, baseline, target, disaggregation, data source, collection methodology, responsible party) correspond directly to CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification. Programs reporting to World Bank Trust Funds that implement CROSS do not produce RF indicator documentation separately; the CROSS round specification produces it as a structural output. The full mapping is in the World-Bank-RF-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### UNDP Integrated Results and Resources Framework (IRRF) and ISO/UNDP 53001

The UNDP IRRF is the corporate results reporting architecture for all UNDP-administered programs, requiring PDO-level outcome indicators and IR-level output indicators with baselines, targets, data sources, collection methods, frequencies, and responsible parties. CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification satisfies all IRRF indicator documentation requirements at both levels. CROSS Part IX-B's Theory of Change hierarchy maps to the IRRF Results Chain: Goal corresponds to long-term impact, outcome-level indicators correspond to PDO indicators, and output-level indicators correspond to IR indicators.

ISO/UNDP 53001, the forthcoming international certifiable standard for SDG contribution claims (expected mid-2026; PAS 53002:2024 guidance available now), requires specifying the SDG target, a measurable indicator, a baseline, a target value, a causal mechanism, and an attribution stance. These requirements are structurally identical to CROSS's Change obligation mode applied at the SDG indicator level: CROSS's causality stance field is the direct expression of ISO/UNDP 53001's attribution claim requirement. Programs implementing CROSS do not need to redesign their indicator architecture to satisfy ISO/UNDP 53001; the conformant round specification satisfies it structurally. The full mapping is in the UNDP-IRRF-ISO53001-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### MCC Compact Logic and USAID Results Framework

The Millennium Challenge Corporation's Compact Logic hierarchy (Goal, Objective, Output, Activity) is the reference hierarchy for CROSS Part IX-B. CROSS's ToC hierarchy and causal pathway specification adopted this structure directly; a CROSS program-level ToC declaration is structurally identical to an MCC Compact Logic results chain. MCC's M&E Policy requires a results framework before implementation begins, quantitative benchmarks for each indicator, baselines measured before activities start, and targets with explicit assumptions. CROSS's entry specification gate requirement, combined with the eleven-field indicator specification, satisfies all four MCC M&E Policy requirements as a structural consequence of conformance. The full mapping is in the MCC-Compact-Logic-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### IFC Operating Principles for Impact Management (OPIM)

The IFC's nine Operating Principles for Impact Management (2019) are the de facto standard for impact investors, with 160+ institutional signatories. Principles 4 (assess expected impact systematically before commitment) and 6 (monitor progress of each investment against expected impact) are the primary alignment points with CROSS.

Principle 4 requires a defined methodology for assessing expected impact, with indicators and baselines established before commitment; this is structurally what CROSS's entry specification gate requires. Principle 6 requires tracking against predefined indicators throughout the investment; this is structurally what CROSS's progress and completion verification gates require. Principles 1 through 3 and 7 concern portfolio-level strategic intent, asset allocation, and manager requirements that are beyond the scope of a single grant round. Principle 8 (review, document, and improve) is addressed by CROSS's continuation gate architecture. Principle 9 (publicly disclose alignment) is satisfied by CROSS's requirement that round specifications be published before any application window opens. The full mapping is in the IFC-OPIM-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### OECD DAC Creditor Reporting System (CRS)

The OECD DAC Creditor Reporting System is the statistical reporting system that all 30+ bilateral DAC donor countries use to classify and report individual aid transactions. It is distinct from the OECD DAC Evaluation Criteria (Section 12.1 of this Part): evaluation criteria cover quality assessment; CRS covers transaction-level data reporting.

CRS sector codes (the five-digit OECD code list covering 60+ categories) map to CROSS Field 10 (institutional framework alignment): programs declaring OECD DAC CRS alignment record their sector code in Field 10. CRS project description, dates, and recipient fields map to CROSS organizational identity declarations and ToC architecture. CRS financial flow fields (commitment amounts, disbursement records) are transactional metadata outside CROSS's scope. For programs reporting to both IATI and CRS, IATI compatibility is established separately; CRS alignment follows from the IATI bridge, since IATI v2.03 produces CRS-compatible output. The full mapping is in the OECD-DAC-CRS-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### DCED Standard for Results Measurement

The Donor Committee for Enterprise Development (DCED) Standard for Results Measurement is used by 100+ programs across 50+ countries and is explicitly interoperable with IRIS+ (joint interoperability document published at iris.thegiin.org). The seven DCED elements map to CROSS as follows: Element 1 (articulate the ToC) is addressed by CROSS Part IX-B; Element 2 (plan data collection on changes) is addressed by the entry specification gate and eleven-field indicator specification; Element 4 (collect data on contribution) is addressed by the progress and completion verification gates; Element 6 (manage the complexity of attributing results) is addressed by CROSS's causality stance field and attribution claim strength dimension; Element 7 (use performance data to improve programs) is addressed by the continuation gate architecture. Elements 3 and 5 (measure changes in the wider market and poverty impacts) require deliberate indicator design choices within the CROSS architecture and are not automatic.

Since IRIS+ and DCED are explicitly interoperable, programs satisfying CROSS's IRIS+ alignment (Section 12.2 of this Part) satisfy the DCED Standard's results measurement requirements through the same pathway. The full mapping is in the DCED-Standard-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### Impact Management Norms (IMP Five Dimensions)

The Impact Management Norms (formerly the Impact Management Project, convened by GIIN, SASB, GRI, PRI, and others) organize impact evidence around five dimensions: What (the outcome and its importance), Who (the affected parties and their baseline conditions), How Much (scale, depth, and duration), Contribution (additionality versus counterfactual), and Risk (the probability that impact differs from expectations). GIIN explicitly maps IRIS+ to the IMP Five Dimensions, so CROSS's IRIS+ alignment (Section 12.2 of this Part) establishes IMP Five Dimensions compatibility through the same pathway.

The direct mapping is: What corresponds to CROSS indicator definition (Field 2) and criterion intent; Who corresponds to CROSS population scope (Field 6) and disaggregation requirements; How Much corresponds to CROSS baseline (Field 10) and target (Field 11) with construction and aggregation methodology (Field 5); Contribution corresponds to CROSS causality stance (Field 11) and the IMP contribution vocabulary added to Part VI-A at v0.3.7; Risk corresponds to CROSS's concurrent funding disclosure, sufficiency position declaration, and the continuation gate's sustainability assessment. The full mapping is in the Impact-Management-Norms-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### FCDO Programme Operating Framework (PrOF)

The UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office Programme Operating Framework covers all FCDO programme spending and defines required processes, evidence standards, and a results chain drawn from the OECD DAC criteria. All FCDO activity data is published to IATI and DevTracker. "Economic self-sufficiency" can be documented at dozens of different income thresholds across different state CSBG systems; without WALKRI's operational definition and conformance threshold requirements applied before any client intake form is deployed, ROMA data remains non-comparable across agencies in the same network. WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements, applied to CAA intake instruments before any program year begins, transform ROMA's goal domains from broad national categories into field-level specifications generating commensurable data across the network. The full mapping is in the CSBG-ROMA-Results-Oriented-Management-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### HUD Continuum of Care System Performance Measures

HUD's seven System Performance Measures for the Continuum of Care program constitute one of the most consequential pre-specified indicator sets in federal homeless services: performance on these measures directly affects funding awards through Section 3A scoring in e-snaps, making the completion gate a financial consequence with immediate reprogramming implications for underperforming Continuums. The seven measures are published by HUD with programming specifications before each annual competition opens, satisfying CROSS's entry specification gate in the strongest form available under the federal grant system. HMIS, the required data infrastructure through which all CoC-funded programs collect client-level data, is the attestation corpus: a persistent, queryable record of verified performance claims used to calculate all seven measures and published annually in the AHAR.

WALKRI's pre-publication requirements apply to HMIS intake instruments at the point where CoC-funded case managers collect client information, which is where the data quality problems that distort all seven measures originate. HUD's HMIS Data Standards Manual provides field definitions at the data system level but does not specify the classification rules that case managers must apply when a client's housing situation is ambiguous, when a reconnection is uncertain, or when an exit destination is contested. Without WALKRI-level specification for fields like "exit to permanent housing" (Measure 7) and "housing situation at program entry" (Measure 1), individual case managers make inconsistent decisions producing HMIS data that is technically conformant but generating non-comparable performance scores across providers in the same Continuum. The full mapping is in the HUD-Continuum-of-Care-System-Performance-Measures-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### LIHEAP Performance Measures

LIHEAP's three statutory performance measures, established under 42 U.S.C. § 8629(b) and formally published in a June 2013 Federal Register notice, are required for all 50 states and DC before any program year begins. All three are Change obligation mode indicators. Measure 1 (average energy burden reduction) requires baseline and post-assistance calculations at the household level, corresponding to CROSS Fields 4 and 5. Measure 2 (energy crisis prevention) requires a counterfactual determination, corresponding to CROSS's causality stance field; this is one of the few instances in federal human services reporting where a statutory performance measure explicitly encodes a counterfactual element. Measure 3 (energy restoration after crisis or disconnection) requires a verified binary status change, corresponding to the completion gate's outcome evidence requirement.

The three measures' statutory origin creates a nominally uniform national dataset. Their operational definition gap destroys comparability in practice: "energy crisis," "energy burden," and "energy restoration" are statutory terms that state caseworkers operationalize differently without field-level definitions specifying what documentation qualifies and what threshold applies. WALKRI's five pre-publication requirements, applied to all 50 states' LIHEAP intake instruments before any program year begins, transform the three statutory measures from nominal uniform indicators into genuinely commensurable outcome metrics capable of supporting cross-state comparison and national program improvement. The full mapping is in the LIHEAP-Performance-Measures-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### UN Trust Fund to End Violence Against Women (UNTF)

The UNTF's threshold-based tiered evaluation model is a formally published, scaled version of CROSS's independent attestation pathway: the USD 150,000 threshold determines whether independent attestation is grantee-managed (larger grants) or co-managed with the UNTF Secretariat (smaller grants), operationalizing the CROSS design principle that attestation independence scales with grant scope. All grantees report on pre-specified KPIs every six months, corresponding to CROSS's progress verification gate, and all completed evaluations are published through the UNTF Learning Hub, constituting the attestation corpus: a publicly accessible, searchable repository of verified performance claims available to other funders, researchers, and the Secretariat for portfolio-level learning.

WALKRI's pre-publication requirements address the layer at which UNTF evaluation data quality is most often lost: the KPI intake instruments through which grantees collect baseline data before programme implementation begins. A KPI field asking grantees to report "number of women who report reduced experience of violence" requires, before any grantee deploys a survey, a precise operational definition of what counts as a reportable experience of violence in that programme context, what instrument or interview protocol constitutes the evidence form, and what response threshold distinguishes meaningful programme impact from a measurement artifact. Without these specifications applied before baseline collection begins, UNTF evaluations produce data that satisfies the evaluation requirement formally but cannot support the portfolio-level learning that the Learning Hub is built to enable. The full mapping is in the UNTF-UN-Women-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### CRS ProPack MEAL System

CRS ProPack I and ProPack II constitute one of the most structurally complete MEAL standards in the humanitarian and development sector. ProPack embeds its five-step MEAL system design inside project design itself, requiring that indicators, data collection instruments, and accountability mechanisms all be finalized before any project activity begins. This pre-activity design requirement is the functional equivalent of CROSS's entry specification gate at the sub-award partner level: implementing partners do not receive a blank slate for evaluation at project launch; they co-develop the full MEAL architecture during the design phase. CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification maps directly to the MEAL plan documentation that ProPack requires, and CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy enforces ProPack's rule that Theory of Change must precede indicator selection rather than follow it.

ProPack's accountability mechanisms identify a structural gap in CROSS's current architecture. Community feedback loops and complaint response mechanisms are not optional add-ons in ProPack; they are structural MEAL system components with defined collection, review, and response requirements. CROSS's coherence disclosure does not extend to beneficiary-facing accountability in this explicit form, and this gap is a candidate for formalization in a future CROSS revision. WALKRI's pre-publication audit applies to every intake field in partner data collection instruments, including feedback and complaint intake instruments: before any partner data collection opens, criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, and conformance threshold must be specified for each field. The full mapping is in the CRS-ProPack-MEAL-System-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### World Vision LEAP Programme Cycle

The LEAP framework's six-component programme cycle introduces a structural innovation absent from every other framework in the corpus: the formally named Reflect stage. In all other covered frameworks, the programme cycle ends at final evaluation and close-out. LEAP requires a distinct stage between the close of one cycle and the opening of the next, in which learning from monitoring and evaluation is reviewed, synthesized, and institutionalized before the next design phase begins. This is not the completion gate of the closing programme and not the entry specification gate of the opening programme. It is a third structural role that CROSS's four-gate architecture does not currently accommodate; this statement names it the inter-cycle reflection stage and identifies it as a candidate for formalization in future CROSS revision.

The LEAP Design stage satisfies CROSS's entry specification gate exactly: baseline and evaluation plan are completed before any programme activity begins. WALKRI's pre-publication audit applies at the Design stage, where data collection instruments are developed as part of the MEAL plan before sharing with implementing partners or beneficiaries. The Community Based Feedback and Response Mechanism, integrated across all six LEAP components, creates the same beneficiary accountability gap identified in the CRS ProPack statement: CROSS does not yet name beneficiary feedback systems as a required gate element, and WALKRI's pre-publication requirements should be applied to CBFRM intake fields with the same rigor as to indicator-level data collection. The full mapping is in the World-Vision-LEAP-Programme-Cycle-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### National Endowment for Democracy Evaluation Framework

NED's explicit, formally stated rejection of activity completion as valid evaluation evidence is the most direct regulatory form of CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy identified in any foundation grant program. NED's grant conditions require outcome evidence, and activity documentation does not satisfy the Evaluation Plan requirement. CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification enforces this at the structural level: an activity-level indicator paired with a causality stance claiming outcome contribution fails CROSS's specification requirements, because the causality stance cannot be supported by activity completion data alone. NED and CROSS are structurally aligned on this point: NED imposes as a grant condition what CROSS imposes as an indicator specification requirement.

The Cumulative Assessment Report is the most significant new primitive in this statement. No other framework in the corpus requires a retrospective multi-cycle evaluation spanning the full history of a multi-year grantee relationship and assessing accumulated impact across all prior cycles. The CAR is neither a final evaluation of the most recent cycle nor a planning document for the next. WALKRI's pre-publication audit, applied consistently at each cycle's entry specification gate, creates exactly the archival record the CAR requires: because each cycle's indicator specifications are committed to before data collection begins and preserved as part of the conformance record, the CAR can draw on a consistent, documented evidence base rather than reconstructing what each cycle's indicators were intended to measure from retrospective inference. The full mapping is in the NED-National-Endowment-for-Democracy-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### Optimism Retro Funding Impact Evaluation Methodology (2025)

The 2025 Retro Funding methodology represents the most structurally developed implementation of retroactive obligation mode in the CROSS+WALKRI coverage map. The weighted impact metrics formula, published and committed to before any application opens, is CROSS's entry specification gate expressed as a computational protocol: the formula cannot change after submissions are received, satisfying the entry gate's irreversibility requirement. The Impact Chain dependency graph is a formal implementation of CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy at the ecosystem level, requiring each project to declare the causal pathway from its outputs through intermediate outcomes to the round's ecosystem goals. The human-in-the-loop override with published conditions is the methodology's most architecturally novel element: CROSS's completion gate pre-specified with an override mechanism, where the gate can be reopened only under defined conditions committed to in advance.

WALKRI's pre-publication requirements apply to the metrics collection layer, where the gap between a mathematically precise formula and consistent data quality is widest. Before any round opens, what evidence form satisfies each impact metric and what documentation threshold distinguishes a verified measurement from a self-reported estimate must be specified, so that the formula operates on inputs of equivalent verifiability. This statement supersedes the prior Optimism RetroPGF compatibility statement for the 2025 and later methodology; the prior statement remains applicable to Rounds 1 through 4. The full mapping is in the Optimism-Retro-Funding-Impact-Evaluation-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### SBIR/STTR Cross-Agency Commercialization Benchmarks

The SBIR/STTR statutory benchmarks (15 U.S.C. § 638) introduce the only portfolio-level accountability structure in the CROSS+WALKRI coverage map where the funder's entire grant portfolio must meet a published performance threshold or face conformance consequences. The Phase I-to-Phase II transition rate minimum (0.25) and the commercialization revenue benchmark ($100,000 average per Phase II award) do not apply to individual grantees; they apply to the agency's portfolio as a whole. CROSS's portfolio aggregation mechanism can express both benchmarks: the transition rate is the proportion of Phase I awards in a cohort that pass the continuation gate, and the revenue benchmark is the average of completion gate commercialization outcomes across the Phase II portfolio. Making these aggregates statutory minimums with published conformance consequences is the structural innovation this statement introduces: portfolio-level continuation benchmarks applied to the funder rather than to individual grantees.

WALKRI applied to Phase I completion instruments and Phase II reporting instruments before each award cycle opens means that the agency's conformance aggregate is built from individually verified data points. The $100,000 commercialization benchmark means nothing if the Phase II reporting instrument does not distinguish a binding sales contract from a letter of intent, or does not specify what documentation standard substantiates a claimed third-party investment. Before any Phase I award is made, what evidence form satisfies the feasibility demonstration required for Phase II consideration must be specified, so that the transition rate reflects genuine technical readiness rather than variable review standards across program officers. The full mapping is in the SBIR-STTR-Commercialization-Benchmarks-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### Arnold Ventures Open Science Guidelines

Arnold Ventures publishes formal Open Science Guidelines requiring preregistration on a recognized registry before any data collection begins, pre-analysis plans filed before data collection for studies with causal identification, open data and code publication, and results publication regardless of direction of findings. These requirements implement the same pre-specification primitive that CROSS codifies: the measurement plan must be committed to in advance, independently and verifiably, before the behavior that generates data begins. Arnold requires this through registry preregistration; CROSS requires it through the entry specification gate. A CROSS-conformant program funded by Arnold can use its entry specification gate publication, recorded in the Attestation Corpus, as the preregistration mechanism. The eleven-field indicator specification produces the substantive content a pre-analysis plan must contain: outcome names, operational definitions, units, baselines, targets, analysis methodology, and the causality stance field captures the attribution declaration and counterfactual specification that distinguish a binding PAP from a descriptive research plan.

WALKRI addresses the weak-PAP problem directly. Arnold's PAP requirement is procedurally satisfied by any filed plan, regardless of whether the outcome definitions and analysis rules are specific enough to constrain post-hoc interpretation. A WALKRI-conformant intake field for each primary outcome specifies criterion intent, operational definition with inclusion and exclusion criteria, response form, evidence form, and conformance threshold, producing outcomes that are substantively preregistered rather than nominally so. WALKRI's conformance threshold requirement, applied to subgroup analysis commitments, forces specification of what evidence standard a subgroup finding must meet to be included in the report, closing the analytic flexibility that vaguely registered PAPs preserve. Arnold's open data, open code, and null-results publication requirements are outside CROSS's core scope and must be added as explicit gate criteria using the external standard reference mechanism in Part IV. The full mapping is in the Arnold-Ventures-Open-Science-Guidelines-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

### ESSA, WWC, and EIR: US Federal Education Evidence Framework

The US federal education evidence framework consists of three interlocking instruments: the Every Student Succeeds Act four-tier evidence hierarchy (codified at 20 USC 7801 and operationalized at 34 CFR 77.1 for all Department of Education discretionary grants), the What Works Clearinghouse Standards Handbook Version 4.0, which provides the federal methodology for rating studies against the ESSA tiers, and the Education Innovation and Research program, which operationalizes both within a competitive grant program requiring progressively stronger evidence to advance from Early-Phase to Mid-Phase to Expansion funding. ESSA Tier 4, the entry-level tier, requires a well-specified logic model or theory of change with an ongoing high-quality study committed to before implementation begins. This is the exact requirement CROSS's entry specification gate implements: the theory of change hierarchy, the eleven-field indicator specification, and the causality stance declaration must all be complete before any application opens. A program operating at ESSA Tier 4 that implements CROSS satisfies the logic model and ongoing study commitment requirements as a structural consequence of conformance. For ESSA Tiers 1 through 3, CROSS's causality stance field maps directly: attribution with RCT design is Tier 1, attribution with quasi-experimental design is Tier 2, and contribution with a comparison group is Tier 3, with the causal claim locked at the entry gate before data collection begins.

EIR's tiered escalation architecture, the most fully articulated version of progressive evidence requirements in US domestic grant-making, corresponds to CROSS's continuation gate. CROSS's continuation gate specifies what a grantee must demonstrate at the close of one funding period to qualify for the next, including the evidence type, strength, and comparison group required. An EIR program using CROSS configures the continuation gate to require evidence meeting WWC quasi-experimental standards for Early-to-Mid-Phase progression, and sizable, significant impact findings from an independent evaluation meeting WWC Tier 1 standards for Mid-Phase-to-Expansion progression. WALKRI addresses the structural underspecification problem at EIR's Tier 4 entry point: programs routinely declare an ongoing evaluation without specifying what it will measure, how, or to what standard. A WALKRI-conformant intake field for the evaluation commitment requires criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, and conformance threshold before any applicant submits a proposal, producing evaluation commitments that are substantively preregistered. The full mapping is in the ESSA-WWC-EIR-Education-Evidence-Framework-CROSS-WALKRI compatibility statement (CC0).

## Part XIII: CRAFT Inheritance Receipt

This Part is CROSS's inheritance receipt under CRAFT Section 10. It declares in one place the full inheritance under which CROSS was built and operates: which of CRAFT's six conditions CROSS realizes and how, which Coordination Structural Integrity Suite standards CROSS rests on and what each requires of it, and the Frame Language vocabulary discipline CROSS follows. CRAFT requires a domain application to carry this declaration. Without it, CROSS would be a chain quality standard for grants rather than a CRAFT-conformant domain application, whatever the substance of its inheritance.

The receipt points at upstream standards by name and version. It does not restate their content; that content lives in those standards and in the per-pass verification records. When an upstream standard revises, the receipt does not go stale in its wording: it enters a version-bump-pending state for that standard, the verification is re-run against the new version, and the version stamp is updated before the receipt is returned to current status. This shared version-coupling procedure is the fifth element of every activity profile below; it is stated once here and applies to each declared inheritance. A revision that does not trigger re-assessment of the affected declaration is a propagation failure under Condition 6 at the specification layer.

The receipt rests on eleven one-to-one conformance passes conducted on 2026-06-02, each reading CROSS in full and then one upstream standard in full, re-assessed on 2026-06-04 against the CRAFT and Precision-First Design Standard revisions of that date. A background state declared `background-satisfied` is anchored to the 2026-06-02 verification. A background state declared `background-unchecked` is a named gap that any conformance review must surface; CRAFT permits declaring an inheritance gap as a legible known-gap rather than closing it, and an undeclared omission is the failure this discipline exists to prevent.

### 1. Chain-level component: how CROSS realizes CRAFT's six conditions

**Condition 1, decision context.** A CROSS round specifies its decision before any application is received. The obligation mode (Part III) names what the round decides; the funder's published gate configuration and eligibility criteria name the boundary population and the frame by which applicants enter (Part IV); the FROM-TO state for change obligation, or the deliverable specification for build obligation, is the estimand (Parts III and V). The primary claim's object type is declared through the indicator measurement form (Part V): build-obligation and retroactive completion claims are criterion-type, anchored to named completion criteria; change-obligation claims are construct-type with a causal-formative indicator direction, their indicators constituting the measured condition rather than reflecting a latent construct behind it; CROSS's primary claim is never a stipulated composite score, so the composite-aggregate routing does not apply to it. The risk-bearers are the applicant whose work may be misclassified, the funder whose resources may be misdirected, and the served population, the people a funded intervention is meant to serve, named in Part II and carried through every gate rather than validated only at entry. The adversarial threat model is carried by the adverse-signal disclosure dimension, the Block J discrepancy machinery, and the round-level Cohort Position assessment (Parts II, IV, VII). Exclusion and out-of-scope declarations are carried by stage targeting and obligation-mode scoping (Part III).

**Condition 2, technical ontology.** Each indicator carries an operational definition with inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, unit of analysis, and edge-case determination (Part V), specified to WALKRI's criterion requirements. The ontology is applicant-specified within funder-set eligibility, which is the grant domain's form of a decision-derived ontology.

**Condition 3, valid instruments.** Indicator instruments are assessed against WALKRI's five data-quality standards (Part VIII). Contract-centric deliverables additionally require named execution and verification instruments: formal verification artifacts, independent audits, or on-chain invariant monitors (Part V). The independent-review and independent-evaluation evidence strengths (Part IV) supply the instrument independence the condition requires.

**Condition 4, pre-specified criteria.** The gate architecture is pre-specified throughout: the gate configuration, the full application question list, and the plain-language summary are published before the round opens (Part IV); the round specification pre-commitment makes that pre-specification independently verifiable; reviewer calibration and the WALKRI criterion-specification bar keep the criteria from collapsing into reviewer judgment. This is the most fully realized of CRAFT's conditions in CROSS, and arguably the most developed instantiation of pre-specified evaluation criteria in any available standard.

**Condition 5, explicit decision logic.** The gate determination logic is specified in the published configuration, and the completion gate requires a documented determination against published criteria, never grantee self-report alone (Part IV). The consequence level and causality stance (Part IV) calibrate evidentiary pressure to the harm potential of a wrong determination. CROSS realizes CRAFT's non-self-adjudication invariant through ex-ante designated attestation throughout, the invariant's Realization A: the independent-review evidence strength, the three-tier conflict-of-interest bar with its attestation-independence provision, the independent-signer requirement, and the Part XI independent redress body. CROSS uses no decentralized-challenge resolution; it does not select Realization B anywhere. Because CROSS's claims are criterion-type or causal-formative rather than resolver-determinate composites, Realization A is the fitting choice, and the consequence-calibrated independence bar is met by the conflict-of-interest tiers scaling with review intensity.

**Condition 6, feedback with propagation.** The continuation gate and the completion gate's unintended-outcomes disclosure surface signals that propagate to the next round's configuration and, for adverse signals, into the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle path (Part IV). Part XI's internal clarification, structured appeals, and optional independent redress are the funder-facing correction surface. The structured dataset and portfolio summary make round-level patterns legible, and version monitoring of this receipt is the specification-layer feedback path. This is the least realized of CRAFT's six conditions in CROSS: the feedback is single-loop with partial double-loop; loop depth is not named as a mechanism; direction-origin validation from the served population's origin is owed rather than installed; and the deployment-scale-context declaration CRAFT Condition 6 requires (effective Radius, minimum CSS conformance, conformance-gap handling) is not yet carried. These are declared here as the standing Condition 6 gaps.

### 2. Precision Toolkit scope

A CRAFT domain application does not inherit from CRAFT alone. Three standards function at once whenever CROSS is built, adopted, or audited. The Coordination Structural Integrity Suite standards are the active normative foundation: they name the foundational commitments from which CRAFT's conditions derive, and they are what gives CROSS grounds for knowing when a CRAFT requirement applies strictly and when a grant-domain application is appropriate. Frame Language sets the vocabulary of every CROSS specification document. CRAFT connects both to the six conditions CROSS must satisfy. Each declared inheritance below carries the five elements CRAFT Section 10 requires; the fifth, version coupling, is the shared procedure stated above.

**CRAFT (Chains Reveal Attested Falsifiable Truth) 0.4.1.** Inherited: all six conditions and the Section 9 construction grammar, instantiated for grants, with the non-self-adjudication invariant realized as Realization A. Foregrounding triggers: any authoring or revision of CROSS or its companions; the independent attestation of a round; any CRAFT revision. Background state: `background-satisfied` for five of the six conditions (verified 2026-06-02, re-assessed against CRAFT 0.4.1 on 2026-06-04, whose changes did not alter CROSS's inheritance); `background-unchecked` for Condition 6 on loop depth, direction-origin validation, and the deployment-scale-context declaration, the gaps named in component 1.

**Precision-First Design Standard 2.4.0.** Inherited: the ten corollaries in the grant domain, the double-negation precision floor (Part IV proportionality), and the consequence-calibrated evidence bar (Part IV consequence level and causality stance). Tier clause: CROSS applies the standard at the Operational tier for the applicant-and-funder transaction; the corollaries bind at gate-criterion specification and at indicator specification, and the inheritance is falsifiable in that a gate criterion or indicator failing WALKRI specification fails Corollary 1. Foregrounding triggers: any authoring or revision of a CROSS specification document. Background state: `background-satisfied` in the grant domain; `background-unchecked` at the reflexive layer, where CROSS does not yet apply the standard to its own specification practice with an obligation-loop closure.

**Information Asymmetry Classification Standard 0.1.25.** Inherited, by the Section 7 class inventory this clause supplies: Class 1 (positional) addressed through the organizational identity declaration and Cohort Position assessment; Class 3 (interpretive) through criterion intent and reviewer calibration; Class 4 (relational) through the conflict-of-interest framework; Class 5 (omission) through concurrent funding disclosure (Part VI), the scoped-strong inheritance; Class 6 (complexity) through the plain-language summary and published question list. Foregrounding triggers: round configuration and gate authoring. Background state: `background-satisfied` on Classes 1, 3, 4, 5, and 6; `background-unchecked` on Class 2 (temporal), where round timing relative to participant response capacity is unaddressed, the one class both applicable and entirely open.

**Structural Consent Legibility Standard 0.3.24.** Inherited: Coordination Consent, Output Consent, and the three core invariants at the Operational tier. Foregrounding triggers: round configuration; any change to the applicant data surface. Background state: `background-unchecked` on three floor gaps each adopting program currently resolves unaided: Data Consent, because the Attestation Corpus Query is funder-initiated collection of applicant data from external sources with no declared data-handling framework; Observation Consent, because the ongoing observation scope created by the structured dataset and portfolio records is unspecified and carries no scope-change re-consent; and participation scope-expansion re-consent, because the mid-grant amendment procedure is mentioned but not specified.

**Structural Power Obligation Standard 0.1.24.** Inherited: Distribute, Authorize, and the interpretive-capacity constraint for the funder-and-applicant structure. Foregrounding triggers: round configuration; any cross-round program design. Background state: `background-satisfied` for the named obligations; `background-unchecked` on two gaps, the absence of an authorized distribution profile for the program-level power structure and the absence of a change-classification architecture for funder-side power drift across rounds.

**Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle 0.7.12.** Inherited: the applicant-disclosure surface, applicants surfacing signals that contradict their self-presentation and reviewers not suppressing signals found in evaluation (Part II). Foregrounding triggers: entry and completion gate assessment; any adverse signal raised in a round. Background state: `background-satisfied` for the applicant-disclosure surface; `background-unchecked` for the absence of a structural channel, classifier, or timed path for adverse signals arising mid-execution or from third parties, so a conformant program can leave mid-execution signals from the served population or observers without a route. The beneficiary floor in Part II names this exposure as a consideration each gate is checked against; a standing channel through which the served population raises concerns during the grant period remains a separate funder configuration.

**Coordination Scaling Standard 0.1.0.** Inherited: CROSS's declared proportionality instrument (Parts II and IV). Foregrounding triggers: setting applicant scale and evidence tier in a round configuration. Background state: `background-unchecked` on Divergence 3, because CROSS names the standard as the calibration but does not require adopters to use its scale-assessment method to set thresholds, so thresholds can be set by funding amount alone and the calibration is notional rather than structural.

**Regenerative Obligation Standard 0.1.7.** Relationship: the direction-versus-destination pairing declared in Part I, with extraction visibility, proximity, and partial embeddedness scoped-strong and correctly outside the standard's internal-coordination core. Foregrounding triggers: funder obligation configuration (Part XI). Background state: `background-unchecked` for the absence of a Tier 1 funder stance declaration within Part XI's funder obligations.

**Four Batteries Capacity Standard 0.3.6.** Inherited: individual contribution legibility, the compressive vector. Foregrounding triggers: portfolio and gate-criterion design. Background state: `background-unchecked` because CROSS can be fully conformant while reproducing the Recognition Gap at the portfolio level, securing individual contribution legibility without requiring a program to audit whether its gate criteria structurally exclude the contribution types it claims to fund, and without a contraction or wind-down mechanism.

**Sensemaking Standard 1.1.22.** Status: mediated, carried by the coordination system that adopts CROSS rather than by CROSS itself. Foregrounding triggers: independent-review gate operation. Background state: `background-unchecked` for the absence of a reviewer or funder capacity-monitoring obligation at independent-review level, so hollow gate assessments from depleted panels are conformance-invisible.

**Conflict Transformation Standard 0.2.9.** Status: orthogonal; inheritance is not declared, and the relationship is carried as compatibility. The boundary gap, named not as an inheritance obligation but as a known edge: Part XI redress terminates in procedural adjudication, with no facilitated mediation step between the internal clarification of Part XI Section 2 and the structured appeals of Part XI Section 3.

**Frame Language vocabulary discipline (v0.1.0).** Inherited: every CROSS specification term passes Frame Language admissibility and watchlist checks, and CROSS does not exempt itself from the discipline it relies on. Foregrounding triggers: any authoring or revision of any CROSS specification document at any level. Background state: `background-satisfied` for this Part on authoring; the obligation re-fires at every revision.

### 3. Axis-level positioning

CROSS implements per-axis quality assessment through WALKRI: the five data-quality standards in Part VIII (Validity, Integrity, Precision, Reliability, Timeliness) each serve CRAFT Condition 3 within the indicator specification. CROSS uses WALKRI; it does not inherit from it. WALKRI is domain-agnostic and travels across CRAFT domain applications; in the grant case CROSS frames the chain and supplies the decision logic (Condition 5) and feedback (Condition 6), and WALKRI ensures the field instruments are sound. This is the relationship the interaction architecture specifies, and it is why this receipt declares WALKRI with an axis-level profile as a standard CROSS uses rather than as a parent CROSS inherits from.

### 4. The inheritance-specification discipline

The single most repeated finding across the eleven passes was structural rather than substantive: CROSS declared its upstream standards without producing the falsifiable inheritance clause each requires, which is Precision-First failure pattern 7.5, inheritance claimed without inheritance specified. This receipt is the direct repair. The tier clause and class inventory above make each inheritance claim falsifiable, and the receipt is itself tier-parameterized so that it is not another instance of pattern 7.5. Where a gap is declared `background-unchecked`, it is an open obligation an adopting program or a future revision may close; the declaration is what makes it legible and actionable rather than a hidden omission.
