# UN Trust Fund to End Violence Against Women (UNTF)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

UN Women's UN Trust Fund to End Violence Against Women (UNTF) operates a tiered mandatory evaluation model in which grantees receiving more than USD 150,000 manage and deliver a mandatory final external evaluation, while grantees receiving less than USD 150,000 undergo a co-managed evaluation with the UNTF Secretariat. All grantees report on key performance indicators every six months, receive ten-module online evaluation training with one-on-one support, and have their evaluations published through the UNTF Learning Hub. CROSS's four-gate sequence is structurally compatible with the UNTF framework: the threshold-based tiered attestation model corresponds to CROSS's independent attestation pathway scaled by grant scope, the six-month KPI cycle corresponds to the progress verification gate, and the mandatory publication of all evaluations through the Learning Hub corresponds to CROSS's attestation corpus. WALKRI's pre-publication requirements apply to UNTF intake instruments to ensure that KPI fields specify what evidence qualifies as a documented change in violence against women or in the enabling environment before any grantee begins baseline data collection.

## The UNTF Evaluation Framework

The UN Trust Fund to End Violence Against Women, administered by UN Women, is the primary UN-wide grant mechanism for programs addressing gender-based violence. The UNTF funds civil society organizations, government entities, and UN agencies working to prevent and respond to violence against women and girls. Its grant framework includes formally stated mandatory evaluation requirements that constitute one of the most structured evaluation architectures in the UN grant ecosystem.

The UNTF's threshold-based tiered model distinguishes two evaluation pathways by grant size. For grants over USD 150,000, grantees are required to manage and deliver a mandatory final external evaluation, meaning the evaluation must be conducted by an evaluator independent of the grantee and the findings reported to the UNTF Secretariat as a condition of grant completion. For grants under USD 150,000, the UNTF Secretariat co-manages the evaluation with the grantee, providing closer technical support for smaller organizations with less internal evaluation capacity. Both pathways produce a mandatory final evaluation; the threshold determines the independence configuration and the level of Secretariat involvement.

All grantees, regardless of grant size, report on KPIs every six months through structured progress reports submitted to the UNTF Secretariat. All grantees participate in a ten-module online evaluation training program and receive one-on-one evaluation support. The UNTF publishes all completed evaluations through its Learning Hub, creating a publicly accessible, searchable repository of evaluation findings across the global UNTF portfolio. This mandatory publication requirement is stated policy, not discretionary practice.

## How CROSS Satisfies the UNTF Evaluation Framework

The UNTF's tiered mandatory evaluation model is a formally published version of CROSS's independent attestation pathway scaled by grant scope. CROSS's design principle is that the independence of attestation should scale with the scope and financial magnitude of the grant; the UNTF's USD 150,000 threshold operationalizes exactly this principle, with the threshold determining whether independent attestation is grantee-managed (larger grants, stronger independence requirement) or co-managed with the funder (smaller grants, supported independence).

**Entry specification gate and KPI pre-specification:** UNTF grantees are required to specify their KPIs in grant agreements before program implementation begins, and the six-month reporting cycle measures progress against those pre-specified KPIs. This satisfies CROSS's entry specification gate: the indicators are established before any program activity begins, and all subsequent reporting is measured against those pre-commitment specifications. CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification provides the instrument through which UNTF grantees can satisfy this requirement with full precision: each KPI is named, defined, unitized, baselined, targeted, timed, sourced, and given a causality stance before the first reporting period opens.

**Progress verification gate and six-month cycle:** The mandatory six-month KPI reporting cycle corresponds to CROSS's progress verification gate. CROSS requires that programs document progress against pre-specified indicators at defined intervals, not merely at completion, and that this mid-stream evidence be maintained in the attestation corpus. The UNTF's six-month cycle creates two progress verification checkpoints within a standard 12-month grant period and four within a 24-month grant period, providing a structured record of trajectory alongside the final evaluation finding.

**Attestation corpus and Learning Hub:** The mandatory publication of all evaluations through the UNTF Learning Hub constitutes CROSS's attestation corpus requirement. CROSS's attestation gate requires that outcome claims be documented in a form accessible to independent verification; the Learning Hub provides this at portfolio scale. A grantee's final evaluation, once published, constitutes a publicly verifiable attestation against the pre-specified KPIs, available to the UNTF Secretariat, to other funders conducting due diligence, and to researchers studying the global evidence base on violence against women programs.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements apply to UNTF intake instruments to ensure that KPI fields specify what evidence qualifies as a documented change in violence against women or in the enabling environment before any grantee begins baseline data collection. The UNTF's mandatory evaluation architecture is structurally sound, but the pre-publication specification of KPI intake fields is the layer at which data quality for gender-based violence programs is most often lost. A KPI field that asks grantees to report "number of women who report reduced experience of violence" requires, before any grantee deploys a survey, a precise operational definition of what counts as a reported experience of violence in this program context, what survey instrument or interview protocol qualifies as the evidence form, and what response threshold distinguishes a meaningful change from a measurement artifact.

WALKRI's criterion intent requirement specifies what the UNTF intends each KPI to capture: not merely that women reported a different experience, but that the change is attributable to the program's theory of change and documented through a specified instrument at a specified time relative to program contact. WALKRI's operational definition requirement specifies the precise scope of the KPI: whether "reduced experience of violence" includes psychological violence alongside physical violence, whether it covers intimate partner violence only or extends to community-level violence, and how the measurement protocol handles respondents who experienced multiple violence types. WALKRI's compliance threshold requirement specifies the minimum response or change that satisfies the KPI at each six-month reporting gate, preventing inflated claims based on marginal changes that do not constitute program impact. Applied before any grantee begins baseline data collection, these requirements produce KPI instruments that generate UNTF evaluation data capable of supporting the portfolio-level learning that the Learning Hub is designed to enable.
