# Arbitrum DAO Grants - CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement

## Summary

Arbitrum DAO Grants is a multi-program funding ecosystem operated by the Arbitrum Foundation under ArbitrumDAO (ARB token holder) governance. Unlike RetroPGF (Optimism's single retroactive mechanism) and Gitcoin Grants (a Quadratic Funding mechanism with multiple round formats), Arbitrum operates a portfolio of concurrent programs each with distinct decision-making, evaluation criteria, and allocation methods: the Arbitrum Foundation Grant Program, the Domain Allocator Offerings (D.A.O., formerly Questbook), Arbitrum Gaming Ventures (AGV), ArbiFuel, the Arbitrum Audit Program (AAP), Stylus Sprint, Firestarters, the DeFi Renaissance Incentive Program (DRIP), Rewarding Active Delegates (RAD), and legacy incentive programs (STIP, LTIPP, STIP Bridge).

CROSS+WALKRI compatibility is strongest at the pre-commitment layer (governance proposals are pre-published on Discourse and approved via on-chain Tally votes before disbursement), at the Determination Body Separation layer (community-elected domain allocators specialize in verticals like gaming, dev tooling, education; ARB token holders are structurally separate from applicants), at the Independent Verifiability layer (on-chain governance records, public KPI dashboards, milestone-verified disbursements), and at the Adverse Signal Engagement layer (the ARDC has published an extensive self-assessment surfacing weaknesses across past programs). Compatibility gaps appear at the cross-program coherence layer (ARDC explicitly identifies fragmentation as a structural issue), at the post-program retention measurement layer (ARDC finding: metrics revert to pre-incentive levels), and at the population-anchored configuration layer (downstream beneficiaries do not formally participate).

This statement covers the 2024-2026 era of Arbitrum DAO Grants, including the ARDC assessment work that itself functions as a structurally-aligned Inter-cycle Reflection Stage retroactively applied to legacy incentive programs.

## The Arbitrum DAO Grants Framework

### Multi-Program Architecture

The Arbitrum Foundation operates a portfolio of grant and incentive programs simultaneously. Active programs as of 2025-2026 include:

- **Arbitrum Foundation Grant Program (AGP):** the Foundation's primary grant track for ecosystem builders
- **Domain Allocator Offerings (D.A.O.):** community-elected domain allocators specializing in verticals; three seasons documented, growing from $250,000 across four domains in Season 1 to $1,000,000 per domain in Season 2 to five domains in Season 3
- **Arbitrum Gaming Ventures (AGV):** an ArbitrumDAO initiative for gaming projects
- **ArbiFuel:** gas fee sponsorship for early-stage teams
- **Arbitrum Audit Program (AAP):** $10M in ARB over 12 months for third-party smart contract audits
- **Stylus Sprint Program**
- **Firestarters**
- **DeFi Renaissance Incentive Program (DRIP)**
- **Rewarding Active Delegates (RAD):** delegate participation rewards
- **DAO Event Budget**
- Legacy incentive programs: STIP, LTIPP, STIP Bridge

### Decision-Making Architecture

Major capital commitments operate under a two-layer decision-making process. The Smart Liquidity Research case study describes the flow: "Grants typically go through forums like Arbitrum DAO's Discourse, followed by on-chain votes via Tally." This places the decision-making discipline at the protocol layer: forum discussion produces a proposal text, the proposal is voted on by ARB token holders on Tally, and on-chain execution proceeds only after DAO approval.

Community-elected domain allocators are a distinct layer: instead of the full DAO voting on each individual grant within a vertical, allocators with specialization in that vertical (gaming, developer tooling, education) make grant determinations within budget bounds approved by the full DAO. Grantees from these tracks are required to publish dashboards, metrics, and distribution data.

### Application Requirements

The Smart Liquidity Research case study summarizes the elements successful applications include: "technical details, requested budget, timeline, KPIs, team background, and Arbitrum-specific impact." Successful applications also "tied grant milestones to specific KPIs (TVL, DAUs, GitHub commits)" and demonstrated "frequent updates, open-source development, and community engagement."

Winning focus areas include "building public goods, onboarding users, or strengthening Arbitrum-native primitives." Notable grant examples include Rage Trade (~$1.5M ARB), Treasure DAO (~$2M ARB), and PlutusDAO (~$400K ARB).

### ARDC Assessment Framework

The Arbitrum Research and Development Collective (ARDC) has produced a published assessment of past Arbitrum incentive programs (STIP, LTIPP, STIP Bridge) and proposed ten recommendations for future programs. The assessment uses a four-component framework: "Goals and Expected Output, Program Design, Actual Outputs, and Changes and Learnings."

For Goals, the assessment evaluates "the core strategy of the program (project-bootstrap X product, objective-target specific assets or users, or metric-centric)" and alignment with ecosystem sustainability.

Key findings the ARDC published:

- "Lack of retention is a common trend among all of Arbitrum's incentive programs to date" (the ARDC notes that metrics typically reverted to pre-incentive levels post-program)
- "The DAO lacked a persistent, full-cycle operating unit responsible for designing, executing, and iterating incentive programs" across cycles
- "Funding was largely generalized and reactive, which meant the most established verticals within Arbitrum captured the majority of incentives"

The ten recommendations: define program purpose upfront and hold to it; build a dedicated incentives operating unit; encourage modularity and program-level cohesion; replace one-shot incentive packages with progressive allocations; formalize reporting standards through centralized data infrastructure; design for post-program retention; make governance more agile by delegating operational control; calibrate program timelines to capital deployment; limit program fragmentation and sharpen objective focus; align incentive programs to DAO-wide goals.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Obligation Architecture Arbitrum DAO Grants Operates Under

### Mixed Obligation Mode

Arbitrum DAO Grants operates across CROSS's three obligation modes simultaneously, depending on the program track. The Foundation Grant Program and the D.A.O. tracks operate primarily in Build mode (the obligation object is the produced output: code, infrastructure, public goods) and Change mode (for tracks that target population-level KPIs like user onboarding). The legacy incentive programs (STIP, LTIPP, STIP Bridge) operated structurally in a continuation-adjacent mode (the obligation object was sustained activity over a defined period).

A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants would declare the obligation mode at each program track's configuration. The current architecture is implicit; declaration would be additive without breaking existing structure.

### Pre-commitment as Structural Posture (Falsifiability Operationalization)

The Discourse-to-Tally flow is structurally aligned with pre-commitment. Proposals must be published on the forum before the vote, and the on-chain vote produces an immutable record of what was approved. Once approved, program parameters cannot be modified mid-cycle without a new on-chain vote. This is pre-commitment as structural posture: criteria are fixed before the program runs, evidence is assessed against the fixed criteria after.

This is structurally stronger than RetroPGF's Season 7 architecture (where algorithms are selected mid-round by badge holder vote) and matches Gitcoin Grants' pre-commitment discipline at the round-configuration layer.

### Determination Body Separation (Layer 4 Primitive)

The Determination Body Separation primitive is satisfied in two distinct ways across the Arbitrum architecture:

**At the macro DAO decision-making layer:** ARB token holders voting on Tally are structurally separate from applicants. The DAO voting process produces determinations that no single applicant can override. The Tally protocol's voting records are publicly queryable, making the determination body's actions independently verifiable.

**At the domain allocator layer:** community-elected domain allocators specialize in verticals (gaming, dev tooling, education) and are structurally separate from applicants within their domain. The community election process creates obligation directions from the allocator to the electing community, with re-election as the named response to non-conformance.

A gap persists at the Foundation operations layer: where the Arbitrum Foundation acts directly (e.g., AGV evaluations, audit program reviews), the body composition and removal mechanisms are not always specified in publicly accessible charter documents at the Determination Body Separation primitive's specificity.

### Attestation Corpus Alignment

The CROSS+WALKRI Attestation Corpus primitive (Layer 4) is satisfied at depth by the Arbitrum architecture. Multiple queryable sources operate without applicant participation: Tally on-chain governance records (every proposal, every vote, every execution), on-chain transaction records (every disbursement, every contract interaction), public KPI dashboards (TVL, DAUs, GitHub commits as required by grantee reporting), the Questbook application platform records, and ARDC research reports. These constitute a de facto Attestation Corpus broader and deeper than most CROSS+WALKRI-mapped frameworks.

A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants would formalize the Attestation Corpus specification at each program track's configuration, naming which sources apply to which evaluation layer.

### Format Agnosticism (Commitment 5)

Arbitrum DAO Grants satisfies Format Agnosticism partially. The program does not prescribe a single output format; grantees can deliver code, dashboards, documentation, contracts, or other artifacts. However, KPI reporting requirements tend toward specific formats (the dashboards, metrics, and distribution data grantees publish). The ARDC's recommendation 5 ("formalize reporting standards through centralized data infrastructure") suggests the Foundation itself sees variance in reporting format as a structural issue.

A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants would explicitly declare which content requirements bind across all programs and which format requirements are themselves primitive (e.g., on-chain attestation format for governance records). Other formats would be neutral to conformance per the commitment.

### Independent Verifiability (Commitment 6)

The Arbitrum architecture's compatibility with Independent Verifiability is layered:

**Strong at the on-chain voting layer.** Tally records are on-chain, immutable, and queryable by any party. DAO determinations are independently re-derivable from public chain data.

**Strong at the disbursement layer.** Milestone-tied payments are recorded on-chain. Verification of milestone completion is structurally separable from applicant self-report when on-chain or KPI dashboard evidence exists.

**Partial at the KPI reporting layer.** Grantees publish dashboards, but the dashboards are often grantee-operated (the metrics are computed from data the grantee provides). The independent-source layer comes from third-party data (Etherscan, Dune Analytics, DefiLlama) that mirrors the same underlying chain data; full Independent Verifiability requires that the metric computation methodology be auditable independently.

**Weak at some review-committee layers.** Where the Foundation or a committee makes a discretionary determination (AGV, certain Foundation grants), the underlying rubric and the named verification source are not always publicly detailed at CROSS Part VIII specificity.

## How WALKRI Complements Arbitrum DAO Grants

### Evidence Form Specifications

WALKRI's evidence form requirement specifies that every measurement instrument include a documented data source and an independently accessible access path. Arbitrum's KPI requirements (TVL, DAUs, GitHub commits) implicitly specify the data sources but rarely formalize the access path at WALKRI's full specification standard (criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, conformance threshold).

A WALKRI-conformant Arbitrum program would specify, for each KPI: what the metric is designed to measure in the ecosystem theory of change, exactly what counts and what does not (operational definition), how the metric is calculated (response form), where the underlying data is sourced and how to access it (evidence form), and the minimum threshold that triggers tranche disbursement (conformance threshold).

### Adverse Signal Engagement Principle (ARDC as Model)

The ARDC assessment is itself a model of Adverse Signal Engagement Principle conformance at the program-portfolio level. The Foundation has publicly surfaced and acknowledged structural weaknesses across past programs: lack of retention, missing operating unit, fragmentation favoring established verticals. These are exactly the adverse signals that the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle requires be surfaced rather than suppressed.

The ARDC's recommendations are also structurally aligned: each names a specific structural gap and proposes a specific structural fix. This is the discipline the principle requires, applied at the program-portfolio scale rather than at the individual-grant scale.

A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants would integrate ARDC-style assessment as a recurring discipline (per ARDC recommendation 2, build a dedicated incentives operating unit) rather than as a one-time research output. The ARDC findings would feed Inter-cycle Reflection Stage publications per program track.

### Inter-cycle Reflection Stage (Layer 4 Primitive)

The ARDC assessment functions as an Inter-cycle Reflection Stage retroactively applied to legacy incentive programs (STIP, LTIPP, STIP Bridge). The Primitives Foundation specifies the Inter-cycle Reflection Stage as a reflection on round outcomes published before the next cycle opens; the ARDC's published assessment was produced after the legacy programs concluded but before subsequent incentive programs launched, structurally satisfying the timing requirement.

A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants would make Inter-cycle Reflection Stage publications a standing pre-committed requirement at each program track's configuration, with publication deadlines tied to subsequent cycle opening.

### Portfolio-level Continuation Benchmark (Layer 7 Primitive)

The ARDC's finding that "lack of retention is a common trend among all of Arbitrum's incentive programs to date" identifies the structural absence of a Portfolio-level Continuation Benchmark. The primitive specifies a funder-declared threshold that the program as a whole must meet to justify continued operation. Without such a benchmark, individual programs can pass milestone-tied disbursements while the portfolio fails to produce sustained ecosystem outcomes.

The ARDC's recommendation 6 ("design for post-program retention") is structurally the request for a Portfolio-level Continuation Benchmark. A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants would declare, at the program level (not the individual grant level), the retention metric, the threshold, and the review period that justifies continued program operation.

### Beneficiary Validation Mechanism (Layer 3 Primitive)

Arbitrum DAO Grants partially satisfies the Beneficiary Validation Mechanism primitive through community-elected domain allocators. The allocators are drawn from or bound by obligation to the relevant ecosystem vertical (gaming, dev tooling, education), and their election by the community is a form of validation of the population's characterization.

However, the formal Beneficiary Validation Mechanism is stronger than allocator election. The primitive specifies that named parties from the affected population assess the applying entity's characterization of the population, the need, and the FROM state. Allocator election validates that the allocator represents the vertical, not that each individual grant's claims about the vertical are validated. The gap is at the per-grant layer.

A CROSS-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants for population-anchored tracks would add a per-grant beneficiary validation step beyond allocator election.

## Compatibility Findings

### Strong alignments

- Pre-commitment as structural posture (Discourse-to-Tally governance flow creates immutable pre-publication)
- Determination Body Separation at macro DAO decision-making and domain allocator layers
- Attestation Corpus structurally present across Tally, on-chain records, KPI dashboards, Questbook, ARDC reports
- Independent Verifiability at decision-making and disbursement layers
- Adverse Signal Engagement at the program-portfolio level (ARDC assessment as model)
- Inter-cycle Reflection Stage structurally satisfied retroactively by ARDC work
- Modularity Preservation: multi-program portfolio with independently adoptable program tracks

### Partial alignments

- Format Agnosticism at the output layer but variance in reporting format (ARDC recommendation 5 acknowledges)
- Independent Verifiability at KPI dashboard layer (grantee-operated dashboards with third-party-mirrored data)
- Beneficiary Validation Mechanism through domain allocator election but not per-grant validation

### Compatibility gaps requiring program adjustment for full conformance

- No declared Portfolio-level Continuation Benchmark across the program portfolio (ARDC recommendation 6 names this gap structurally)
- Inter-cycle Reflection Stage not standing or pre-committed (ARDC assessment is retroactive ad hoc, not a continuing discipline)
- Determination Body Separation at Foundation operations layer lacks publicly accessible charters for some bodies
- Cross-program coherence (ARDC recommendation 3 on modularity and program-level cohesion identifies this gap)
- Affected Population Verification Gate not implemented at completion across most program tracks

### Structural compatibilities of historical interest

The Arbitrum DAO Grants architecture is unique in the CROSS+WALKRI-mapped corpus for its multi-program scope and its published self-assessment work. RetroPGF, Gitcoin Grants, and most other web3 grants programs operate a single allocation mechanism; Arbitrum operates a portfolio of mechanisms simultaneously. The ARDC assessment is the closest analog in the corpus to a structurally pre-committed Adverse Signal Engagement Principle discipline at the program-portfolio level.

The Octant comparison is instructive: Octant currently operates a single allocation mechanism per epoch but is structurally adjacent to Arbitrum in DAO decision-making lineage. A CROSS+WALKRI adoption at Octant could draw on Arbitrum's portfolio architecture pattern without inheriting the fragmentation issue the ARDC identified.

## Calibration Tier Assessment

Arbitrum DAO Grants 2024-2026 sits at a mixed calibration tier in the CROSS+WALKRI Lenses Framework:

- **Tier 3 (Outcome Specified, Self-Reported)** at the KPI dashboard layer for the grantee tracks with milestone-tied disbursement
- **Tier 2 (Process Conformant)** at the decision-making layer (Discourse-to-Tally flow is publicly documented and conforms to its own published process)
- **Tier 1 (Impressionistic)** at some Foundation review-committee layers (AGV, certain Foundation grants) where rubrics are not publicly detailed

Aggregating, the program operates at Tier 2-3 (Process Conformant to Outcome Specified mixed). The ARDC's recommendations point toward Tier 4 trajectory through formalized reporting standards, dedicated operating unit, post-program retention measurement, and centralized data infrastructure.

**Current+target trajectory:**

- Current: Tier 2-3 aggregate as described above
- Next achievable target (within current architecture, post-ARDC implementation): Tier 3 across all program tracks via formalized reporting standards (ARDC recommendation 5) and dedicated incentives operating unit (ARDC recommendation 2)
- Long-range target (within 2-3 years if ARDC recommendations fully adopted): Tier 4 (Independently Verified) via centralized data infrastructure, post-program retention measurement, and standing Inter-cycle Reflection Stage publications per program track

## Pre-Funding Specification Layer

A CROSS+WALKRI-conformant Arbitrum DAO Grants round produces something the current architecture approximates at the decision-making layer but does not formalize at the program-portfolio layer: a pre-funding specification layer that publishes (per program track) the Determination Body charter, the KPI evidence form specifications, the milestone verification methodology, the Adverse Signal Engagement plan, the Inter-cycle Reflection Stage commitment, and the Portfolio-level Continuation Benchmark. The ARDC recommendations effectively prescribe this specification layer; full conformance requires the recommendations to move from research output to standing discipline.

## Compatibility Statement Maturity

This is a Tier 2 compatibility statement under the CROSS+WALKRI primitives framework index: the source framework is a structurally novel exemplar of multi-program DAO grants portfolio architecture with published self-assessment, and it warrants a full statement.

The statement will be revised as ARDC recommendations are implemented, as new program tracks launch, and as the post-ARDC era of Arbitrum DAO Grants matures.
