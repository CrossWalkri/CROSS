# LBG / B4SI Corporate Community Investment Standard

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The London Benchmarking Group (LBG) model, now managed under B4SI (Business for Societal Impact; b4si.net), is the primary international standard for measuring corporate community investment, used by over 300 companies in more than 40 countries. The LBG Guidance Manual (2018) specifies a three-level measurement chain: inputs (what is invested), outputs (what is directly produced), and outcomes (what changes for the community). CROSS+WALKRI's obligation architecture maps directly and precisely onto the LBG chain, providing the structured data production mechanism that corporate grantmaking programs need to generate LBG-conformant measurement records.

---

## The LBG / B4SI Standard's Approach

The LBG model emerged from a practical challenge that corporate grantmakers faced in the 1990s: how to measure and communicate the value of community investment in a way that was credible to external audiences, comparable across companies, and structured enough to support internal decision-making. The model's three-level chain was designed to distinguish between what a company actually invested (inputs), what that investment directly produced (outputs), and what broader changes resulted in the community (outcomes). This distinction mattered because corporate community investment was frequently reported using only input figures, which conveys cost but not value.

Inputs in the LBG model include cash contributions, employee time valued at market rates, and in-kind contributions such as product donations or facility use. The model specifies that management costs should also be documented, since the cost of administering a community investment program is part of the total cost of the investment. This level of specificity makes LBG more demanding than most corporate social responsibility reporting frameworks, which typically require only cash figures.

Outputs in the LBG model are what the investment directly produces: the deliverables created, the services delivered, the events held. Outputs are within the direct control of the investing company and its grantees. They do not depend on community behavior or external conditions; they are the necessary product of executing the program as designed.

Outcomes in the LBG model are changes in the community that result from the outputs. Outcomes are not directly within the company's control: they depend on how community members engage with outputs, what other factors are present in the community, and how conditions evolve over time. The LBG model does not require companies to claim sole causation for outcomes; it requires them to document the outcomes they contributed to and to specify the causal pathway connecting inputs through outputs to outcomes.

B4SI extended the LBG model to include ESG alignment documentation and social value quantification, reflecting corporate sustainability reporting requirements that have become prominent since the LBG model was originally designed. The B4SI framework asks companies to connect their community investment measurement to ESG frameworks such as GRI and SASB, and to quantify the social value of outcomes where feasible using Social Return on Investment or comparable methods.

## How CROSS Satisfies the LBG / B4SI Standard

CROSS's obligation architecture maps precisely onto the LBG three-level chain, providing a structural implementation of LBG measurement that generates all three levels of data as a natural output of program operation rather than as a retrospective reporting exercise.

LBG inputs correspond to CROSS's concurrent funding declarations. CROSS requires programs to document all sources of concurrent investment, including non-cash contributions valued at market rates. This produces the complete input picture the LBG model requires, including in-kind and time contributions that are frequently undercounted in corporate community investment reporting.

LBG outputs correspond to CROSS's completion gate deliverable specification. CROSS's Build obligation mode specifies at intake what deliverable the program will directly produce: the artifact, service, or capability that exists at program completion as a direct result of the investment. The completion gate requires that the specified deliverable be demonstrated before the gate is passed. This creates the output documentation the LBG model requires: a record that the investment produced the directly attributable output it was supposed to produce.

LBG outcomes correspond to CROSS's Change obligation mode and its associated indicator specifications. The CROSS eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1 through 11) provides the measurement structure for documenting what community conditions changed, for which population, over what time period, against what baseline, and with what causal modifier. Fields 1 through 5 cover the indicator's name, definition, unit of measurement, baseline value, and target value. Fields 6 through 9 address the population specification and causal stance. Fields 10 and 11 document the evidence source and evidence strength.

The LBG distinction between outputs (directly produced by the investment) and outcomes (broader community changes) corresponds precisely to the CROSS distinction between Build obligation (producing a specified deliverable) and Change obligation (achieving a measured condition shift). This is not an approximate correspondence: the two frameworks draw the same conceptual boundary between what is directly within program control and what depends on community response and external conditions.

LBG management costs documentation maps to CROSS's organizational identity efficiency declarations. B4SI's ESG alignment extension maps to CROSS's coherence disclosure, which documents how the program relates to other interventions in the same problem area, including other ESG-aligned activities the company is undertaking.

| LBG / B4SI Component | CROSS Provision |
|---|---|
| Inputs (cash, time, in-kind) | Concurrent funding disclosure (including non-cash) |
| Outputs (directly produced) | Build obligation completion gate |
| Outcomes (community changes) | Change obligation indicator specification, Fields 1-11 |
| Management costs | Organizational identity efficiency declarations |
| ESG alignment | Coherence disclosure |
| Causal pathway documentation | Contribution-vs-attribution causal stance field |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI ensures that the intake fields corporate grantmaking programs use to collect LBG measurement data are specified as measurement instruments before any grantee or community partner engages with them. For LBG compliance, this matters because the standard requires that outputs and outcomes be measured consistently across programs and across years. Inconsistent operational definitions, which produce incomparable data, are the most common failure mode in corporate community investment reporting.

WALKRI's pre-publication audit specifies what each field collects, what measurement purpose it serves, and what evidence the respondent must provide. For an LBG-aligned corporate grantmaking program, this means that intake fields collecting output data, outcome data, and population specification data each have operational definitions that make the resulting dataset comparable across program cycles. WALKRI also flags fields that conflate outputs and outcomes, which is a significant risk in corporate community investment reporting: programs frequently state community change aspirations as if they were directly produced deliverables, which inflates the output measurement and obscures the causal pathway the LBG model requires.
