# IFAD Results Measurement Architecture (RMF, RIMS, IPAF)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

This statement covers the International Fund for Agricultural Development's full measurement architecture in a single compatibility document: the Results Management Framework (RMF) at the replenishment and corporate level, the Results and Impact Management System (RIMS) and its current operationalization through the Core Outcome Indicators (COI) at the project level, the Independent Office of Evaluation (IOE) as the independent assessment layer, and the Indigenous Peoples Assistance Facility (IPAF) as the specific application that extends the architecture with Indigenous well-being indicators developed through the Indigenous Peoples' Forum at IFAD. A CROSS-conformant program satisfies the indicator documentation requirements of RIMS and the RMF as a structural consequence of conformance. The RMF's three-tier structure maps to CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy; the COI's mandatory project-level survey cadence maps to CROSS's gate architecture; the IOE's independent evaluation function maps to CROSS's gate function distinction between developmental and summative gates. IPAF is treated as a configured instance of this architecture rather than a separate framework: it inherits the RMF and RIMS infrastructure and adds an Indigenous well-being indicator layer that activates dimension-specific obligations CROSS already accommodates through optional dimensions and through the population scope and disaggregation provisions. This statement is the first dedicated Indigenous-extension Part XII entry in CROSS+WALKRI's coverage; it does not displace the broader Indigenous and First Nations grantmaking gap, which is being addressed separately for Te Puni Kokiri and remains structurally distinct.

---

## What the IFAD Measurement Architecture Is

IFAD is a specialized agency of the United Nations and an international financial institution funding agricultural development in rural areas of low- and middle-income countries. Its measurement architecture has been built up over two decades and operates at three nested layers: corporate, project, and evaluation.

The corporate layer is the Results Management Framework (RMF), revised at every three-year replenishment cycle and published as a board-adopted document. The IFAD13 RMF covers 2025-2027. The framework organizes indicators across three tiers. Tier I tracks long-term contribution to the Sustainable Development Goals and global outcomes IFAD does not control but contributes to. Tier II tracks country-level development outcomes and impact delivered by IFAD-supported projects. Tier III tracks operational and organizational effectiveness, the layer over which IFAD has direct control and on which it is most directly accountable. Earlier replenishment cycles used a five-tier structure; the move to three tiers (introduced in IFAD11 and refined in IFAD12 and IFAD13) was a deliberate consolidation, and the IFAD13 RMF moves further by tracking results rather than fixed targets at output and outcome levels and by publishing detailed indicator definitions on a dedicated webpage rather than embedding them in replenishment documents.

The project layer is the Results and Impact Management System (RIMS), originally established in 2004 as a corporate menu of indicators for measuring and reporting on IFAD-supported country programmes at activity, output, and impact level. RIMS is now operationalized through the Core Outcome Indicators Measurement Guidelines (COIM, October 2021), which specify 1 outreach indicator, 20 output indicators, and 22 outcome indicators mapped to IFAD's Strategic Framework 2016-2025 and aligned with the SDGs. Core Indicators are mandatory whenever relevant to the project Theory of Change and can be complemented by project-specific indicators. Results for outcome-level Core Indicators are captured through three surveys carried out at project baseline, midterm, and completion, with a mandatory methodology specified in the COIM Guidelines.

The evaluation layer is the Independent Office of Evaluation (IOE), governed by IFAD's Evaluation Policy and operating under the Evaluation Manual, now in its third edition (2022). The 2022 edition is the first to apply to both self-evaluation and independent evaluation, formalizing the boundary between project teams' own results reporting under the RMF and RIMS and the IOE's independent assessment of those results.

The Indigenous Peoples Assistance Facility (IPAF), administered by IFAD since 2007, is a small-grants facility funding projects designed and implemented by Indigenous Peoples' communities and their organizations. Grants range from US$20,000 to US$50,000 and run up to two years. IPAF is co-managed and coordinated by three regional Indigenous organizations: Foro Internacional de Mujeres Indígenas (FIMI) for Latin America and the Caribbean, Samburu Women Trust for Africa, and Tebtebba Foundation for Asia and the Pacific. Performance assessments are published cycle by cycle, with the fourth and fifth cycle assessments publicly available at ifad.org. IPAF grantees report under the IFAD RMF and RIMS Tier II indicators, complemented by Indigenous well-being indicators developed through the Indigenous Peoples' Forum at IFAD and informed by the monitoring and evaluation systems built by FIMI, Samburu Women Trust, and Tebtebba. The well-being indicators address areas the corporate RMF does not surface on its own face: identity and cultural continuity, land and resource rights, Indigenous knowledge systems, gender (with specific attention to Indigenous women), and youth participation in community decision-making.

## How CROSS Satisfies the IFAD RMF and RIMS

CROSS provisions address the RMF's three-tier structure, the RIMS Core Outcome Indicators specification requirements, and the project survey cadence at the gate architecture level.

| IFAD RMF / RIMS Element | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| RMF Tier I: SDG and global outcome contribution | Goal tier in the Theory of Change architecture (long-term conditions to which the program contributes but does not control) |
| RMF Tier II: country-level development outcomes and impact | Outcome tier in the Theory of Change architecture; CROSS Field 11 (causality stance) accommodates the contribution claim Tier II indicators express |
| RMF Tier III: operational and organizational effectiveness | Output tier in the Theory of Change architecture; funder-side reporting accommodated through the funder obligation provisions in Part XI |
| Replenishment-cycle target setting (3-year horizon) | Continuation gate configured at the program level; gate configurations are pre-published and may not be retrospectively tightened |
| IFAD13 move from targets to tracked results at output and outcome levels | CROSS obligation modes (build, change, retroactive) accommodate both target-bound and tracked-result reporting; the funder declares which applies at round configuration |
| RIMS Core Outcome Indicators: 1 outreach + 20 output + 22 outcome (mandatory when relevant to ToC) | CROSS Field 10 (institutional framework alignment) records the Core Indicator reference code; CROSS allows project-specific indicators alongside core indicators as Field 11 declares custom-versus-core stance |
| Core Indicator mandatory methodology (COIM Guidelines) | CROSS Field 9 (collection method) records the COIM methodology reference; WALKRI Section 3 specifies the field instrument that implements the methodology |
| Three-survey cadence: baseline, midterm, completion | Field 4 (baseline) + progress verification gates (midterm) + completion verification gate (completion) |
| Indicator definitions published on dedicated webpage (IFAD13 reform) | CROSS pre-round publication requirement: full application question list and plain-language summary must be publicly accessible before any applicant submits |
| IOE Independent Office of Evaluation, Evaluation Manual (2022) | Completion verification gate cannot be informal (Part IV); the IOE's independent function corresponds to the named, published mechanism for determining whether completion criteria are met |
| IOE 2022 boundary between self- and independent evaluation | Gate function distinction (developmental vs. summative): progress gates formative, completion gates summative; the IOE's role is the independent layer above the funder's self-assessment |
| Strategic Framework 2016-2025 alignment | Coherence disclosure requirement: the program declares how it relates to other interventions and to higher-order frameworks operating in the same domain |

The RMF's three-tier structure maps to CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy with the specificity CROSS was designed to accommodate. Tier I expresses the global outcome to which IFAD contributes without claiming attribution, which CROSS's goal tier and contribution causality stance (Field 11) express as a structural feature rather than a rhetorical hedge. Tier II expresses the country-level outcomes IFAD's funded projects are designed to produce, which is the standard outcome-tier register in CROSS's ToC architecture. Tier III expresses the organizational and operational results IFAD itself controls, which CROSS treats as funder-side performance under the Part XI funder obligation provisions rather than as a grantee obligation. A CROSS-conformant IFAD program produces this tier separation as a direct consequence of the ToC declaration, without the operational difficulty IFAD's RMF reviewers have repeatedly noted in attempting to keep the tiers analytically distinct when programs are designed across them.

The RIMS Core Outcome Indicators are a fixed corporate indicator menu of the kind CROSS Part V accommodates through Field 10 (institutional framework alignment). When an IFAD-funded project's outcome indicators correspond to RIMS Core Indicators, Field 10 records the Core Indicator reference. When project-specific indicators are added (which the COIM Guidelines explicitly permit alongside Core Indicators), the eleven-field specification governs them. The COIM Guidelines specify a mandatory methodology for outcome-level Core Indicators; CROSS Field 9 (collection method) carries the COIM reference. WALKRI Section 3 carries the field-instrument-level requirements that implement the methodology in the survey instruments themselves.

The three-survey cadence at baseline, midterm, and completion is the strongest direct mapping between the IFAD project measurement model and CROSS's gate architecture. The COIM Guidelines treat the baseline survey as the establishing measurement against which change will be assessed, the midterm survey as the formative check that allows course-correction, and the completion survey as the summative determination of project performance. CROSS Field 4 (baseline) is established at the entry specification gate; progress verification gates correspond to the midterm survey; the completion verification gate corresponds to the completion survey. The CROSS gate function distinction (developmental at progress, summative at completion) maps directly to the COIM's distinction between midterm and completion survey purposes, which is the same distinction IFAD's 2022 Evaluation Manual formalized at the institutional level between self- and independent evaluation.

The Independent Office of Evaluation function is the layer that distinguishes IFAD's architecture from frameworks where the funder is the only assessor of grantee performance. CROSS Part IV requires that the completion verification gate cannot be informal: the funder must have a named, published mechanism for determining whether completion criteria have been met, and grantee self-report alone does not satisfy the gate. The IOE is precisely this mechanism at the IFAD level, with the Evaluation Policy and Manual specifying the published methodology under which independent determination occurs. A CROSS-conformant IFAD program that routes completion gate determinations through the IOE (for evaluations within IOE's scope) or through a self-evaluation function operating under the 2022 Manual's standards has the institutional infrastructure CROSS Part IV requires.

## How IPAF Extends the Architecture

IPAF is not a separate measurement framework. It is a configured instance of the IFAD architecture that adds an Indigenous well-being indicator layer and inherits the rest of the infrastructure unchanged. This is the structurally important point: a CROSS-conformant program that satisfies the RMF and RIMS satisfies IPAF's framework-level requirements at the same time, and the IPAF-specific extension activates additional dimensions CROSS already accommodates.

The Indigenous well-being indicator layer was developed through the Indigenous Peoples' Forum at IFAD, drawing on indicators suggested by Indigenous Peoples' representatives at the Forum's second global meeting and on the monitoring and evaluation systems built by the three regional IPAF partners. The layer addresses domains the corporate RMF does not surface in indicator form: identity and cultural continuity, Indigenous knowledge systems and their transmission, land and natural resource rights including secure tenure, livelihoods rooted in traditional practice, gender with specific attention to Indigenous women's empowerment, and Indigenous youth participation in community decision-making and national development plans. The framework draws explicitly on UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples principles, particularly self-determined development, which IFAD has stated as the principle on which IPAF rests.

CROSS accommodates this extension through three existing provisions, without requiring the standard to be modified.

First, the population scope declaration (Part II, beneficiary engagement and validation dimension) requires that programs claiming to address a problem experienced by a defined population validate the claimed FROM state with parties outside the applicant's control. For IPAF grants, this is operationalized through the co-management structure: FIMI, Samburu Women Trust, and Tebtebba serve as the regional bodies whose validation is independent of any single applicant and culturally competent within their region. The Indigenous Peoples' Forum at IFAD provides a second layer of validation at the global level. CROSS's population scope provisions accommodate this co-management structure as the form independent validation takes when the funded population is an Indigenous community rather than a generic named-population member group.

Second, the disaggregation requirement (which most CROSS-aligned frameworks treat as a sex-disaggregation minimum) is configurable at the program level to add Indigenous identity, age cohort (particularly youth), and traditional knowledge-holder status as additional disaggregation categories. CROSS's disaggregation ratchet provision (categories established at the entry gate cannot be dropped at subsequent gates) ensures that an IPAF grantee who disaggregates by Indigenous women, Indigenous youth, and traditional knowledge holders in the baseline survey cannot drop those disaggregations at midterm or completion. The ratchet addresses the attrition failure mode that has been observed in mainstream development programs that introduce Indigenous-specific disaggregation under pressure and then quietly drop it when the operational difficulty becomes visible.

Third, the optional environmental and social impact dimension (Part II) is activated for IPAF projects given the cultural heritage and land rights stakes. When activated, the dimension follows the A/B/C classification structure derived from IFC Performance Standards and Green Climate Fund Environmental and Social Policy, both of which specify Free, Prior and Informed Consent requirements for projects affecting Indigenous Peoples. For IPAF specifically, the FPIC requirement is structural to the program rather than a triggered safeguard, which CROSS accommodates by allowing the funder to configure the dimension as universally active across the program.

The IPAF cycle assessment publication practice (with the fourth and fifth cycle assessments publicly available) is the form completion gate determinations take at the program level. CROSS treats these publications as gate records under the Part IV gate record legibility provisions: they constitute publicly verifiable evidence of the program's determinations about its grantees' performance, queryable by any independent party, which is the function CROSS expects of completion gate documentation regardless of the format the program uses to produce it.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements address the data quality risks that the IFAD architecture's strengths do not by themselves resolve. The RMF specifies what indicators must be reported and to what tier they belong. RIMS and the COIM Guidelines specify what methodology must be used for outcome-level Core Indicator surveys. Neither layer specifies what makes the intake fields and survey instruments collecting indicator data into measurement instruments in the WALKRI sense: fields whose criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, and conformance threshold are committed to in writing before any data is collected.

For RIMS Core Indicators, the COIM Guidelines provide much of this specification at the methodology level. A WALKRI pre-publication audit applied to the survey instruments that implement the COIM methodology resolves the remaining specification work at the instrument level: which response options are admissible for each Core Indicator, what the unit of analysis is for each survey question, what evidence form an enumerator must record alongside a positive coding, and what the conformance threshold is below which the data point cannot be entered into the project's official RIMS submission. This is the layer at which the COIM Guidelines' "mandatory methodology" becomes a measurement instrument rather than a procedural reference.

For the Indigenous well-being indicators that extend RIMS in IPAF projects, WALKRI's pre-publication audit is structurally more consequential because the well-being indicators are by design contextually variable. The criterion intent requirement ensures that what each well-being indicator is designed to detect (cultural continuity, secure land tenure, traditional knowledge transmission, Indigenous women's empowerment) is articulated in writing in terms appropriate to the specific Indigenous community before the survey instrument enters the field. The operational definition requirement specifies what an observed condition must look like to qualify as a positive data point, in the community's own terms rather than in generic development vocabulary. The response form requirement specifies whether the instrument is a structured interview guide, a community mapping protocol, a self-assessment tool developed with the regional IPAF partner, or another instrument culturally appropriate to the context. The evidence form and conformance threshold close the audit chain.

Applied to the Indigenous well-being indicator layer, WALKRI's pre-publication requirements do not eliminate the contextual variation that is the whole point of the layer existing alongside the corporate RIMS Core Indicators. They ensure that the contextually appropriate definition is committed to in writing before the survey begins, which is the minimum condition for the resulting data to be auditable, comparable within the grant's own reporting cycle, and usable in the IPAF cycle assessment without falling back on generic development indicator coding that the Indigenous well-being layer was created to avoid.

## Position Relative to Existing CROSS+WALKRI Coverage

Two cross-references are worth stating explicitly.

This statement is the first dedicated Indigenous-extension Part XII entry in CROSS+WALKRI's coverage. It does not close the broader Indigenous and First Nations grantmaking gap documented in the coverage gap map. The Te Puni Kokiri Effectiveness for Maori Framework is being promoted separately as a Crown-Indigenous outcomes framework with a distinct lineage from settler-state development frameworks. The structural gap on the Indigenous-led side, which the gap map sector note attributes to a deliberate refusal by Indigenous-led funds to encode measurement architecture imported from settler philanthropy, persists. This statement covers an IFI-administered framework with substantive Indigenous co-design at the indicator layer; it does not represent the Indigenous-led side of the sector.

IFAD itself is not displaced as a coverage entry by any prior Part XII statement. The Multilateral Development Banks group statement covers ADB, AfDB, IDB, EBRD, GCF, and GEF; it does not cover IFAD, which operates as a specialized UN agency and IFI with its own corporate measurement architecture distinct from the MDB group's structures. This statement adds IFAD as a standalone Part XII entry while consolidating the RMF, RIMS, IOE, and IPAF layers under a single compatibility document. If a subsequent Part XII pass extends the MDB group to include IFAD or treats IFI architectures as a unified group, this statement should be cross-referenced as the IFAD-specific entry rather than absorbed, because the Indigenous well-being extension is specific to IPAF and would be lost in a generic IFI consolidation.
