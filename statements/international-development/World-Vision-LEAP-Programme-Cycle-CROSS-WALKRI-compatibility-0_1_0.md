# World Vision LEAP Programme Cycle

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

World Vision International publishes the Learning through Evaluation with Accountability and Planning (LEAP) framework, 2nd Edition, as its formal programme cycle methodology governing all World Vision programmes globally and required of implementing partners. LEAP is a six-component framework: Design, Monitor, Evaluate, Transition, Reflect, and Document. The Reflect stage is a structural innovation not found in any other framework covered by CROSS+WALKRI: it is a formally named, required programme cycle stage in which learning from monitoring and evaluation is institutionalized before the next programme cycle opens. This creates a gap in CROSS's current architecture, which defines four gates covering a single programme cycle but does not name a structured inter-cycle reflection stage as a gate type. CROSS satisfies LEAP's Design, Monitor, and Evaluate requirements directly. The Reflect stage is documented here as a new primitive in the CROSS+WALKRI framework map.

---

## The World Vision LEAP Framework

World Vision International's LEAP framework, 2nd Edition, is the organisation's formally stated programme cycle methodology applied across all World Vision programmes worldwide. It is required of implementing partners and governs the full lifecycle of programme design, implementation, monitoring, evaluation, and learning.

LEAP defines six formal components. Design covers programme and project design, baseline, and MEAL plan development. Monitor covers ongoing data collection and routine programme performance review. Evaluate covers formal evaluations, including mid-term and final evaluations. Transition covers close-out and transition planning. Reflect is a formally named stage in which the programme team and partners review learning from monitoring and evaluation and institutionalize lessons before the next design phase opens. Document covers knowledge management and dissemination of learning.

The Reflect stage is structurally unlike anything else in CROSS+WALKRI's coverage. In all other frameworks, the cycle ends at evaluation and close-out: final evaluation findings are written into a report, and the report may or may not feed into the next design cycle. LEAP requires a formal Reflect stage between cycles: the learning from one cycle's monitoring and evaluation must be reviewed, synthesized, and institutionalized before the next programme design phase begins. This is not the completion gate of the closing programme, nor is it the entry gate of the opening programme. It is a distinct, required stage occupying the space between them.

Two additional elements distinguish LEAP from most covered frameworks: the requirement for a baseline and evaluation plan at Design stage (before any programme activity begins), and the Community Based Feedback and Response Mechanism (CBFRM), which is a structured beneficiary accountability system built into the programme cycle across all six components, not treated as a separate accountability function.

## How CROSS Satisfies the LEAP Framework

CROSS addresses LEAP's Design, Monitor, and Evaluate requirements directly through its four-gate sequence, but the Reflect stage introduces a structural gap that CROSS's current gate architecture does not cover.

**Entry specification gate and LEAP Design:** LEAP's requirement that a baseline and evaluation plan be completed at the Design stage, before any programme activity begins, is CROSS's entry specification gate. CROSS requires that indicators, baseline values, target values, data sources, collection methods, and causality stances all be declared before applications open. A CROSS-conformant programme funded under World Vision LEAP will have its evaluation plan fully specified at entry, satisfying LEAP's Design-stage baseline requirement as a structural consequence of conformance.

**Application and completion gates and LEAP Monitor and Evaluate:** LEAP's Monitor component corresponds to ongoing performance tracking against pre-specified indicators, which CROSS's application gate covers through its requirement that programs demonstrate ongoing conformance to their entry specifications. LEAP's Evaluate component, covering formal mid-term and final evaluations, corresponds to CROSS's completion gate: the structured evidence review against pre-specified indicators that determines whether the programme produced what it stated it would produce.

**Reflect stage as a new primitive:** The LEAP Reflect stage does not correspond to any existing CROSS gate. It is neither the completion gate of the closing cycle nor the entry specification gate of the next cycle. It is a required structured stage between cycles in which learning is reviewed and institutionalized. This is a new primitive in the CROSS+WALKRI framework map, here named the inter-cycle reflection stage. Whether this should be modeled as a fifth gate type in CROSS, as a required transition procedure between the attestation gate of one cycle and the entry specification gate of the next, or as a distinct protocol outside the gate sequence is a decision for future CROSS revision. This statement documents the gap and proposes the name.

**Attestation gate and LEAP Document:** LEAP's Document component, covering knowledge management and dissemination of evaluation learning, corresponds to CROSS's attestation gate: the public record that the programme completed its cycle in conformance with its entry specification, available for independent verification.

**CBFRM and CROSS coverage:** LEAP's Community Based Feedback and Response Mechanism, built into all six programme cycle components, is the same coverage gap identified in the CRS ProPack compatibility statement: CROSS's current gate architecture does not name beneficiary accountability mechanisms as a required gate element. LEAP makes this gap particularly visible because the CBFRM is not an add-on to the programme cycle but is integrated into all six components.

| LEAP Component | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| Design (baseline and evaluation plan before activities) | Entry specification gate |
| Monitor (ongoing performance data) | Application gate conformance tracking |
| Evaluate (mid-term and final evaluation) | Completion gate evidence review |
| Transition (close-out and handover) | Attestation gate (cycle closure record) |
| Reflect (inter-cycle learning institutionalization) | New primitive: inter-cycle reflection stage (not yet in CROSS architecture) |
| Document (knowledge management and dissemination) | Attestation gate (public cycle record) |
| Community Based Feedback and Response Mechanism | Coverage gap: beneficiary accountability not named as a required CROSS gate element |
| Indicator specification in MEAL plan | Eleven-field indicator specification, Fields 1-5 (name, definition, unit, baseline, target) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements apply at the intake instrument level for LEAP programmes. The LEAP Design stage requires that intake instruments be developed as part of the baseline and MEAL plan before programme activities begin. The WALKRI audit must therefore occur at the Design stage, before any baseline data collection instrument is shared with programme participants or implementing partners.

For each intake field in a LEAP programme's data collection tools, WALKRI requires: criterion intent (what the field is designed to measure within the Theory of Change), operational definition (exactly what qualifies and does not qualify for that field), response form (how a valid response is structured), evidence form (what documentation is required to support the response), and compliance threshold (the minimum standard that constitutes a valid data point). In the LEAP context, these requirements apply across all six programme cycle components that use intake instruments, including the Community Based Feedback and Response Mechanism.

The CBFRM creates a specific WALKRI application: feedback and complaint intake fields must specify what constitutes a valid complaint (operational definition), how a complaint is documented (response form), what evidence is required to close the complaint (evidence form), and what the resolution timeline and standard is (compliance threshold). Without this level of specification, CBFRM data across different programme contexts cannot be compared and the mechanism cannot be audited. WALKRI's pre-publication audit applied to the CBFRM intake instruments before any programme opens makes this comparability possible.
