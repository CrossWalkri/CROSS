# Lutheran World Relief (LWR) Design, Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (DMEL) Framework

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

Lutheran World Relief (LWR), a Corus International member organization headquartered in Baltimore and operating in approximately 29 countries across Africa, Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America, publishes a Design, Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (DMEL) Framework as its formal MEL system. The framework, available publicly at lwr.org/static/dmel/index.html and lwr.org/dmel, was developed through a five-step participatory approach drawing on LWR's own best practices and adapted peer and donor resources. It is formally stated to apply to "all LWR staff and partners worldwide."

A scope note is required at the outset. The DMEL Framework is not a grantee-facing standard imposing requirements on independent applicants in the manner of a foundation grant program. LWR is primarily a direct implementer working through local partner organizations rather than a grantmaker funding open competitive rounds. The DMEL Framework governs LWR's own program design and the MEL systems co-developed with its local implementing partners (for example, NEEDS Nepal and DAFUO in LWR's Youth and Women Entrepreneurship Development project). CROSS+WALKRI compatibility is meaningful here because LWR's local implementing partners are functionally equivalent to grantees in measurement terms: they receive funds, co-design MEL systems, collect data against pre-specified indicators, and report outcomes against a published framework. The DMEL Framework is the standard those measurement obligations rest on. CROSS satisfies the framework's structural requirements; WALKRI ensures that intake instruments built under DMEL are measurement instruments before partners encounter them.

This statement is distinct from any treatment of the Lutheran World Federation (LWF) Collective Outcome Framework. LWF is a separate global communion of Lutheran churches, not a Corus International organization. LWF's Collective Outcome Framework applies to LWF's own country programs through different decision-standing rules and reporting architecture, and is treated separately in CROSS+WALKRI's coverage notes.

## The LWR DMEL Framework's Approach

The DMEL Framework was developed by LWR's MEL leadership (notably Narayan Gyawali, then Regional M&E Manager for Asia and the Middle East) as an upgrade to LWR's prior M&E system. It was built through a participatory process across country, regional, and headquarters levels rather than handed down from Baltimore, and it draws openly on peer agency and donor practice. The framework is published in English, French, and Spanish and is offered for public use with attribution.

The framework's structural backbone is a five-step approach to designing a robust result-oriented M&E system. Although LWR uses its own internal step terminology in operational tools, the substantive ordering documented in published materials and the Gyawali 2014 paper "Five Steps Approach of Result Oriented M&E System: Lessons Learnt from LWR's Participatory DMEL Framework" proceeds through problem and solutions analysis, Theory of Change development, indicator and means-of-verification specification in a logframe, transfer of indicators to an M&E matrix as the operational instrument, and project-cycle learning and accountability practices. The DMEL site organizes its tools around tabs that correspond to these substantive stages, including Solutions Analysis and Theory of Change, Logframe and M&E Matrix, baseline and endline design, and Accountability and Quality.

Three elements of the DMEL Framework are structurally significant for CROSS+WALKRI mapping.

First, Theory of Change precedes indicator selection. The Solutions Analysis and Theory of Change tab documents how the project design is expected to work before indicators are chosen. Indicators and means of verification are then derived from the Theory of Change, organized in a logframe, and transferred to the M&E Matrix as the operational starting point. Selecting indicators before specifying the Theory of Change is not DMEL-conformant.

Second, baseline and endline are budgetary requirements at design time. All projects under the DMEL Framework must budget for an M&E kickoff meeting, a baseline, and an endline. This is a design-stage commitment, not a reporting-stage afterthought: the resources required to make measurement possible are reserved before implementation begins.

Third, the DMEL Framework names accountability and quality as a distinct framework element rather than treating accountability as an optional add-on. Quality, transparency, and accountability are formally surfaced as requirements that operate across the project cycle, which positions community feedback and partner co-development as system components rather than discretionary practices.

The participatory development process is itself part of the framework's identity. The DMEL Framework was co-developed across LWR's country, regional, and headquarters levels, and LWR's published account presents the framework as continuing to operate participatorily with local implementing partners during project-level MEL plan development. Local partner co-design of the MEL plan is consequently a feature of the framework rather than a project-specific accommodation.

## How CROSS Satisfies the DMEL Framework

CROSS addresses the DMEL Framework at the level of its gate sequence and indicator specification fields.

**Entry specification gate and the DMEL design stage.** The DMEL Framework's requirement that the M&E system, baseline plan, Theory of Change, logframe, and M&E Matrix be developed at the design stage before implementation begins is the partner-facing equivalent of CROSS's entry specification gate. CROSS requires that the obligation mode, indicators, baseline conditions, target conditions, evidence scope, and evidence strength all be declared before the round opens and the entry submission is accepted. A CROSS-conformant program built under the DMEL Framework has its full MEL system specified at the entry gate as a structural consequence of conformance, satisfying the design-stage budgetary and specification commitments DMEL imposes.

**Theory of Change ordering.** The DMEL Framework's requirement that Theory of Change be specified before indicator selection is exactly the ordering CROSS enforces. CROSS's outcome and impact tiers, together with the indicator specification fields, require that the construct being measured be derived from a stated change pathway. An indicator that does not correspond to a level in a stated Theory of Change cannot be entered into a CROSS-conformant gate configuration.

**Indicator specification and the M&E Matrix.** The DMEL Framework's M&E Matrix collects, for each indicator, the name, definition, unit, baseline value, target value, data source, collection method, frequency, and responsibility. These are precisely the fields CROSS's indicator specification requires at the entry gate, and the M&E Matrix is functionally a DMEL-flavored implementation of the same content. A program operating a DMEL-conformant M&E Matrix produces the data that a CROSS-conformant entry specification gate requires, in the same fields, before any data collection begins.

**Build, change, and retroactive obligation modes.** The DMEL Framework operates primarily in a change-obligation register: its purpose is to measure changes in defined populations resulting from LWR programs and their partner implementers. CROSS's change-obligation mode covers this directly. Where LWR programs include infrastructure construction, deliverable production (training materials, agricultural tools, irrigation systems), or other build-character outputs, CROSS's build-obligation mode applies to those components. The DMEL Framework does not prescribe an obligation mode in the CROSS sense; CROSS provides the mode taxonomy that lets a DMEL program declare its obligation explicitly rather than implicitly.

**Local partner co-design and coherence disclosure.** The DMEL Framework's reliance on local partner co-design of MEL plans is structurally compatible with CROSS's coherence disclosure requirement, which requires programs to document how their work relates to other interventions and contributing entities in the same problem space. A CROSS-conformant program implemented through a local partner can use the coherence disclosure to document the partner relationship, the division of MEL responsibilities, and the shared specification of the indicators that govern the work. This is the same documentation a DMEL Framework partner co-design process produces.

**Baseline budgeting and the sufficiency architecture declaration.** The DMEL Framework's requirement that every project budget for an M&E kickoff, baseline, and endline at the design stage is satisfied through CROSS's sufficiency architecture declaration and Part IV gate configuration. CROSS requires the funder to publish what completion-gate evidence will be assessed and at what strength, which forces the program to plan and resource baseline and endline measurement at the design stage as a precondition for offering the round at all.

**Accountability and quality as a coverage observation.** The DMEL Framework's formal naming of accountability and quality as a distinct framework element, including community feedback obligations toward beneficiaries, surfaces the same coverage gap identified in the CRS ProPack and World Vision LEAP compatibility statements. CROSS's current gate architecture does not yet name affected-population verification mechanisms as a required gate element. DMEL is the third major international development MEL framework in CROSS+WALKRI coverage to make this gap visible. This is documented here for consideration in a future CROSS revision.

| DMEL Framework Element | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| Design stage MEL system specification before implementation | Entry specification gate (full indicator and gate specification before activities begin) |
| Theory of Change before indicator selection | CROSS Theory of Change hierarchy; indicator construct derivation requirement |
| Logframe and M&E Matrix | CROSS indicator specification fields (name, definition, unit, baseline, target, source, method, frequency, responsibility) |
| Baseline and endline budgeted at design stage | Part IV gate configuration; sufficiency architecture declaration; pre-round publication of completion-gate evidence requirements |
| Local partner co-design of MEL plans | Coherence disclosure (relationship to contributing entities documented at entry gate) |
| Accountability and quality (community feedback) | Coverage gap: affected-population verification mechanisms not yet named as a required CROSS gate element |
| Project-cycle learning and adaptation | Completion gate evidence review; ongoing progress-gate documentation against pre-specified indicators |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements apply at the level of each intake field in the DMEL Framework's data collection instruments. Because the DMEL Framework requires that the M&E Matrix and data collection tools be specified at the design stage, before partner implementation begins, the WALKRI audit must occur at the same stage: before instruments are shared with local implementing partners and before any baseline data are collected.

For each intake field in a DMEL data collection tool, WALKRI requires criterion intent (what the field is designed to measure within the Theory of Change), operational definition (exactly what counts and does not count for that field), response form (how a valid response is structured), evidence form (what documentary evidence is required to support the response), and conformance threshold (the minimum standard a response must meet to be a valid data point). In the DMEL context, these requirements apply to every field that feeds an indicator in the M&E Matrix, including the fields that local partner enumerators use during baseline, midline, and endline data collection.

The DMEL Framework's local partner co-design model creates a specific WALKRI opportunity. Because MEL plans are co-developed with local implementing partners rather than imposed on them, partners can participate in specifying the five WALKRI elements for each intake field at design time. This is the strongest form of co-production WALKRI's pre-publication audit makes possible: partners are not handed a finished instrument and asked to collect against it; they participate in specifying what each field is meant to capture, what evidence form satisfies it, and what threshold determines a valid entry. Without WALKRI, the DMEL participatory design process risks producing instruments that look well-specified at the framework level but are operationally underspecified at the field level. WALKRI provides the field-level discipline that turns a DMEL participatory design process into auditable data collection.

The DMEL Framework's accountability and quality element extends WALKRI's relevance to community feedback intake. Where the DMEL Framework requires community feedback mechanisms as part of accountability and quality, those mechanisms collect intake data: what constitutes a valid complaint or feedback item, how it is documented, what evidence resolves it, and what timeline applies. WALKRI applied to community feedback intake fields prevents the common failure mode in which feedback mechanisms collect narratives that cannot be aggregated, compared across project contexts, or audited for resolution.
