# IDRC Research Quality Plus (RQ+)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

Canada's IDRC developed Research Quality Plus (RQ+) as an evaluation framework for research-for-development programs that deliberately departs from bibliometric proxies and standard OECD DAC criteria in favor of four substantive dimensions: Research Integrity, Legitimacy, Importance, and Positioning for Use. Each dimension maps directly onto structural elements of CROSS gate architecture and WALKRI's field specification requirements. RQ+ is gaining adoption beyond IDRC, including uptake by CGIAR and other research-for-development funders, making this compatibility statement relevant to a growing category of research grant programs seeking a shared accountability infrastructure.

---

## The IDRC RQ+ Framework's Approach

Research Quality Plus was developed by IDRC to address a persistent problem in evaluating research-for-development work: conventional research quality proxies, particularly bibliometric measures such as citation counts and journal impact factors, do not measure what matters in the development context. A paper cited 500 times by other academics is not necessarily a paper that informed a policy change, supported a practitioner decision, or addressed a question that affected communities recognized as significant. RQ+ replaces these proxies with four substantive dimensions that ask different questions about research quality.

Research Integrity (Dimension 1) concerns methodological rigor: whether the research design is appropriate to the research question, whether methods were applied correctly, and whether findings are adequately supported by the evidence generated. This is not equivalent to asking whether research was published in a peer-reviewed journal; it is asking about the internal quality of the research process itself.

Legitimacy (Dimension 2) concerns whether the research design is appropriate to context and whether affected communities were meaningfully included in shaping the research agenda. Legitimacy in the RQ+ sense is not a procedural box; it is an assessment of whether the research process respected the knowledge, priorities, and authority of the communities whose situations the research examined. Research conducted on a community without involving that community's perspectives in research design fails the Legitimacy dimension regardless of its methodological rigor.

Importance (Dimension 3) concerns the significance and relevance of the research question itself. High-quality research on an unimportant question does not constitute high-quality research-for-development. Importance is assessed relative to development needs, practitioner priorities, and the significance of potential findings for the populations the research concerns.

Positioning for Use (Dimension 4) concerns the steps taken by researchers to increase the likelihood that findings will inform policy and practice. This dimension explicitly recognizes that research quality cannot be assessed by looking only at the research process; it must also examine whether the research was designed and communicated in ways that make uptake plausible. Positioning for Use does not require that findings actually change policy, but it does require evidence that researchers actively worked to make that pathway viable.

RQ+ explicitly excludes bibliometric measures from its quality assessment. This is a principled departure from academic research evaluation norms, grounded in the recognition that development impact and academic citation patterns are structurally weakly correlated.

## How CROSS Satisfies the RQ+ Framework

Each of RQ+'s four dimensions maps to a CROSS structural element, and in each case the mapping is functional rather than superficial: CROSS satisfies the structural requirement the RQ+ dimension is designed to assess.

**Research Integrity (Dimension 1) and CROSS entry gate indicator specification.** RQ+ Dimension 1 asks whether research methods are appropriate, correctly applied, and evidentially sufficient. CROSS's entry gate requires eleven-field indicator specifications that include the methodological choices underlying each measurement: what is being measured, how it is being measured, with what instrument, and what verification pathway exists for the evidence produced. This specification requires program administrators to commit to methodological choices before data collection begins, creating the pre-commitment record that methodological rigor assessment requires. A CROSS-compliant program has documented its research design at instrument level before any application opens; RQ+ Dimension 1 assessment can examine that record against the evidence subsequently produced.

**Legitimacy (Dimension 2) and CROSS population scope and coherence disclosure.** RQ+ Dimension 2 asks whether affected communities were included and whether the research design respected community knowledge and priorities. CROSS's population scope declaration specifies who is affected by the program, with what granularity and by what evidence of community connection. CROSS's coherence disclosure, requiring documentation of how the program relates to the broader landscape of interventions in the same problem area, captures the multi-actor environment within which community knowledge is situated. Together these fields constitute the structural form of legitimacy evidence: who is affected is documented, how the program situates itself relative to other actors in that community's context is documented, and independent evidence sources that fall outside the grantee organization are specified in the evidence strength taxonomy.

**Importance (Dimension 3) and CROSS Theory of Change goal declaration.** RQ+ Dimension 3 asks what significance attaches to the research question relative to development needs. CROSS requires that every program declare its long-term goal: the condition in the world that the program is designed to contribute to achieving. This declaration must be made before applications open and must be specific enough to be evaluated against the development needs landscape. A program that cannot articulate a significant goal-level condition it is contributing to cannot satisfy CROSS's entry gate, and for the same reason it cannot satisfy RQ+ Dimension 3.

**Positioning for Use (Dimension 4) and CROSS continuation gate.** RQ+ Dimension 4 asks what steps were taken to make findings actionable by policy and practice audiences. CROSS's continuation gate requires that programs document what was learned in a completed cycle and specify how that learning is being incorporated into the next cycle's obligations before a new round opens. This documentation constitutes the structural form of Positioning for Use evidence: findings are not merely produced and archived, they are documented as inputs to subsequent program design, creating a traceable pathway from evidence to programming decision.

| RQ+ Dimension | CROSS Structural Element |
|---|---|
| Research Integrity (methodological rigor) | Entry gate eleven-field indicator specification + WALKRI instrument precision |
| Legitimacy (context appropriateness, community inclusion) | Population scope declaration + coherence disclosure + independent evidence pathway |
| Importance (significance of research question) | Theory of Change goal declaration (required before applications open) |
| Positioning for Use (steps toward policy/practice uptake) | Continuation gate (learning documented before next round opens) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements are the primary mechanism by which CROSS satisfies RQ+ Dimension 1's methodological rigor standard at the data collection instrument level. RQ+ Dimension 1 is not satisfied by a general description of research methods; it requires evidence that methods were appropriate to the question, correctly specified, and applied with sufficient precision to generate defensible findings. WALKRI's requirement that each intake field be specified with instrument type, unit, collection method, verification pathway, and access information before applications open creates exactly the instrument-level documentation that RQ+ Dimension 1 assessment examines.

WALKRI's evidence form requirement, specifying artifact type and access path for each evidence item, also supports RQ+ Dimension 4. Positioning for Use requires not only that findings be produced but that they be accessible to policy and practice audiences in usable forms. WALKRI's access path specification ensures that evidence artifacts are documented with sufficient specificity for a Dimension 4 assessor to evaluate whether findings are actually accessible to the intended audiences, or whether they exist only in formats or locations that functionally restrict access despite nominal availability.

For programs being evaluated under RQ+, CROSS+WALKRI compliance provides a documentation infrastructure that satisfies all four dimensions by design, eliminating the need to construct RQ+ compliance evidence separately from program operations.
