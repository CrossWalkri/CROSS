# Nordic Bilateral Aid Frameworks (Sida, Norad, Danida) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

Three Nordic bilateral aid agencies, Sida (Sweden), Norad (Norway), and Danida (Denmark), each publish evaluation frameworks that apply OECD DAC criteria as their primary reference while adding agency-specific requirements. Because CROSS addresses the full OECD DAC framework as a structural consequence of conformance (documented in a separate compatibility statement), CROSS-conformant programs satisfy the Nordic agencies' shared OECD DAC baseline. This statement documents the agency-specific requirements that go beyond the DAC baseline: Sida's theory-of-change validation requirement, Norad's dual-level results reporting structure, and Danida's extension to ALNAP humanitarian criteria.

The three agencies collectively fund a significant portion of European bilateral development cooperation and apply shared expectations about results measurement and evaluation quality to their partner organizations. Programs that receive funding from Sida, Norad, or Danida are expected to meet these standards at the organizational and project level. CROSS+WALKRI satisfies the agency-specific requirements through provisions that are already part of the CROSS architecture, not through additions specific to any individual agency.

---

## OECD DAC Baseline

All three agencies apply the OECD DAC evaluation criteria as their primary framework: relevance, coherence, effectiveness, efficiency, impact, and sustainability. CROSS satisfies these six criteria through its gate architecture and obligation structure, as documented in the OECD DAC compatibility statement. This statement does not re-establish that baseline; it addresses the Nordic-specific additions above it.

Programs that have satisfied CROSS's entry specification gate, progress verification gates, completion gate, and continuation gate have produced the evidence infrastructure that OECD DAC-aligned evaluations require for all six criteria. The DAC criteria are not additional requirements for CROSS-conformant programs in Nordic bilateral contexts; they are baseline consequences of CROSS conformance.

## Sida: Theory of Change Validation

Sida requires that partner organizations have an articulated theory of change that Sida evaluators can test and validate. This requirement goes beyond requiring a ToC to exist; Sida evaluators are expected to assess whether the ToC's causal logic is plausible, whether the assumptions are explicit and testable, and whether the evidence plan is adequate to support the causal claims made.

CROSS's Theory of Change architecture produces a theory of change that is structured for external validation at the level Sida requires. The CROSS ToC hierarchy declares Goals, Outcomes, Outputs, and Activities with explicit causal pathways linking each level. The pathway registry requires that causal links be named and that the assumptions underlying each link be stated as testable propositions, not narrative assertions. This structural explicitness is what Sida evaluators need to assess causal plausibility; a ToC that exists only as a diagram or a paragraph cannot be systematically validated.

Sida also requires decentralized evaluations with publicly accessible reports. CROSS's Attestation Corpus is the structural mechanism for this requirement. Gate records accumulated across the program lifecycle constitute an auditable, publicly disclosed record of the program's performance against its declared ToC. A Sida evaluator can assess whether the ToC's causal claims are supported by the Attestation Corpus records without requesting additional documentation from the partner organization.

WALKRI supports Sida's validation requirement at the instrument level. The criterion intent requirement ensures that each field collecting ToC evidence has a stated measurement purpose, enabling Sida evaluators to assess whether the data collection design is adequate to test the causal assumptions. The validity requirement ensures that fields measure what the ToC claims they measure, not proxies that have drifted from the intended construct.

## Norad: Dual-Level Results Reporting

Norad's "Guidelines for Applicants on Results and Knowledge Management" require results at output, outcome, and impact levels with corresponding indicators, reported at two levels: project-level Results Assessments (annual and final) and a portfolio-level Results Report aggregating across Norad's full program portfolio.

CROSS's gate architecture produces the project-level Results Assessment structure directly. Progress verification gates generate milestone-level output evidence. The completion gate generates the output-level Results Assessment. The continuation gate generates the outcome-level assessment, including the sustainability stance that Norad's impact-level reporting requires. These are not additional reports that CROSS-conformant programs must produce; they are the gate records that CROSS's architecture generates as a consequence of gate passage.

Norad's portfolio-level Results Report depends on commensurable data across all funded projects: if each project defines outputs, outcomes, and impacts differently, aggregation is impossible. CROSS addresses this at the specification layer. When Norad applies CROSS as the program specification standard for its grant portfolio, the indicator specification, ToC hierarchy, and gate architecture are consistent across all funded projects. Portfolio aggregation becomes a query on the Attestation Corpus, not a reconciliation exercise across heterogeneous datasets.

| Norad Requirement | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Output-level results (annual) | Progress verification gate records; completion gate deliverable specification | Full satisfaction. Gate records at each milestone produce timestamped output evidence against pre-specified indicators. |
| Outcome-level results (final) | Change obligation target condition; continuation gate sustainability stance | Full satisfaction. The completion gate requires outcome evidence against the pre-specified change condition. |
| Impact-level results | Goal-level ToC declaration; causality stance (contribution claim to higher-level change) | Full satisfaction. CROSS requires a goal-level declaration situating the program's outcomes within a broader impact claim. |
| Portfolio aggregation | Attestation Corpus as queryable record across all CROSS-conformant programs in a portfolio | Full satisfaction given consistent CROSS specification across the portfolio. |

## Danida: Evaluation Policy 2020 and ALNAP Extension

Danida's Evaluation Policy 2020 and Evaluation Guidelines (April 2024) apply the five OECD DAC criteria and extend to ALNAP humanitarian criteria for evaluations of Danish-funded humanitarian interventions. ALNAP criteria add connectedness, coverage, and appropriateness to the DAC baseline.

CROSS's compatibility with the Core Humanitarian Standard (CHS), documented in a separate statement, is the primary structural pathway for Danida's ALNAP extension. The CHS is the sector's primary humanitarian accountability framework; programs that satisfy CHS Commitments 7, 8, and 9 through CROSS+WALKRI are operating at the humanitarian accountability level that ALNAP criteria assess. The CHS-CROSS compatibility statement documents this alignment in detail.

For Danida's evaluation quality requirements specifically, WALKRI's five field requirements satisfy the Accuracy standard that all three Nordic agencies apply in their evaluations. Danida's Evaluation Guidelines require that evaluation evidence be collected through instruments that are valid, reliable, and independent of program self-reporting. These are precisely the requirements that WALKRI's criterion intent, operational definition, response form justification, evidence form, and compliance threshold requirements address at the intake instrument design level.

## WALKRI and Nordic Accuracy Standards

All three Nordic agencies apply evaluation Accuracy standards drawn from the OECD EvalNet norms and, in the case of Sida and Norad, from their own published quality criteria for decentralized evaluations. Accuracy at the evaluation stage depends on Accuracy at the data collection stage: an evaluation can only be as accurate as the instruments that generated the evidence it relies on.

WALKRI's pre-publication audit applies before CROSS's entry specification gate opens. This means that by the time any applicant submits data, the instruments collecting that data have been reviewed for criterion intent clarity, operational definition precision, response form appropriateness, evidence form independence, and compliance threshold explicitness. Programs passing WALKRI's audit before activation are producing evidence that meets Nordic bilateral Accuracy standards by design, not by post-hoc quality review.

For Sida specifically, this matters because Sida conducts decentralized evaluations using partner-collected data. If that data was collected through instruments that were not validated before deployment, the evaluation cannot meet Sida's quality threshold regardless of how well the evaluation itself is designed. WALKRI is the specification-layer quality assurance step that makes Sida-quality evaluations possible.
