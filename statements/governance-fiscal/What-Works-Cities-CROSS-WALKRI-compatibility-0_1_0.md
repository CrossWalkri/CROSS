# What Works Cities Certification (Bloomberg Philanthropies, administered by Results for America) Compatibility - CROSS+WALKRI

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

What Works Cities Certification is the international certification standard for data-informed municipal decision-making. Launched in 2017 by Bloomberg Philanthropies and administered by Results for America, it evaluates municipal governments against criteria spanning eight foundational practice areas: Data Management, Leadership and Capacity, Performance and Analytics, Data-Driven Budget and Finance, Open Data, Rigorous Evaluations, Stakeholder Impact, and Results-Driven Contracting. Cities reaching 51 to 67 percent of criteria are recognized at Silver, 68 to 84 percent at Gold, and 85 percent or more at Platinum. Any city in Canada, Latin America, or the United States with a population of 30,000 or more is eligible to participate by completing the What Works Cities Assessment. What Works Cities Certification is a government performance certification applied to municipal governments, not a grant evaluation framework. Its structural alignment with CROSS+WALKRI is relevant in two distinct directions: when a CROSS-conformant funder grants to a What Works Cities certified municipality, the certification serves as independently verifiable evidence of grantee institutional capacity at the entry gate; and when What Works Cities itself functions as a multi-criterion certification with published evidence thresholds, its architecture maps onto CROSS's gate criterion specification and evidence strength taxonomy.

---

## The What Works Cities Certification Approach

What Works Cities Certification exists to recognize municipal governments that have built durable, citywide capacity for data-informed decision-making, not merely individual successful initiatives. Stephen Goldsmith's framing document for the program describes Certification as the translation of the What Works Cities Standard (Commit, Measure, Take Stock, Act) into concrete indicators a city's day-to-day operations can be assessed against. The point is to document sustained capacity in named practice areas rather than discrete achievements.

The eight practice areas establish the dimensions across which capacity is assessed. Data Management covers the policies and processes through which a local government inventories, shares, secures, and improves the quality of its data, including disaggregated data for decision-making and qualitative data collection from residents. Leadership and Capacity covers the chief executive's demonstrated commitment to data-informed government and the existence of designated leaders for performance management, data strategy, rigorous evaluation, and results-driven contracting. Performance and Analytics covers strategic goal selection, regular performance management meetings, public sharing of progress, and the integration of analysis into resource allocation decisions, including the disparate-impact assessment of automated decisions.

Data-Driven Budget and Finance covers the alignment of budget processes with strategic priorities and the shifting of funds from ineffective to evidence-based programs. Open Data covers codified open data policy, user-facing guidance, and the maintenance of a central public open data portal. Rigorous Evaluations covers documented commitment to evaluation, the regular launch and resourcing of evaluations, the use of evaluation results in decisions, and the adaptation of evidence-based programs from outside the city. Stakeholder Impact covers the city's role as a leader in the broader data ecosystem, including community training, resident-facing analytics services, and contribution to a broader knowledge base. Results-Driven Contracting covers the use of procurement as a strategic tool: defining goals for key procurements, measuring outcomes, assessing vendor performance, embedding open procurement data, and supporting vendor competition.

The Certification tier structure is the operational expression of the program's standard. A certified Silver city has demonstrated at least 51 percent of the 43 criteria with the required evidence; a Gold city at least 68 percent; a Platinum city at least 85 percent. The thresholds are published before any city submits, and assessment proceeds against the published criteria, not against retrospective interpretation of what should have counted. Recertification is required to maintain a city's status, which means a Silver, Gold, or Platinum designation is a present-tense statement about a city's current capacity rather than a historical recognition.

Eligibility is universal at the population threshold (30,000 or above, across Canada, Latin America, and the United States), and the assessment portal is open to any eligible city. What Works Cities partners (the Behavioral Insights Team, the Center for Government Excellence at Johns Hopkins, the Government Performance Lab at Harvard Kennedy School, Results for America, and others over time) provide coaching, training, and technical assistance, but participation does not require partnership engagement. The assessment itself is a self-administered instrument that produces a benchmark and a customized roadmap.

What Works Cities Certification is administered by Results for America, with Bloomberg Philanthropies as the founding and ongoing funder. The administration role is substantive: Results for America operates the assessment portal, evaluates submissions against the criteria, makes the certification determination, and maintains the certified-cities list as a public record. Bloomberg Philanthropies provides the funding and the brand association that makes the certification a recognized signal in municipal government circles globally.

## How CROSS Accommodates the What Works Cities Architecture

CROSS+WALKRI accommodates What Works Cities Certification in two distinct directions, and the distinction matters for how the alignment is described.

The first direction is as evidence at the entry gate of a CROSS-conformant grant program that funds municipal governments. A grant program that funds city governments faces an institutional capacity assessment dimension under CROSS Part II: the funder must determine whether the applying municipality has the operational capacity to deliver the obligations the grant entails. A What Works Cities Certification at any tier is independently verifiable evidence of broad municipal capacity in data management, performance analytics, evaluation, and results-driven contracting. The certification was produced by a named external authority (Results for America) against published criteria, which is the structural form of evidence CROSS recognizes as independent. Silver, Gold, and Platinum tiers correspond to documented capacity at increasing strength levels, with Platinum representing capacity demonstrated across 85 percent or more of the 43 criteria.

For programs that fund municipal performance improvement, evaluation work, or data infrastructure, the What Works Cities tier may be used as a development stage proxy under CROSS Part II's development stage dimension. A Platinum-certified city proposing further work in performance analytics is at Stage 4 (established infrastructure) on the development stage taxonomy, not at Stage 1 (proof-of-concept), and the evidence requirements at the completion gate should be calibrated accordingly. A non-certified city proposing the same work may be at Stage 1 or Stage 2 and the gate configuration should reflect that. This is the operational use of an external certification as evidence: the certification status is not a scoring criterion but a calibration input for the funder's gate configuration.

The second direction is as a structural parallel to CROSS's own gate architecture. What Works Cities Certification is itself a multi-criterion certification with published evidence thresholds, named criteria, and a documented administration authority. Its 43 criteria across 8 practice areas are functionally equivalent to a CROSS gate configuration: each criterion has a stated requirement, an evidence specification, and a determination that an external reviewer makes against the published standard. The Silver, Gold, and Platinum thresholds operationalize the principle that evidence strength is calibrated, not binary: a city is not simply certified or not certified, but certified at a stated strength level that reflects how much of the standard it satisfies. CROSS's evidence strength taxonomy makes the same distinction within an individual gate, and CROSS's tier structures in capacity and outcome documentation rigor make the same distinction across programs.

The recertification requirement is the structural counterpart to CROSS's continuation gate. A Certification that did not require recertification would be a historical statement about a moment of past achievement, useful for recognition but unreliable as a continuing claim. The recertification requirement ensures that the Certification status is current at the time it is consulted. CROSS continuation gates serve the same structural function: they require that obligations met at one stage remain demonstrably met to receive funding at the next stage, rather than treating past performance as a permanent warrant.

The eight practice areas map to CROSS provisions as follows.

| What Works Cities Practice Area | CROSS Provision |
|---|---|
| Data Management | Outcome documentation rigor dimension; evidence quality specifications at gates |
| Leadership and Capacity | Institutional capacity dimension; disbursement authority declaration |
| Performance and Analytics | Outcome tier; progress verification gate; performance metric specification |
| Data-Driven Budget and Finance | Sufficiency architecture declaration; revenue architecture declaration |
| Open Data | Public benefit mechanism: access provision; gate record legibility |
| Rigorous Evaluations | Impact evidence tier; pre-award indicator confirmation |
| Stakeholder Impact | Coherence disclosure and population scope declaration |
| Results-Driven Contracting | Procurement integrity dimension; gate criterion specification |

The mapping is not an instruction to municipalities that they should adopt CROSS to satisfy What Works Cities requirements, or to Results for America that the Certification should be redesigned around CROSS. The mapping documents that the two systems are addressing the same underlying questions about how a public-sector entity demonstrates capacity, and that a funder operating across both contexts can use the alignment to reduce duplicate documentation burden on grantees who are also certified municipalities.

## Where the Alignment Has Limits

What Works Cities Certification is a government performance certification, not a grant evaluation framework. The Certification assesses an existing municipal government's data and operational practices; it does not specify what a city should commit to as a condition of receiving funds for a specific project. CROSS+WALKRI specifies obligations on funded interventions and the funders who structure them. The two systems address different objects: a city's general operational capacity (What Works Cities) versus a specific grant's obligations to demonstrate intended results (CROSS+WALKRI).

This boundary matters when a CROSS-conformant program funds a What Works Cities certified municipality. The Certification establishes broad institutional capacity; it does not satisfy the entry specification gate for the specific grant, the completion verification gate for the specific deliverable, or the obligation fulfillment record for prior grants from the funder. A Platinum-certified city applying for a grant must still declare the FROM state, the population, the deliverable, and the evidence specifications for the specific work being funded. The certification reduces the institutional capacity burden but does not displace the obligation specification burden.

The boundary also matters when What Works Cities Certification is described as a "standard." It is a certification standard administered by a named authority, with criteria, thresholds, and a determination process. CROSS is a specification standard for the obligation content of grant programs, published under CC0 without an administering authority. The two are structurally different: a certification standard determines whether a specific entity meets a standard at a specific time; a specification standard defines the content of obligation that any conformant program can adopt independently. A municipality cannot be "CROSS-certified" because CROSS does not certify entities; a grant program can be CROSS-conformant because CROSS specifies what such conformance requires.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI applies where intake forms collect data that supports CROSS gate assessments. For a CROSS-conformant program that grants to municipal governments, the intake form fields collecting institutional capacity evidence should be designed as measurement instruments before any applicant encounters them. If the form asks "does your city have a designated data leader?" the field must specify what evidence answers that question (a position description, an org chart, a public-facing data leadership page) and what level of evidence satisfies the field at what strength level. The What Works Cities criteria themselves illustrate the level of specificity required: each criterion has a stated requirement and a stated evidence basis, which is precisely what WALKRI's pre-publication audit verifies for intake fields.

For municipalities that are also What Works Cities certified, intake fields can reference the certification as one acceptable evidence form, alongside other acceptable forms, rather than treating the certification as the sole acceptable evidence. This is the WALKRI-conformant way to incorporate external certifications into intake design: the certification is named as one evidence type with a stated strength level, and other evidence types are also accepted with their own strength levels. A field that accepts only What Works Cities certification as evidence would exclude non-certified municipalities that may nevertheless have the capacity the field is measuring. A field that accepts the certification alongside other evidence types preserves the strength signal of the certification while leaving the field accessible to applicants without it.
