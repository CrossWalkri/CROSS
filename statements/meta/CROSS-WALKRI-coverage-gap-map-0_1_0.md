## Purpose

This document records every major giving tradition, foundation, bilateral agency, philanthropic body, and grant evaluation framework that was researched during the development of CROSS+WALKRI compatibility documentation and for which no formally published, linkable standard was found.

A formally published standard, for the purposes of CROSS+WALKRI compatibility, means a document that: is publicly accessible at a stable URL, specifies requirements that grant programs or grantees must meet, and is either a regulatory instrument, a formal standard with declared decision-standing rules and named maintainers, or a widely adopted sector-reference framework. Practice descriptions, job postings, internal policy summaries, annual reports, and eligibility criteria alone do not qualify.

Null findings are documented here for three reasons. First, they demonstrate that CROSS+WALKRI's compatibility claims rest on genuine research and that the absence of coverage reflects the absence of published material rather than incomplete search. Second, they map real gaps in the grants ecosystem's standards infrastructure: the traditions and organizations listed here represent contexts where grantees, reviewers, operators, and funders operate without a shared measurement framework. Third, the pattern of absences reinforces why a universal standard is needed: CROSS+WALKRI was designed to provide the measurement architecture that these traditions lack at the specification level.

The following US federal grant programs were researched and found to have formally published reporting or evaluation structures that are MEDIUM priority: they have real, distinctive requirements beyond 2 CFR 200, but those requirements are thin, derivative, or program-specific rather than constituting a cross-program measurement standard.

### Pooled Funds and Intermediary Grantmakers

This category covers donor collaboratives, fiscal sponsors, affinity networks, and aggregator vehicles that pool capital from multiple sources and re-grant it. Because these entities typically pass funds through to sponsored projects, member funders, or downstream subgrantees, they rarely publish a grantee-facing measurement framework of their own; reporting standards are inherited from the underlying funders, the sponsored project's own commitments, or the fiscal host's conformance infrastructure. Each entry below was searched for a publicly accessible grantee MEL standard meeting the threshold defined in this document's Purpose section.

| Organization | What Was Found | Why Not a Standard |
|:--|:--|:--|

### Indigenous and First Nations Grantmaking

Indigenous-led and Indigenous-focused grantmaking is a distinct subsector defined by participatory decision-rules, cultural due diligence, and explicit Indigenous Data Sovereignty norms. Most Indigenous-led funds operate by design without prescriptive grantee measurement methodologies, treating measurement frameworks themselves as artifacts of settler philanthropic culture that participatory grantmaking is intended to displace. The result is a sector that has rich practice documentation and ethical guidelines but very few formally published outcome measurement standards in the CROSS+WALKRI sense.

**Sector note:** Several broader frameworks intersect with Indigenous and First Nations grantmaking in CROSS+WALKRI's existing coverage. The IFC Performance Standards and Green Climate Fund Environmental and Social Policy specify Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirements for projects affecting Indigenous Peoples. The IUCN Global Standard for Nature-based Solutions explicitly requires Indigenous rights compliance in its criteria. The IDB Invest DELTA framework and Inter-American Development Bank Corporate Results Framework operate in regions with substantial Indigenous populations and incorporate Indigenous community indicators. The CGIAR MELIA framework's domain 3 (gender, diversity, and inclusion) provides disaggregation requirements that intersect with Indigenous Data Sovereignty norms. None of these is an Indigenous-led standard; the gap on the Indigenous-led side is structural and may reflect a deliberate refusal to encode measurement architecture imported from settler philanthropy.

### Open Source and FOSS Grantmaking

Open Source and Free/Libre Open Source Software (FOSS) grantmaking has matured into a distinct subsector since roughly 2020, driven by the recognition (particularly after Log4Shell and the xz utils backdoor) that critical digital infrastructure is sustained by under-resourced maintainers. The field's most distinctive feature is that funders typically specify license requirements and project visibility criteria as eligibility gates rather than specifying outcome indicators. Several organizations below publish criteria documents that are closer to formal published standards than is typical in other sectors, and at least two are strong candidates for full Part XII treatment.

|:--|:--|:--|

## What the Gap Map Shows

The pattern across these null findings reveals three distinct types of absence:

**Type 1: Measurement happens but is not codified.** Many major foundations and bilateral agencies conduct serious, high-quality evaluation work but have not published that work as a standard others can adopt. Robert Bosch Stiftung, King Baudouin Foundation, and Leverhulme Trust are examples. In these cases, CROSS+WALKRI provides a published architecture that these foundations' evaluation practices already implicitly implement, and that grantees seeking to satisfy their unstated expectations can use as a conformant baseline.

**Type 2: Cultural giving traditions without administrative infrastructure.** Mécénat, mäzenatentum, ubuntu-based giving, and religious giving traditions predate formal measurement frameworks and operate without them by design. CROSS+WALKRI does not seek to impose measurement on traditions that have not requested it; the gap map records that these traditions exist and that no incompatible published standard was found.

**Type 3: Sector infrastructure gaps where no standard should exist but does not.** The absence of a pan-African philanthropic measurement standard, a unified Latin American grant evaluation framework, or a published standard for historical patronage traditions is a genuine gap in the ecosystem. The global grants sector lacks standards for several significant geographic and cultural contexts. CROSS+WALKRI's format-agnostic, culturally neutral architecture is designed to be adoptable in these contexts precisely because it does not encode any particular cultural giving tradition.

## Implications for Future Compatibility Work

Where a publishing body listed here subsequently publishes a formal standard, a compatibility statement should be written and this entry updated or removed. The gap map is a living document.

For organizations listed as "practice norm only," CROSS+WALKRI compatibility claims in those contexts should be framed as: "A CROSS+WALKRI-conformant program satisfies the unstated measurement expectations of [organization] by providing structured documentation of what the program produces, what evidence supports that claim, and how the causal pathway is declared."
