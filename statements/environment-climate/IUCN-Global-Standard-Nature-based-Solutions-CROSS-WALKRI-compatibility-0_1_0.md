# IUCN Global Standard for Nature-based Solutions

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The IUCN Global Standard for Nature-based Solutions (IUCN NbS Standard), adopted by the IUCN Council at its 98th meeting in 2020 and developed with more than 800 practitioners across more than 100 countries, provides the primary international framework for verifying that interventions labeled as nature-based solutions genuinely address societal challenges while maintaining or enhancing biodiversity. The Standard's eight Criteria and 28 Indicators constitute a pre-specified outcome framework that must be applied before any NbS-classified project is funded; the Green Climate Fund, the Global Environment Facility, and multiple bilateral donors now require conformance with the Standard as a condition of NbS-classified grant approval. CROSS+WALKRI satisfies this framework structurally: the eight Criteria map to CROSS's four-gate sequence, Criterion 2's biodiversity benchmarking requirement is directly satisfied by CROSS's baseline and counterfactual reference fields, and WALKRI's pre-publication requirements address the operational definition gaps that produce non-comparable biodiversity outcome data across the global NbS portfolio.

## The IUCN Global Standard for Nature-based Solutions

The IUCN Global Standard for NbS is the authoritative international definition of what constitutes a verifiable nature-based solution. Its development engaged more than 800 practitioners across more than 100 countries through a multi-year consultative process, and it was formally adopted by the IUCN Council in 2020. The Standard is not merely aspirational: GCF, GEF, and bilateral donors including KfW and the UK FCDO now require conformance for NbS-classified grantees, making the eight Criteria a de facto pre-condition for access to the largest pools of public climate and biodiversity finance.

The Standard's eight Criteria span the full project lifecycle. Criterion 1 requires that the NbS addresses a defined societal challenge. Criterion 2 requires that biodiversity is maintained or enhanced and that outcomes are benchmarked against a reference state and periodically assessed. Criterion 3 requires net gain for biodiversity. Criterion 4 requires economic viability. Criterion 5 requires inclusive, transparent, adaptive governance. Criterion 6 requires that trade-offs are balanced. Criterion 7 requires that the NbS is adaptive. Criterion 8 requires long-term sustainability. The 28 Indicators attached to these Criteria provide the measurement framework through which compliance is assessed.

The Standard does not prescribe the data collection instruments through which project developers document their baseline biodiversity state, define their target outcomes, or specify what evidence will constitute net gain. That gap is the primary source of non-comparability across the global NbS portfolio: two projects may both claim to satisfy Criterion 2 while using incompatible definitions of "biodiversity outcome" and incompatible evidence thresholds.

## How CROSS Satisfies the IUCN NbS Standard

CROSS addresses the IUCN NbS Standard's eight Criteria at the structural level through the four-gate sequence and the eleven-field indicator specification.

**Entry specification gate and Criterion 2 benchmarking:** Criterion 2's requirement to benchmark biodiversity outcomes against a reference state and assess them periodically is directly satisfied by CROSS's entry specification gate. Field 4 (baseline) requires that the pre-intervention state of the ecosystem be documented before any application opens. Field 5 (target) requires that the magnitude of expected change be specified relative to that baseline. The causality stance field requires that the project declare what the counterfactual trajectory would have been without the intervention, satisfying the reference-state comparison that Criterion 2 demands. The periodic assessment requirement corresponds to CROSS's progress verification checkpoint, and the completion gate assessment corresponds to the final period evaluation.

**Theory of Change hierarchy:** The Standard's logic connecting NbS activities to societal challenge reduction runs through the same activity-output-outcome-goal hierarchy that CROSS enforces through the Theory of Change gate. Criterion 1 (addresses societal challenge) is the goal tier; Criterion 3 (net gain for biodiversity) is the outcome tier; the specific activities producing those outcomes are the activity tier. CROSS rejects outcome claims that conflate outputs with outcomes, which prevents the common error of treating habitat restoration activity (hectares planted) as equivalent to biodiversity outcome (species abundance change against reference state).

**Disaggregation ratchet and Criterion 5 governance:** Criterion 5 requires inclusive and transparent governance with specific attention to gender and marginalized groups. CROSS's disaggregation ratchet requires that populations are not reported in aggregated form when disaggregated data is available, and that disaggregation categories established at the entry gate are maintained through completion. For NbS projects in which governance inclusivity is a mandatory indicator, CROSS's disaggregation requirement prevents the aggregation that would otherwise conceal participation gaps.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

The IUCN NbS Standard's 28 Indicators define what must be measured but do not specify how measurement instruments should be constructed before any project developer submits an application. WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements address this gap at the instrument level. Before any NbS project intake form is deployed, WALKRI requires: the criterion intent for each indicator field (what the Standard intends the field to capture and why), the operational definition (what counts as a "biodiversity outcome" or "net gain" in this specific ecosystem and measurement context), the response form (what format project developers use to document their evidence), the evidence form (what data qualifies as sufficient documentation of the claimed biodiversity state), and the conformance threshold (what minimum level of evidence satisfies the Standard's indicator at each gate).

The Standard's most acute operational definition gap is Criterion 2's biodiversity benchmark requirement. "Biodiversity" can be operationalized as species richness, species abundance, functional diversity, ecosystem integrity, or any combination of these, each requiring different data collection protocols and producing non-comparable datasets. Without WALKRI-level specification applied before any project developer begins baseline data collection, two projects both claiming to satisfy Criterion 2 may have produced data that cannot be compared in any portfolio review. WALKRI's operational definition requirement, applied to the intake instrument before deployment, locks in the specific ecological measurement protocol that constitutes biodiversity evidence for that project and for GCF or GEF portfolio comparison purposes.
