# Gold Standard for Global Goals

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Gold Standard for the Global Goals (goldstandard.org) certifies emission reductions and sustainable development co-benefits, requiring mandatory dual-track reporting (GHG outcomes plus quantified SDG co-benefits measured against SDG targets) and operating under ISEAL-compliant assurance with sequential third-party validation, verification, and certification review. From August 2025, all projects must use the digital SDG Impact Tool exclusively. CROSS satisfies Gold Standard's requirements through its multi-dimensional outcome declaration capability, eleven-field indicator specification, independent attestation pathway, and Attestation Corpus. WALKRI ensures that each outcome dimension's intake fields function as operationally defined measurement instruments before any project enters the Gold Standard pipeline.

---

## The Gold Standard's Approach

The Gold Standard for the Global Goals is the most demanding voluntary carbon and sustainable development certification standard currently in common use. It was developed specifically to address a recognized limitation of pure carbon credit standards: that certifying GHG emission reductions alone does not ensure that certified projects contribute to sustainable development or avoid harm to local communities and ecosystems. The Gold Standard requires projects to demonstrate both GHG outcomes and quantified positive sustainable development co-benefits, measured against specific SDG targets, as conditions of certification.

The mandatory dual-track structure is the Gold Standard's most distinctive feature relative to comparable standards. A project cannot certify GHG outcomes alone; it must also identify, measure, and report on sustainable development co-benefits linked to specific SDG targets. From March 2022, the SDG Impact Tools became mandatory for documenting co-benefits. From August 2025, the digital SDG Impact Tool is required exclusively, replacing earlier paper-based and Excel-based approaches. This tool-based approach ensures that co-benefit claims are structured, comparable across projects, and linked to standardized SDG indicator frameworks rather than project-specific narrative descriptions.

Gold Standard's assurance architecture is governed by ISEAL Code of Good Practice compliance. ISEAL is the global membership organization for credible sustainability standards, and its Code of Good Practice sets requirements for how standards systems are designed, governed, implemented, and evaluated. Gold Standard's ISEAL compliance links it to the international standards assurance ecosystem: it means that the standard's verification process meets internationally recognized requirements for independence, transparency, and rigor. This is a significant institutional marker. It distinguishes Gold Standard from self-defined certification schemes and places it within a broader system of mutual recognition and assurance.

The certification process follows a sequential three-party structure: third-party validation (pre-implementation assessment of project design), third-party verification (post-monitoring assessment of results), and Gold Standard Secretariat certification review (final confirmation of certification eligibility). All three stages produce formal documents; all are recorded in the Gold Standard registry. Many grant-funded clean energy, sustainable land use, and community development projects use Gold Standard certification alongside or as a condition of their grant funding, making compliance with Gold Standard's requirements a practical necessity for programs operating in these sectors.

The Gold Standard also governs the Safeguarding Principles and Requirements that apply to all certified projects: projects must conduct stakeholder consultations, assess risks to vulnerable groups, and document mitigation measures. These apply across both the GHG and co-benefit tracks, adding a procedural layer to the dual-track substantive requirements.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Gold Standard

The mandatory dual-track reporting requirement (GHG outcomes plus SDG co-benefits) maps to the CROSS multi-dimensional outcome declaration capability. Under CROSS, programs can declare separate outcome indicators for each impact dimension, each with its own eleven-field specification and its own gate test. A Gold Standard-aligned program operating under CROSS would declare, at the entry gate, a distinct outcome indicator for each GHG reduction metric and a separate distinct outcome indicator for each SDG co-benefit being tracked. The gate sequence then applies separately to each dimension: the completion gate evidence must demonstrate progress against the GHG indicators AND against the SDG co-benefit indicators, not simply against an aggregate.

This is consequential because the failure mode Gold Standard is designed to prevent is the substitution of good GHG performance for poor sustainable development performance, or vice versa. CROSS's multi-dimensional outcome declaration, with separate gate tests for each dimension, enforces the same logic structurally: a program cannot pass the completion gate on GHG performance alone if its SDG co-benefit indicators are not met.

The SDG co-benefit quantification requirement maps to the CROSS eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1 through 5: indicator name, operational definition, unit of measurement, baseline value, and target value) applied separately to each co-benefit indicator. The Gold Standard's digital SDG Impact Tool requires that co-benefits be linked to specific SDG targets with specified baselines, measurement approaches, and quantified outcomes. The CROSS eleven-field specification provides the instrument for doing this: each co-benefit is treated as a distinct indicator with its own name, definition, unit, baseline, and target, specified at the entry gate before any implementation begins. The entry gate record becomes the design document against which Gold Standard validation reviews the co-benefit measurement plan.

Gold Standard's ISEAL-backed assurance system maps to the CROSS independent attestation pathway combined with the WALKRI compliance threshold requirement. ISEAL Code of Good Practice requires that the standard's verification process use accredited, independent assessors following documented methodology, and that assessments produce records available for review. The CROSS independent attestation pathway provides the structural framework: when a Gold Standard validator or verifier reviews a CROSS-compliant program's entry gate or completion gate documentation, that review constitutes an independent attestation event at the highest evidence strength tier, with the attestation record held in the Attestation Corpus.

The sequential third-party review structure (validation, verification, certification) maps to the CROSS three evidence strength tiers: self-reported, peer review, and independent attestation. Gold Standard validation (pre-implementation design review) corresponds to CROSS independent attestation at the entry gate. Gold Standard verification (post-monitoring results review) corresponds to CROSS independent attestation at the completion gate. Gold Standard Secretariat certification review corresponds to the cross-gate review that the Attestation Corpus enables: a review of the complete gate history from design through to completion, not merely the terminal result.

The Gold Standard registry maps to the CROSS Attestation Corpus. Both are persistent public records of certified claims. The Gold Standard registry records the outcome of the certification process; the CROSS Attestation Corpus records the complete gate history. Together, they provide the end-to-end public record that Gold Standard's ISEAL compliance requires: design commitments documented at entry, independently validated, monitored, independently verified, and certified, with all records publicly accessible.

| Gold Standard Requirement | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Mandatory dual-track reporting (GHG + SDG co-benefits) | CROSS multi-dimensional outcome declaration (separate indicators and gate tests per dimension) |
| SDG co-benefit quantification with baselines and targets | CROSS eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1-5) per co-benefit indicator |
| ISEAL-backed assurance; independent validation | CROSS independent attestation pathway + WALKRI compliance threshold specification |
| Sequential third-party review (validation, verification, certification) | CROSS three evidence strength tiers across entry and completion gates |
| Gold Standard registry (persistent public record) | CROSS Attestation Corpus (complete gate history; publicly queryable) |
| Safeguarding Principles and Requirements | CROSS conflict of interest provisions + WALKRI evidence independence requirement |
| Digital SDG Impact Tool (mandatory from August 2025) | CROSS entry gate co-benefit indicator specification (structured field inputs per SDG target) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI ensures that each outcome dimension in a Gold Standard-aligned program has intake fields functioning as operationally defined measurement instruments, not narrative description fields. In a dual-track program, this means that GHG reduction intake fields and SDG co-benefit intake fields each have separately specified operational definitions, evidence forms, and compliance thresholds. The co-benefit fields in particular require WALKRI treatment: the transition from paper-based to digital SDG Impact Tool reporting (mandatory from August 2025) imposes a structured data format on co-benefit claims, and WALKRI-compliant intake design ensures that the data collected in the field is in a form that can be submitted through that tool without transformation.

WALKRI's compliance threshold requirement also addresses a specific Gold Standard validation challenge: validators must assess whether the project's monitoring plan will produce evidence adequate for verification. A WALKRI-compliant monitoring plan specifies, for each co-benefit indicator, what data will be collected, in what form, with what chain of custody, and against what compliance threshold. This gives the Gold Standard validator a structured, assessable monitoring design rather than a narrative description, reducing validation uncertainty and increasing the probability that verification will find the monitoring data adequate. WALKRI converts the Gold Standard's dual-track substantive requirements into structured intake design specifications, making co-benefit claims traceable from certification back to the original intake instrument.
