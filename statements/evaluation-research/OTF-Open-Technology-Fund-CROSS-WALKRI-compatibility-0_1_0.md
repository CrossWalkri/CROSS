# Open Technology Fund Compatibility - CROSS+WALKRI

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

This statement documents structural alignment between the Open Technology Fund (OTF) and the CROSS+WALKRI specification standards. OTF is an independent nonprofit grantee of the U.S. Agency for Global Media (USAGM) operating under the statutory authority of 22 U.S. Code § 6208a (the OTF Authorization Act, codified in 2020). The statute mandates support for fully open-source tools, code, and components "to the extent practicable" and requires that supported technologies undergo comprehensive security audits against periodically updated procedures reflecting current industry security standards. OTF operates several program lines (Internet Freedom Fund, Free and Open Source Software Sustainability Fund, Rapid Response Fund, Surge and Sustain Fund, and others) and assesses applications against published criteria including remit fit, cost-effectiveness, sustainability, non-duplication, and complementarity with existing efforts. Successful applicants are paired with an OTF program manager who oversees monitoring and evaluation against predetermined and agreed-upon metrics, deliverables, and goals laid out in the project proposal.

The framework's compatibility with CROSS+WALKRI carries two explicit edge conditions. First, the published evaluation criteria are eligibility and selection criteria, not per-grantee outcome indicators; they specify which projects qualify rather than what each grantee must demonstrate at completion. Second, the monitoring and evaluation model is grantee-and-program-manager negotiated rather than centrally specified across the portfolio, which produces consistent oversight at the project level but does not generate a single portfolio-wide outcome measurement standard. The framework qualifies for a compatibility statement nonetheless because (1) the statutory open-source and security-audit requirements function as CROSS-conformant Access Conditions for the output production Public Benefit Mechanism; (2) the published criteria are stable, publicly accessible, and applied across programs; and (3) the program manager M&E architecture, with its commitment to predetermined and agreed-upon metrics in the project proposal, is structurally a CROSS Part IV build-obligation gate sequence with a pre-award indicator confirmation phase between selection and disbursement.

## The OTF Framework

OTF operates as an independent nonprofit corporation since November 2019 and receives annual congressional appropriations through USAGM. Its statutory basis is 22 U.S. Code § 6208a, which articulates the purpose of advancing freedom of the press and unrestricted access to the internet in repressive environments, and which sets the open-source and security-audit conditions under which OTF funds may be deployed. The framework has four structural components that are load-bearing for a CROSS+WALKRI mapping.

**Statutory open-source mandate.** The Authorization Act requires that OTF support "fully open-source tools, code, and components, to the extent practicable" and that "any such tools, components, code, or technology supported by the Open Technology Fund remain fully open-source, to the extent practicable." The phrase "to the extent practicable" appears twice, creating a default toward open source with a narrowly scoped exception pathway for cases where full open-sourcing is operationally infeasible. The condition applies at the output level: it is a statutory requirement on the artifact produced with OTF funds, not only on the application narrative.

**Statutory security-audit mandate.** The statute requires that supported technologies "undergo comprehensive security audits" and that OTF "periodically review and update security auditing procedures... to reflect current industry security standards." OTF's published record confirms that this requirement is operationalised: the organisation funds third-party audits for code-related projects it supports and has documented audits across more than thirty projects identifying hundreds of vulnerabilities. The audit requirement is a published standard of evidence quality on the deliverable, not a generic encouragement.

**Published evaluation criteria.** OTF assesses applications against a stable set of published criteria including: fit with OTF's remit; whether the proposal solves an existing problem or pre-empts a rising one; cost-effectiveness, with budgets justified and reasonable for the scope of work; sustainability, meaning whether the project will endure beyond OTF's support; and non-duplication and complementarity with existing efforts. The Advisory Council and other technical reviewers apply these criteria in an independent review process. The criteria operate as round-level eligibility and selection filters rather than per-grantee outcome indicators.

**Program manager monitoring and evaluation.** Successful applicants are paired with an OTF program manager who oversees project monitoring and evaluation for the duration of the contract. The program manager M&E model is grounded in predetermined and agreed-upon metrics, deliverables, and goals laid out by the applicant in the project proposal. This produces a per-grantee measurement specification negotiated at the start of the project rather than a portfolio-wide standardised indicator set. The Internet Freedom Fund's published staging structure (ideation, development, deployment) gives this negotiation a stage-aware frame: ideation-stage grantees produce applied research; development-stage grantees produce prototypes and infrastructure; deployment-stage grantees produce ecosystem-strengthening and capacity-building outputs.

OTF also operates the Rapid Response Fund, which provides emergency support to journalists, media outlets, and human rights defenders facing acute digital attacks, with open agreements that pre-position digital security audits, urgent risk mitigation, organisational security improvements, forensic analysis, and secure web hosting. The Rapid Response Fund's contracting model and obligation structure differ from the Internet Freedom Fund's project-grant model and require separate treatment within the gate architecture mapping below.

## How CROSS Satisfies the OTF Framework

CROSS satisfies the OTF framework at four layers: obligation mode, public benefit mechanism and access condition, development stage, and gate architecture.

**Obligation mode: predominantly Build, with stage-aware coherence.** Most OTF-funded projects produce a falsifiable deliverable: a circumvention tool release, a security audit report and remediation, a research paper documenting censorship mechanisms, a deployed ecosystem-strengthening program. CROSS Part III's Build obligation mode is the operative mode across these. The Theory of Build is the correct and complete specification: an application that names a falsifiable deliverable with independently verifiable completion criteria satisfies the CROSS entry specification gate in build mode. Where a deployment-stage Internet Freedom Fund grant proposes to produce a measurable shift in a defined population's secure-access conditions, the round configuration may select Change obligation for that grant specifically; this is consistent with CROSS Part III's mixed and staged modes provision.

**Public benefit mechanism: Output production. Access condition: SPDX-identified open-source license at the time of delivery, plus completed security audit against current standards.** OTF's public goods claim rests on the existence and accessibility of the funded artifact under an open-source license, paired with the audit-confirmed security posture of the artifact. The output production mechanism type in CROSS Part II and in the Public Benefit Mechanism primitive (CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 3) is the direct match. The statutory open-source mandate is operationally an Access Condition declaration: a CROSS-conformant OTF round configuration publishes the accepted SPDX license identifiers, the location of the license file required in the repository, and the time at which the license must be in place (at delivery, not only at application). CROSS Part IV's access condition provision was written to accept exactly this kind of statutory requirement: it requires the SPDX identifier and the file location, and states that a future licensing commitment without a current license does not satisfy the access condition at the entry gate where the artifact already exists.

The security-audit mandate extends the access condition with a second evidence form. A CROSS-conformant OTF round configuration declares the security audit as a completion gate condition: the audit must be performed by a named independent party against the periodically updated procedures, with the audit report and remediation evidence linked from the completion gate submission. This is the structural form CROSS uses to bind external standard references to gate criteria with specified conformance thresholds (Part IV).

**Development stage: Internet Freedom Fund stages map directly to CROSS Stage 1 through Stage 3.** The Internet Freedom Fund's published staging (ideation, development, deployment) maps onto the CROSS Development Stage primitive (Primitives Foundation, Layer 3, Layer 2 placement varies by version). Ideation-stage grants correspond to Stage 1 (proof of concept) where output evidence of the existing research artifact is required at completion. Development-stage grants correspond to Stage 2 (early adoption) where usage evidence from at least one named external user is required at completion, with prototype releases functioning as the artifact under independent test. Deployment-stage grants correspond to Stage 3 (growth) where independently verifiable adoption data with a trend is required at completion. The Surge and Sustain Fund corresponds to Stage 4 (established infrastructure), with the obligation object being maintained operation at declared service standards rather than a new deliverable. The Rapid Response Fund operates under a distinct retroactive-or-contracted services structure addressed separately below.

**Gate architecture: project proposal as entry specification; agreed-upon metrics negotiation as pre-award indicator confirmation; periodic progress reporting as progress verification; final report plus audit completion as completion verification.** OTF's application process and program manager M&E model map cleanly onto the CROSS Part IV gate architecture. The project proposal, which contains the deliverables and metrics the applicant commits to, is the Entry Specification Gate submission. The pre-award negotiation between applicant and program manager, in which predetermined and agreed-upon metrics are finalised, maps directly onto CROSS's Pre-Award Indicator Confirmation window: a structured pre-disbursement opportunity to surface indicator specification problems before work begins and to issue a written confirmation that binds subsequent gate assessments. CROSS's pre-award confirmation provision was written to do exactly this work. Progress reports submitted to the program manager are Progress Verification Gates with output evidence at minimum. The final report, combined with the completed security audit and the publicly accessible open-source release, is the Completion Verification Gate, summative in character. For build-obligation rounds, CROSS Part IV requires confirmation that the specified deliverable is publicly accessible before final payment is released; this is precisely the form the OTF completion process already takes for code-producing grants.

**The Rapid Response Fund: contracted-service architecture rather than grant architecture.** The Rapid Response Fund provides pre-positioned services through open agreements with partners (security auditors, forensic analysts, secure hosts) rather than awarding open-call grants. The obligation object is the service delivered to the affected party (a journalist, media outlet, or human rights defender), and the verifier is the affected party combined with the contracted service provider. Under CROSS, this is a Build obligation round with the deliverable defined as the service event, the access provision public benefit mechanism active (rather than output production), and an Affected Population Verification Gate configured as the completion verification mechanism (CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation, Layer 4). The Affected Population Verification Gate primitive was added in version 0.1.4 specifically to address the case where independent review by a technically qualified party would not satisfy the gate without structured input from the affected population; the Rapid Response Fund context is a direct application.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

OTF's published criteria are the primary case where WALKRI's contribution is operative. Each criterion is a measurement instrument in waiting: an applicant asked whether their project is "cost-effective" or "sustainable" or "non-duplicative" cannot answer in a way that supports cross-applicant comparison without an operational definition of each term, a unit of measurement, and an evidence form that confirms the claim. The published OTF criteria are conceptual statements rather than measurement specifications; applied as intake fields, they require the five-element WALKRI specification before they function as instruments rather than labels.

The sustainability criterion, applied as a WALKRI-conformant field, would require a criterion intent statement (what sustainability measures, and why), an operational definition (does sustainability mean continued maintenance by the named team, continued community contribution after the team departs, continued operation of deployed infrastructure, or continued funding from non-OTF sources; over what horizon; from what evidence base), a response form (declared sustainability stance, named successor mechanism, named complementary funder, or named maintainer roster), an evidence form (the artifact that confirms the claim, with a public source where possible), and a conformance threshold (the minimum standard above which the sustainability claim is satisfied). The CROSS Sustainability Stance primitive (Primitives Foundation, Layer 6) provides the response form vocabulary directly: sustained, conditional, or dependent, with the dependent stance recognised as a disclosure rather than a disqualifier. A WALKRI-conformant OTF sustainability field would adopt this three-state structure, declare the conditions under which a conditional stance is satisfied, and specify what evidence supports each declared state.

The non-duplication and complementarity criterion, applied as a WALKRI-conformant field, would require the applicant to name the existing efforts that the proposed work could duplicate or complement, identify the differential function this work performs that the existing efforts do not, and provide evidence from sources outside the applicant's control confirming that the cited existing efforts have the function described. Without this structure, "non-duplicative and complementary" is a claim no applicant ever fails because no applicant ever defines what it would mean to fail. With WALKRI's conformance threshold, the criterion becomes assessable: a project that cannot name the field it claims to complement, or that mischaracterises an existing effort's scope, has surfaced a misrepresentation in the criterion that the gate assessment must address.

The cost-effectiveness criterion is the case where WALKRI's evidence form requirement is most consequential. A budget being "justified and reasonable for the scope of work" is not a measurement; it is a judgement frame. The WALKRI-conformant version asks the applicant to specify the cost per declared output (cost per audited tool, cost per research output, cost per population served), to declare the reference class against which that cost is benchmarked (other OTF grantees at the same stage, public-sector procurement of equivalent services, market price of equivalent contracted work), and to provide the evidence base for that reference class. The conformance threshold then becomes the cost level above which the project must affirmatively justify the variance rather than the level at which it is presumptively reasonable.

The Internet Freedom Fund's staging structure benefits from WALKRI's evidence form requirement applied to the stage declaration itself. An applicant who declares ideation-stage but cites prior usage of a built tool by external parties is either misrepresenting the stage or understating it; the discrepancy is detectable only if the stage field carries an evidence form requirement specifying what artifact confirms the stage. The CROSS Development Stage primitive specifies the evidence types appropriate to each stage; WALKRI's contribution is to require that the stage declaration field collect that evidence at intake, before the program manager negotiation begins, so the negotiation has an evidence-grounded starting point.

Finally, the security-audit completion condition benefits from WALKRI's conformance threshold for external standard references. The statutory phrase "current industry security standards" cannot be a gate criterion in itself; it requires an operational specification of which standards are currently accepted, at what version, with which auditor categories qualified to assess against them, and what level of remediation completion satisfies the gate. A WALKRI-conformant OTF round configuration would publish this specification before the round opens. The CROSS Part IV requirement for conformance threshold language ("which components apply, what evidence satisfies each, and what the minimum threshold for passage is") is the structural form this publication takes.
