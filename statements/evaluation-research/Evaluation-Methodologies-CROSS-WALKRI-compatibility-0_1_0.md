# Evaluation Methodologies (Outcome Harvesting, MSC, Developmental Evaluation, Realist Evaluation) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

CROSS+WALKRI is compatible with all four of these widely used evaluation methodologies because CROSS specifies what must be demonstrated at each gate rather than prescribing how evidence is produced. Outcome Harvesting, Most Significant Change, Developmental Evaluation, and Realist Evaluation each have published practitioner guides and distinct epistemological commitments; they differ from one another at the level of method, not at the level of gate-deliverable. WALKRI's pre-publication audit ensures that whichever methodology a program uses, the evidence it produces will be collected against well-specified measurement surfaces rather than ambiguous prompts.

These four methodologies are among the most frequently specified in grant evaluation plans in the international development, social innovation, and philanthropic sectors. They appear in evaluation frameworks published by USAID, the UK Foreign Commonwealth and Development Office, the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, and numerous bilateral development agencies. A CROSS-conformant program may specify any of these methodologies in its evaluation design without modification to its gate architecture; the methodology determines how evidence is gathered, while CROSS determines what evidence must be present at each gate.

## The Four Methodologies

**Outcome Harvesting** (Wilson-Grau and Britt, 2012; practitioner guide at betterevaluation.org) works backward from observed changes in social actors. Rather than measuring progress against pre-specified targets, Outcome Harvesting identifies "outcome statements" describing behavioral changes in individuals, groups, organizations, or institutions, then verifies the program's contribution to those changes. The methodology is particularly suited to complex and emergent contexts where pre-specifying outcomes in advance would foreclose attention to unexpected or indirect change.

**Most Significant Change** (Davies and Dart, 2005; practitioner guide at betterevaluation.org) is a ten-step participatory process in which program staff, participants, and stakeholders collect stories of significant change within specified domains. Domain collectors then deliberate to select the most significant story from each domain; selections are passed up organizational levels until a final selection is made. The process requires no numeric metrics; it is explicitly designed to surface qualitative change evidence through structured participatory deliberation.

**Developmental Evaluation** (Patton, 2010; Guilford Press) positions the evaluator as an embedded thought partner to social innovators working in complex and emergent environments. Rather than producing a summative judgment about whether a program worked, Developmental Evaluation produces real-time or near-real-time feedback that enables program designers to make adaptive decisions as their intervention evolves. The evaluator's role is ongoing sense-making alongside program staff rather than periodic external assessment.

**Realist Evaluation** (Pawson and Tilley, 1997; SAGE Publications) produces context-mechanism-outcome (CMO) configurations that specify which mechanisms produce which outcomes under which contextual conditions. Rather than asking whether a program works, Realist Evaluation asks what works for whom in what circumstances. CMO configurations are developed through iterative theory testing using both qualitative and quantitative methods, and they accumulate across evaluations to build generalizable knowledge about causal mechanisms.

## How CROSS Satisfies These Evaluation Methodologies

The compatibility between CROSS and these four methodologies follows from a single structural fact: CROSS specifies gate deliverables, not evidence production methods. This distinction is the foundation of all four mappings below.

| Methodology | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Outcome Harvesting | Completion gate evidence requirement; backward-looking discovery maps to forward-specified indicator | Full satisfaction. In Outcome Harvesting, the evaluator identifies outcome statements after they have occurred and then verifies the program's contribution. In a CROSS-conformant program, the completion gate specifies what outcome-level evidence must be present; Outcome Harvesting is the method by which that evidence is gathered. The harvested outcome statement IS the completion gate evidence, mapped against the pre-specified indicator. The backward-looking discovery method and the forward-specified requirement are not in tension; they operate at different analytical levels. |
| Most Significant Change | CROSS evidence flexibility: qualitative narrative accepted as evidence form; WALKRI artifact type specification does not require numeric format | Full satisfaction. CROSS accepts qualitative narrative as an evidence form. WALKRI's evidence form field specifies artifact type (story document, deliberation record, selection rationale) without requiring numeric formats. An MSC story that has been through the domain collector deliberation process and produced a selection rationale is a well-specified artifact that satisfies a CROSS completion gate evidence requirement configured to accept qualitative narrative. No modification to the MSC process is required. |
| Developmental Evaluation | Progress verification gate architecture: designed for intermediate check-ins during implementation; developmental gate character | Full satisfaction. CROSS's progress verification gate is structurally aligned with Developmental Evaluation's real-time feedback function. The developmental gate character within CROSS is designed precisely for the intermediate assessment of direction of change rather than target attainment. An embedded Developmental Evaluation produces the evidence that populates progress verification gates; the gate structure gives that evidence a formal decision moment rather than allowing it to remain informal practitioner insight. |
| Realist Evaluation | CROSS Theory of Change hierarchy: Outcome field, coherence disclosure and population scope, Activity and Output declarations | Full satisfaction. The CMO configuration produced by Realist Evaluation maps directly onto the CROSS ToC hierarchy. Outcome corresponds to the Outcome field. Context corresponds to the coherence disclosure (which contextual conditions bound the program's theory of change) and the population scope (for whom the mechanism is claimed to operate). Mechanism corresponds to the Activity and Output declarations in the CROSS ToC, which specify how the program produces change rather than merely asserting that it does. A program using Realist Evaluation can populate its CROSS ToC with CMO configurations at the appropriate level of the hierarchy. |

**Result:** All four methodologies are fully compatible with CROSS+WALKRI. The compatibility is not coincidental; it follows from CROSS's decision to specify what must be demonstrated rather than how evidence is produced. A grant program that specifies any of these methodologies in its evaluation plan and adopts CROSS+WALKRI as its documentation architecture does not face a conflict between the methodology and the standard.

This compatibility also has implications for multi-method evaluation designs. Programs that combine Outcome Harvesting for complex pathway evidence with Most Significant Change for participatory standing, or that use Developmental Evaluation during implementation and Realist Evaluation for cross-program synthesis, can accommodate the outputs of all methodologies within a single CROSS gate architecture. Each gate event draws on whichever methodology is most appropriate to the program's current stage and evidentiary needs.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication specification requirement ensures that evaluators using any of these four methodologies receive well-specified measurement surfaces rather than ambiguous prompts, making the evidence produced by each methodology more comparable and trustworthy. This function is equally important for all four methodologies and operates independently of how different the methodologies are from one another.

The problem WALKRI addresses is consistent across methodologies: if the intake fields or gate criteria against which evidence is assessed are poorly specified, then the quality of evidence produced by even the most rigorous methodology is difficult to evaluate. An Outcome Harvesting process that produces outcome statements against a vague criterion produces outcome statements that are difficult to assess. An MSC process whose domain definitions are unclear produces stories that cannot be compared across domains. A Developmental Evaluation whose progress gate criteria are unspecified produces feedback that cannot be linked to a formal determination. A Realist Evaluation whose CMO scope is not bounded in the CROSS ToC produces configurations that cannot be mapped to gate requirements.

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements address all of these problems at source. Criterion intent specifies what the measurement surface is trying to detect. Operational definition bounds the criterion so that evaluators and programs share a common understanding of its scope. Response form specifies the format in which evidence should be provided. Evidence form specifies what artifact constitutes acceptable evidence, accommodating the distinct artifact types that each methodology produces: outcome statement documents, MSC story collections, developmental feedback summaries, CMO configuration documents. Conformance threshold specifies the minimum standard.

For programs using any of these four methodologies, WALKRI conformance means that the measurement surfaces the methodology encounters are as well-specified as the methodology itself. The resulting evidence base is more reliable, more comparable across programs and periods, and more resistant to the retrospective redefinition of what would have counted as success.
