# COPE Ethical Guidelines for Peer Reviewers and IDEAS Competencies for Development Evaluation

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

This statement covers two professional-society standards that set binding conduct rules for evaluators and reviewers without specifying what grantees must measure or report: the Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) Ethical Guidelines for Peer Reviewers, and the International Development Evaluation Association (IDEAS) Competencies for Development Evaluation. They are treated as a joint compatibility entry because their structural function in the evaluation field is parallel. Both are published by professional bodies whose membership constitutes the relevant practitioner community. Both bind the assessor rather than the assessed. Both presume that downstream measurement frameworks exist and that their own contribution is to the independence and verifiable conduct of the people interpreting those measurements. CROSS+WALKRI satisfies the structural requirements both standards impose by treating evaluator independence, conflict of interest, and reviewer competency as architectural conditions of conformant gate operation rather than as supplementary professional obligations.

The pairing follows the precedent of the IFAD-RMF-RIMS-IPAF joint compatibility statement: consolidated coverage of multiple instruments within a single coherent professional architecture is preferred to statement proliferation when the instruments share structural function. COPE and IDEAS together with the AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards (covered separately) constitute the professional-evaluation-standards cluster within Part XII. AEA/JCSEE specifies the properties an evaluation must have. COPE specifies the ethical conduct required of peer reviewers. IDEAS specifies the competencies an evaluator must hold. None specifies grantee MEL; all three specify evaluator and reviewer properties. The same structural basis on which AEA was admitted to Part XII applies to COPE and IDEAS.

## What COPE and IDEAS Are

COPE, the Committee on Publication Ethics, is the principal standard-setter for publication ethics in scholarly communication. Its membership exceeds thirteen thousand journals, publishers, universities, and research institutions worldwide. COPE's Ethical Guidelines for Peer Reviewers (current version 2) specify the conduct expected of any party who evaluates the work of others under conditions of asymmetric information and confidential access. The guidelines address core obligations (timeliness, competence, confidentiality, constructive feedback) and structural duties (declaring conflicts of interest, declining when appropriate, not exploiting privileged access to advance personal interests, reporting suspected misconduct). COPE itself does not enforce the guidelines; member organizations adopt them as their own editorial and review policies, and the guidelines accordingly function as the de facto baseline of peer review ethics across English-language scholarly publishing. COPE's own guidance acknowledges that grant peer review is covered by the same ethical framework as journal peer review: the structural relationship between reviewer and reviewed is sufficiently similar across the two contexts that a separate framework is not required.

IDEAS, the International Development Evaluation Association, is the global professional society for development evaluation. Its membership spans more than one hundred and ten countries. The IDEAS Competencies for Development Evaluation (2012) specify the knowledge, skills, and dispositions an evaluator working in international development contexts is expected to hold. The competencies are organized into four domains: professional foundations (ethics, evaluation theory, knowledge of the development context), technical evaluation skills (design, data collection, analysis, reporting), management of evaluation (planning, budgeting, team management, quality assurance), and interpersonal competencies (communication, cultural awareness, conflict management). The IDEAS Competencies are explicitly designed as a professional development framework rather than a measurement framework: they specify what an evaluator must be capable of, not what a program must demonstrate. Adoption is by professional self-identification through the IDEAS membership structure and by reference in evaluator training programs and qualification schemes operated by member societies in their respective countries.

Both standards share four structural properties that distinguish them from grantee MEL frameworks. First, the obligation runs to the assessor, not to the assessed: a journal reviewer or a development evaluator must hold the property the standard specifies; the author or grantee being assessed is not subject to the standard. Second, the standard is published by a professional society whose membership constitutes the community of practice: adoption is by professional affiliation rather than by legal or contractual obligation. Third, the standard presumes that substantive measurement frameworks operate downstream: COPE presumes editorial criteria for the journal in question; IDEAS presumes the measurement instruments of whatever development program the evaluator is assessing. Fourth, the standard's contribution is the infrastructure of independent and verifiable review: it ensures that the people interpreting downstream measurements do so under conditions that make their judgments trustworthy.

These four properties are also the properties of the AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards. The structural argument for admitting AEA to Part XII rests on the recognition that evaluator-side standards are not adjacent to outcome measurement; they are the independent-review layer that makes outcome measurement worth reading. The same argument applies to COPE and IDEAS without modification.

## How CROSS Satisfies COPE's Ethical Guidelines for Peer Reviewers

COPE's guidelines specify reviewer obligations under nine categories: only agreeing to review when qualified and able to deliver on time, declaring competing interests, recognizing the distinction between confidential review communication and public commentary, providing constructive feedback, avoiding unsubstantiated assertions about competing work, declining to exploit privileged access, declining to use confidential review materials for personal benefit, reporting suspected misconduct, and conducting review consistent with the policies of the body that commissioned it.

| COPE Reviewer Obligation | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Only review when qualified and able to deliver on time | CROSS Part VII reviewer assignment provisions; gate configuration must publish reviewer qualification requirements before round opens | Full satisfaction. Reviewer qualification is treated as a published gate configuration parameter, not a discretionary editorial judgment. Reviewers self-decline against the published criterion. |
| Declare competing interests | CROSS Part VII Conflict of Interest tier structure (categorical bar, qualified waiver, disclosure-encouraged); declaration is a pre-condition for review, not a supplementary disclosure | Full satisfaction. The COPE expectation that competing interests be declared is implemented in CROSS as a structural pre-condition for reviewer assignment rather than a reliance on individual reviewer conscience. |
| Distinguish confidential review communication from public commentary | CROSS Part IV gate record legibility provisions: review communications operate within the published gate configuration; reviewer notes are part of the assessment record, not public attribution | Full satisfaction. The boundary between confidential review and public commentary is applied through gate record structure rather than reviewer self-policing. |
| Provide constructive feedback | WALKRI calibration record requirement (Section 3.5); reviewer assessment must be grounded in the published conformance threshold rather than personal judgment | Full satisfaction. Constructive feedback is operationalized as feedback referenced to a published threshold, not as a generic professional norm. |
| Avoid unsubstantiated assertions about competing work | CROSS Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle (incorporated by reference from the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite); reviewer assertions about competing or adjacent work must be sourced from public evidence | Full satisfaction. Reviewer claims about competing work are subject to the same evidence sourcing requirements as applicant claims. |
| Decline to exploit privileged access | CROSS Part VII Tier 1 categorical bar provisions extend to any reviewer who would derive material benefit from access to a specific application's confidential content | Full satisfaction. Exploitation of privileged access is treated as a Tier 1 conflict requiring reviewer replacement, not a residual ethical risk. |
| Decline to use confidential materials for personal benefit | CROSS Part IV gate record provisions; review materials are scoped to the assessment record and not portable to other contexts | Full satisfaction. The constraint is applied through the gate architecture rather than relying on reviewer self-restraint. |
| Report suspected misconduct | CROSS Part XI funder obligation provisions and Block J material discrepancy investigation procedure | Full satisfaction. Reviewer reports of suspected misconduct activate the Block J investigation procedure; this is a published procedure with a named authority rather than an ad hoc escalation. |
| Conduct review consistent with commissioning body's policies | CROSS Part IV pre-publication requirement for full gate configuration; reviewers operate against the published configuration | Full satisfaction. Reviewer conduct is bound to the published gate configuration; the configuration is the commissioning body's policy in operational form. |

The structural pattern is that COPE's reviewer obligations are implemented in CROSS as architectural conditions of conformant gate operation. A reviewer assigned to a CROSS-conformant gate operates within a published configuration that already implements the COPE expectations as structural constraints. Conformance is not a matter of the reviewer's individual ethical disposition; it is a property of the gate architecture they operate within. This is a stronger guarantee than reliance on professional ethical self-policing, which COPE itself acknowledges is the limit of what an editorial society can provide.

The Conflict of Interest mapping deserves elaboration. COPE treats competing interest declaration as a reviewer obligation that activates whenever a competing interest exists. CROSS Part VII implements the same expectation through a three-tier structure that classifies the competing interest at the point of reviewer assignment: Tier 1 conflicts (financial interest, employment relationship, familial relationship) are categorical bars admitting no waiver; Tier 2 conflicts (prior collaboration, public advocacy, prior funder-grantee relationship) require disclosure and qualified waiver subject to program administrator approval; Tier 3 conflicts (professional acquaintance, network overlap) are disclosure-encouraged under a reasonable third-party standard. The classification is not a reviewer judgment; it is a published rule applied at the assignment stage. COPE's expectation that reviewers decline when competing interests would compromise review integrity is operationalized as a structural exclusion before assignment rather than a self-declaration after assignment.

## How CROSS Satisfies the IDEAS Competencies for Development Evaluation

The IDEAS Competencies are not a measurement framework; they are a description of what an evaluator must be capable of in order to conduct a verifiable development evaluation. The competency framework is structured around four domains: professional foundations, technical evaluation skills, management of evaluation, and interpersonal competencies. CROSS does not specify how an evaluator acquires these competencies, which is properly the work of training institutions and professional certification bodies. CROSS specifies the conditions under which an evaluator's work is admissible at a gate, which is the operational complement to the competency framework.

| IDEAS Competency Domain | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Professional foundations (ethics, evaluation theory, development context knowledge) | CROSS Part VII reviewer qualification publication requirement; the round's reviewer qualification declaration names the foundations expected | Full satisfaction. The expectation is operationalized as a published qualification requirement against which prospective reviewers self-assess; reviewers declining the assignment because the qualification exceeds their foundation is the correct outcome. |
| Technical evaluation skills (design, data collection, analysis, reporting) | WALKRI five criterion specification elements (criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, conformance threshold); reviewer is bound to a measurement instrument specified in advance, reducing the technical skill demand required at the review stage | Full satisfaction. Technical evaluation skills are required of evaluators conducting independent evaluation at the highest Evidence Strength level (Layer 4 of the Primitives Foundation); for lower evidence strength levels, the WALKRI instrument specification reduces the skill demand the review imposes on the reviewer. |
| Management of evaluation (planning, budgeting, team management, quality assurance) | CROSS Part XI funder obligation provisions; evaluation management is a funder-side property, not a reviewer-side property; the funder publishes the gate configuration that implements the funder's configuration decisions for the round | Full satisfaction. The IDEAS expectation that evaluations be managed by competent parties is implemented in CROSS as a funder obligation: the funder is the party who configures the gate, publishes the configuration, and is bound to obligation to grantees with a named redress procedure under Part XI. |
| Interpersonal competencies (communication, cultural awareness, conflict management) | CROSS beneficiary engagement and validation dimension (Part II); WALKRI participatory specification element (Section 3.6) | Full satisfaction. Cultural awareness is operationalized in the beneficiary validation dimension as a requirement that claims about a defined population be validated by parties within or proximate to that population. Participatory specification provides the structural form for incorporating beneficiary input into instrument design. |

The IDEAS Competencies' four-domain structure maps to the four CROSS layers that bear on evaluator-side properties. Professional foundations map to the Conflict of Interest primitive (Layer 3) and to the reviewer qualification publication requirement in Part VII. Technical evaluation skills map to the Criterion Specification Elements primitive (Layer 5) operating through WALKRI: a reviewer working against a WALKRI-conformant field specification needs less independent technical skill than a reviewer working against an unstructured criterion, because the instrument carries the methodology. Management of evaluation maps to the funder obligation provisions in Part XI: the IDEAS expectation that evaluations be competently managed is implemented as a funder-side obligation with redress under Part XI rather than as a reviewer-side competency requirement. Interpersonal competencies map to the beneficiary engagement and validation dimension and to the participatory specification element of WALKRI Section 3.6, which is the operational form participatory and culturally aware specification takes.

The Evaluator Independence Requirement primitive (added in parallel to the Primitives Foundation as the structural anchor for evaluator-side standards) provides the layer-level point of reference for IDEAS. The primitive specifies that where evidence strength at a gate is configured at independent review or independent evaluation level, the evaluator must satisfy named competency criteria from a published source. IDEAS is one of the named sources a program may declare as the basis for its evaluator competency criteria; AEA evaluator competencies are another; domain-specific certification bodies are a third. The structural point is that the program names the source rather than relying on undeclared judgment about who qualifies as a competent evaluator.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five criterion specification elements address a structural risk that COPE and IDEAS identify but cannot themselves resolve: the risk that a reviewer or evaluator competent in the general sense conducts a specific review against an instrument so loosely specified that competent judgment cannot produce consistent results. COPE acknowledges this risk in its expectation that reviewers provide constructive feedback grounded in clear criteria; IDEAS acknowledges it in the technical evaluation skills domain. Neither standard specifies what the criteria should look like; both presume that the commissioning body has produced criteria of sufficient quality. WALKRI specifies what sufficient quality means: criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, and conformance threshold, all committed to in writing before a review occurs.

The calibration record requirement (WALKRI Section 3.5) is the structural mechanism by which the COPE expectation of constructive feedback and the IDEAS competency of consistent technical evaluation are operationalized. A conformance threshold paired with a calibration record (two clearly-satisfying examples, two clearly-failing examples, one borderline case with documented resolution) gives a reviewer who did not design the instrument the basis on which to render consistent judgments. Without the calibration record, two equally COPE-conformant and equally IDEAS-competent reviewers may produce divergent assessments of the same submission because the threshold operates differently in each reviewer's head. With the calibration record, the threshold operates the same way in each reviewer's hands, because the threshold has been operationalized rather than left to interpretation.

For the IDEAS Competencies specifically, WALKRI's pre-publication audit shifts the technical evaluation skills demand from the reviewer to the instrument designer. An evaluator working against a WALKRI-conformant instrument set requires less of the technical evaluation skills domain than an evaluator constructing the methodology in real time. This is not a substitution: the IDEAS competencies remain necessary for evaluators conducting Independent Evaluation at the highest evidence strength level, and the WALKRI instrument is the floor below which an evaluator's competency cannot rescue the assessment. The two layers are complementary: WALKRI provides the instrument; IDEAS provides the practitioner; CROSS specifies the gate at which both meet.

## Position Relative to Existing CROSS+WALKRI Coverage

This statement completes the professional-evaluation-standards cluster within Part XII alongside the AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards compatibility statement. The three statements together cover the principal English-language professional-society standards that set binding conduct and competency requirements for evaluators and reviewers: AEA/JCSEE for evaluation properties, COPE for peer review ethics, IDEAS for development evaluation competencies. A program declaring conformance to CROSS+WALKRI and configuring its gates to reference any of the three standards has the structural infrastructure to satisfy the standard as a consequence of conformance.

This statement migrates COPE and IDEAS out of the Partial Coverage Map (Adjacent Frameworks section) where they were previously recorded. The original classification rested on the observation that neither standard specifies grantee MEL. That observation remains correct, but it does not distinguish COPE and IDEAS from AEA/JCSEE, which was admitted to Part XII on the same basis. The structural argument for admitting AEA applies to COPE and IDEAS without modification. The migration corrects an inconsistency in the prior classification and consolidates the professional-evaluation-standards cluster under joint coverage rather than fragmenting it across the Partial Coverage Map and Part XII.

Two cross-references are worth stating explicitly. First, the AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards compatibility statement should be read alongside this one as the third member of the cluster; programs configuring evaluator-side standards should reference the appropriate member of the cluster for the practitioner community their reviewers and evaluators belong to. Second, the Volkswagen Foundation Distributed Peer Review guideline remains in the Partial Coverage Map; it specifies a peer review methodology rather than a peer review ethic, and the structural argument for joining the professional-evaluation-standards cluster does not extend to it without separate analysis.
