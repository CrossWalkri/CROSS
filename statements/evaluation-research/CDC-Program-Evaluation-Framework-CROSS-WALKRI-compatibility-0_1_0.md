# CDC Program Evaluation Framework (2024) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

CROSS+WALKRI satisfies the five federal evaluation standards specified in the CDC Program Evaluation Framework (2024) and supports the framework's six-step process through structural provisions that operate as architectural consequences of conformance. The framework's standards (relevance and utility, rigor, independence and objectivity, transparency, ethics) are derived from OMB Memorandum M-20-12 and are positioned by CDC as a practical, nonprescriptive tool rather than as a compliance instrument. CROSS+WALKRI provides the documentation and measurement architecture against which a CDC-funded program or any other adopter can demonstrate conformance to those standards in a verifiable form.

The 2024 framework is the formal updated successor to the 1999 CDC Framework for Program Evaluation in Public Health. It was published in MMWR Recommendations and Reports on September 26, 2024 (Vol. 73, No. 6, pp. 1-37). It is voluntary in posture at the framework level, but it is incorporated by reference into specific CDC Notices of Funding Opportunity, which makes it operationally grantee-facing for those programs. This compatibility statement therefore matters in two registers: as a reference standard for the wider evaluation community working in or alongside US public health funding, and as the effective evaluation specification for grantees of CDC programs that cite the framework in their NOFO terms.

---

## The CDC Program Evaluation Framework's Approach

The 2024 framework is organized as a six-step iterative process for planning, conducting, and using evaluations, paired with five federal evaluation standards that apply across all steps. The framework is described by CDC as "a practical, nonprescriptive tool" and as "intentionally broad to give evaluators and interest holders the flexibility to consider their unique circumstances." Its function is to specify what evaluations must address rather than to mandate specific methods.

The six steps are: assess context (the setting, interest holders, place, and evaluation capacity in which the program operates); describe the program (narrative and logic model statements of activities and intended outcomes); focus the evaluation questions and design (the purpose, intended users, evaluation questions, and overall design approach); gather credible evidence (data collection methods, indicators, measures, and data sources, with evidence that is "reliable, valid, authentic, and informative for their intended use"); generate and support conclusions (analysis, interpretation, and recommendations that are "clearly worded, provide multiple potential options for action, are in alignment with potential users' roles and responsibilities, and are feasible to implement"); and act on findings (dissemination and implementation of changes informed by the evaluation).

The five federal evaluation standards apply across all six steps. Relevance and utility require that evaluations address questions of importance and produce findings that are actionable, timely, and presented in ways that are understandable and culturally responsive. Rigor requires that evaluations produce findings interest holders can confidently rely on, using the most appropriate design and methods. Independence and objectivity require that evaluations be insulated from political and other undue influences. Transparency requires that evaluation activities be transparent in planning, implementation, and reporting, with clear documentation of decisions. Ethics requires that evaluations be conducted to the highest ethical standards and safeguard the dignity, rights, safety, and privacy of participants.

The framework is anchored in the Foundations for Evidence-Based Policymaking Act of 2018, which the framework describes as elevating program evaluation as a critical agency function. The federal standards themselves come from OMB Memorandum M-20-12, the Phase 4 implementation guidance for the Evidence Act. Adopting the CDC framework therefore aligns a program not only with CDC's expectations for evaluation quality but with the broader federal evidence-building infrastructure that M-20-12 establishes.

## How CROSS Satisfies the CDC Program Evaluation Framework

| CDC Standard / Step | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Relevance and Utility | Pre-commitment requirement: evaluation criteria and question list published before submissions arrive; gate outputs structured for funder decision gates; plain-language applicant-facing summary required before round opens | Full satisfaction. CROSS's pre-round publication requirements (gate configuration, plain-language summary, full application question list) ensure that what an evaluation will assess is known to the people it is meant to inform before they engage with it. Findings produced at each gate are structured to feed the funder's decision points rather than to accumulate as a separate evaluation deliverable. |
| Rigor | WALKRI five pre-publication field requirements; CROSS evidence scope ladder (output, usage, outcome, impact) calibrated by the Coordination Scaling Standard; explicit causality stance declaration | Full satisfaction. The evidence scope ladder calibrates rigor to the claim being made: small-team output claims require output evidence, population-change claims require outcome or impact evidence with appropriate causal warrant. The causality stance primitive forces an explicit choice between contribution and attribution claims, which is the most common rigor failure in program evaluation. |
| Independence and Objectivity | Part VII conflict of interest provisions; Block J discrepancy procedure; Attestation Corpus query as funder-side independent verification | Full satisfaction. CROSS Part VII specifies the structural conditions under which a reviewer or attestor is disqualified, requires disclosure, or is permitted to participate with disclosure. Independence is implemented through structural conditions on reviewer eligibility rather than through individual evaluator judgment. The Attestation Corpus query introduces an independent factual basis for prior-history claims that does not depend on either the applicant's or the funder's account. |
| Transparency | Pre-round publication of full question list, gate configuration, and plain-language summary; Attestation Corpus as queryable record of gate determinations; published completion gate criteria with named determination authority | Full satisfaction. Every element of the evaluation surface (questions, criteria, evidence forms, conformance thresholds, gate determination authority) is publicly published before the round opens and remains accessible for the duration of the round. Gate determinations are recorded as verifiable artifacts. The transparency standard is implemented at the artifact level rather than as a reporting promise. |
| Ethics | Privacy and data sensitivity dimension; affected population verification gate; adverse signal disclosure including unintended outcomes at completion; revenue architecture and additionality disclosure protecting against extractive use of public funds | Full satisfaction. CROSS's privacy and data sensitivity dimension provides for privacy-preserving measurement where standard analytics would compromise named-population member safety. The unintended outcomes disclosure at the completion gate forces an explicit account of negative effects on populations adjacent to the target population. These provisions implement the ethics standard's requirement to safeguard the dignity, rights, safety, and privacy of participants. |
| Step 1 (Assess Context) | Organizational identity declaration; sufficiency architecture declaration; portfolio context statement; development stage declaration | Full satisfaction. The four-field organizational identity declaration and the sufficiency architecture declaration produce the contextual record the framework's first step requires, with corroborating evidence from sources outside the applicant's control. |
| Step 2 (Describe the Program) | Theory of Change hierarchy: Outcome field, Activity and Output declarations, coherence disclosure, population scope | Full satisfaction. CROSS's ToC hierarchy is a logic model in formal specification rather than diagrammatic form. Activities and outputs are declared at the appropriate layer; population scope and coherence disclosure bound the theory to its claimed context. |
| Step 3 (Focus Evaluation Questions and Design) | Obligation mode declaration (build, change, retroactive); gate architecture configuration; entry specification gate content scoped to declared mode | Full satisfaction. The obligation mode is the framework's "evaluation purpose" decision, declared by the funder before the round opens. The gate architecture is the evaluation design, declared in publishable form. Intended users (applicants, reviewers, beneficiaries, funder) are addressed by the corresponding rights and obligations specified in each part of the standard. |
| Step 4 (Gather Credible Evidence) | Evidence scope ladder; evidence strength ladder; evidence type declaration; WALKRI five-element field specification; data quality standards primitive | Full satisfaction. The evidence scope and strength ladders specify what verifiable evidence consists of in calibrated form. The data quality standards primitive specifies the reliability, validity, and authenticity attributes the framework requires of verifiable evidence. |
| Step 5 (Generate and Support Conclusions) | Documented gate determination by named authority; explicit evaluation reasoning recorded in the gate record; published completion criteria against which the determination is made | Full satisfaction. CROSS requires that completion gate determinations be made by a named authority against published criteria, with the basis for the determination recorded. This is the structural implementation of the framework's requirement that conclusions be generated and supported. |
| Step 6 (Act on Findings) | Continuation gate architecture; obligation fulfillment record; portfolio-level continuation benchmark; multi-cycle retrospective assessment | Full satisfaction. The continuation gate is the formal mechanism through which findings from one round affect funding decisions in subsequent rounds. The obligation fulfillment record carries forward findings from prior rounds into the next entry specification. The portfolio-level continuation benchmark and multi-cycle retrospective assessment provide funder-level structures for acting on findings across programs and over time. |

**Result:** CROSS satisfies all five federal evaluation standards and supports all six steps of the CDC Program Evaluation Framework as structural consequences of conformance. No supplementary evaluation architecture is required to achieve alignment.

The relationship to the framework's nonprescriptive posture deserves elaboration. CDC describes the framework as a practical, nonprescriptive tool that gives evaluators and interest holders flexibility to consider their unique circumstances. CROSS specifies what must be demonstrated at each gate rather than prescribing how evidence is produced; this is the same posture. A program that adopts CROSS retains the methodological flexibility the framework preserves: it may use any evaluation methodology compatible with the declared obligation mode and the evidence scope required at each gate. CROSS does not constrain the methods; it constrains the deliverables. This is why the AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards (voluntary and professional in character) and the CDC framework (voluntary and federal in character) are both satisfiable under the same conformant architecture: both standards specify properties of evaluations rather than methods.

The relevance to CDC NOFO recipients deserves separate note. Where a CDC NOFO incorporates the framework by reference, the framework becomes operationally grantee-facing for that program even though it remains nonprescriptive at the framework level. A CROSS-conformant grantee in such a program can demonstrate framework conformance directly from their CROSS gate records without producing parallel framework-specific documentation. The CROSS evidence scope and strength declarations satisfy the credible evidence requirement; the gate determination records satisfy the conclusions and acting-on-findings requirements; the pre-round publication requirements satisfy the relevance, utility, and transparency standards. CDC program officers familiar with the framework can read a CROSS gate configuration and identify each framework element it implements.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication audit is the structural implementation of the framework's Step 3 (focus the evaluation questions and design) and Step 4 (gather credible evidence) applied at the instrument design stage. The framework requires that evaluation questions be focused before evidence is gathered and that the evidence gathered be reliable, valid, authentic, and informative for its intended use. Evaluation instruments that pass WALKRI's five-element specification check satisfy this requirement structurally: each intake field has a stated criterion intent, a bounded operational definition, a response form, an evidence form, and a conformance threshold before any applicant or grantee encounters it.

The framework's nonprescriptive posture means that programs adopt the framework without inheriting a common instrument library; each program designs its own data collection consistent with framework standards. This is the condition under which WALKRI's pre-publication audit is most useful. Without a structural specification requirement, programs adopting the framework can satisfy the framework's surface requirements while producing instruments that fail the verifiable evidence standard in practice: questions that are ambiguous, response formats that accept incommensurable answer types, evidence requirements that are stated in terms too general to produce consistent judgments across reviewers. WALKRI eliminates this class of failure at source.

The framework's rigor and transparency standards are also addressed at the instrument level by WALKRI. Rigor at the instrument level means that the measurement procedure is specified with sufficient precision that an independent evaluator can reconstruct it; this is what WALKRI's five fields produce. Transparency at the instrument level means that the measurement procedure is publicly accessible before evidence is gathered; this is what WALKRI's pre-publication audit and the CROSS full application question list publication requirement require together.

For CDC NOFO recipients in programs that incorporate the framework by reference, WALKRI conformance produces an artifact that is auditable both against the framework's federal evaluation standards and against the technical evidence quality requirements that NOFO-level guidance frequently leaves underspecified. The same WALKRI artifact satisfies AEA/JCSEE Accuracy standards (where applicable), framework rigor standards, and the federal evidence quality standards from OMB M-20-12.
