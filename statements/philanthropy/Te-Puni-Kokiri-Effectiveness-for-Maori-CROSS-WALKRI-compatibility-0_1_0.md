# Te Puni Kokiri Effectiveness for Maori Framework (New Zealand)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

This statement documents structural alignment between Te Puni Kokiri's Effectiveness for Maori Measurement and Reporting Framework, published in 2014 as *Ka mohio, ka matau, ka ora: He ia korero* (TPK, 2014), and the CROSS+WALKRI specification standards. Te Puni Kokiri is the New Zealand government's Ministry of Maori Development, established under the Ministry of Maori Development Act 1991. The Effectiveness for Maori Framework (EFM) is the Crown's published methodology for state sector agencies to measure and report on their results for Maori, and on the effectiveness of activities that contribute to those results. The framework's five-phase measurement and reporting cycle, its two overarching principles of Engagement with Maori and State Sector Collaboration, its Poutama self-assessment model, and its insistence on both universal and Maori-specific indicators map onto CROSS gate architecture, the WALKRI pre-publication field requirements, and CROSS+WALKRI Primitives Foundation provisions on beneficiary engagement and population scope.

The lineage matters. EFM is a Crown-Maori results framework derived from Treaty of Waitangi obligations and Te Puni Kokiri's statutory monitoring role under Section 5 of the Ministry of Maori Development Act 1991. It is structurally distinct from settler-state development frameworks that treat Indigenous populations as one disaggregation category within a generic population. EFM treats Maori as Treaty partners whose aspirations, indicators, and engagement processes are framework inputs, not residual considerations.

---

## The Effectiveness for Maori Framework

The Effectiveness for Maori Measurement and Reporting Framework was developed by Te Puni Kokiri to assist state sector agencies in measuring and reporting on results for Maori, and on the effectiveness of agency activities that contribute to those results. The framework was prepared in accordance with Te Puni Kokiri's two particular statutory responsibilities under Section 5 of the Ministry of Maori Development Act 1991: promoting increases in the levels of achievement attained by Maori with respect to education, training and employment, health, and economic resource development; and monitoring and liaising with each department and agency that provides services to, or for, Maori for the purpose of ensuring the adequacy of those services. The framework is the operational instrument by which Te Puni Kokiri discharges the monitoring responsibility across the state sector.

The framework is structured around a five-phase Effectiveness for Maori Measurement and Reporting Cycle. The phases are: defining Effectiveness for Maori measurement and reporting priorities; planning for collecting Effectiveness for Maori information; collecting and analysing Effectiveness for Maori information; reporting and disseminating Effectiveness for Maori information; and utilising Effectiveness for Maori information for performance improvement. Each phase incorporates two overarching principles. Engagement with Maori requires that Maori, as significant consumers of government services and as Treaty partners, are involved in measurement and reporting decisions that affect them. State Sector Collaboration requires that agencies working toward the same or similar results share information and coordinate data collection to reduce duplication and improve coherence.

The framework requires agencies to consider both universal and Maori-specific indicators across the full performance hierarchy of inputs, outputs, impacts, and outcomes or results. Universal effectiveness indicators capture views or outcomes that are valued regardless of ethnicity and that can be disaggregated by ethnicity to highlight differences. Maori-specific indicators reflect needs and aspirations that are culturally significant to Maori, including measurement at the whanau (extended family) level, which the framework treats as a key building block of society. The framework explicitly cautions against reducing Maori measurement to disparities analysis alone, on the grounds that disparities reporting fails to capture variation within and between Maori communities, hapu, and iwi, and may obscure Maori-defined success.

The Treaty of Waitangi grounding is named explicitly in the framework's text. Treaty obligations, as articulated in Treaty principles, require the Crown to actively protect the interests of its Maori Treaty partners. This requirement means that state sector agencies should consider and engage with Maori on issues that concern them. The framework operationalizes that obligation by making engagement with Maori a precondition of every measurement and reporting phase, not a consultation step appended after design is complete.

The framework includes the Poutama (staircase) model, a four-stage self-assessment instrument: Te Kakano (potential), Tipuranga (activation), Whanaketanga (development), and Puawaitanga (realisation). Agencies use the Poutama to locate their current measurement and reporting practice and to identify next steps toward Puawaitanga, the state in which an agency measures and reports its results for Maori across all areas of its work as part of business as usual. The Poutama is paired with a published table of stage descriptors for each phase of the cycle, allowing self-review against a public benchmark rather than against agency-defined criteria.

The framework's distinct lineage warrants emphasis. EFM is not a settler-state development framework in which an Indigenous population appears as a disaggregation category within a universal evaluation logic. It is a Crown-Maori results framework grounded in the Crown's Treaty obligations to its Maori Treaty partners. The framework's treatment of Maori-specific indicators, whanau-level measurement, tikanga Maori in data collection, and engagement with Maori as a structural precondition reflects this lineage. Compatibility statements for frameworks of this lineage must preserve the distinction rather than collapse it into generic outcomes evaluation vocabulary.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Effectiveness for Maori Framework

CROSS's gate architecture, Theory of Change hierarchy, and identity primitives implement the structural requirements EFM places on state sector measurement and reporting.

EFM's first phase, defining measurement and reporting priorities, maps to CROSS's pre-round publication requirements. CROSS requires that the funder publish a gate configuration and a plain-language applicant-facing summary before any round opens, naming what will be measured, against what indicators, and to what evidence standard. An agency adopting CROSS for a grant program that funds Maori-targeted activity is required to publish its priorities, its priority indicators, and its priority audiences before applications open. EFM's directive that agencies define priorities and identify priority indicators at the start of the cycle is the same structural commitment, expressed in the public-sector reporting context rather than in the grants context.

EFM's requirement that agencies consider both universal and Maori-specific indicators maps to CROSS's indicator specification provisions in Part V and to the Public Benefit Mechanism and Access Condition declaration in Part IV. CROSS requires the funder to specify the indicators against which outcomes will be assessed before applications are accepted. The choice between universal indicators (capable of disaggregation by ethnicity) and Maori-specific indicators (reflecting Maori-defined aspirations) is a gate configuration decision under CROSS. A funder configuring a program that funds activities affecting Maori is required, under EFM, to consider both indicator types; under CROSS, that consideration is recorded in the published gate configuration where it is visible to applicants and reviewable by Te Puni Kokiri in its monitoring capacity.

EFM's requirement that agencies measure at the whanau level where appropriate maps to CROSS's beneficiary engagement and beneficiary validation provisions in Part II. CROSS requires that the claimed affected population be defined and that the population's needs be validated by parties outside the applicant's control. In change-obligation rounds, the claimed FROM state must be validated externally; in build-obligation rounds, the claimed need for the deliverable must be validated by at least one party outside the applicant's organization who would use or benefit from it. EFM's directive that Maori service users have valuable insights about the best way to collect data from them, and that engagement with Maori produces more verifiable measurement decisions, is implemented in CROSS as a structural requirement rather than a recommended practice.

EFM's collect-and-analyse phase, with its directive that researchers apply tikanga Maori (Maori protocols) where relevant and that information received from Maori participants be treated as taonga (treasure), maps to CROSS's evidence scope and evidence strength specifications. The funder configuring a CROSS round that involves data collection from Maori participants is required to declare the data collection methodology, the evidence type, and the evidence strength tier at gate configuration. Tikanga-aligned methodologies (including kanohi ki te kanohi, face-to-face engagement, and acknowledgment of koha) are specifiable as the methodology of record in the gate configuration. CROSS's format agnosticism (Part I) explicitly accommodates methodologies that satisfy the content requirement through means other than standard analytics; tikanga Maori methodologies are conformant under this provision.

EFM's report-and-communicate phase, which requires bilingual reporting where appropriate and explicit reporting back to whanau, hapu, and iwi using kanohi ki te kanohi methods, maps to CROSS's Attestation Corpus provisions and to the published gate determination requirement. CROSS requires that gate determinations be recorded as funder-signed records that an independent party can verify. Where a CROSS-conformant program operates in a context where EFM applies, the reporting requirement is satisfied when gate determinations are produced in the form EFM specifies (bilingual where appropriate, communicated to Maori parties including whanau, hapu, iwi representatives, and Maori service users in a culturally appropriate manner) and are recorded in the Attestation Corpus in a form queryable by Te Puni Kokiri for its monitoring role.

EFM's utilise phase, in which agencies use Effectiveness for Maori information to improve subsequent practice, maps to CROSS's continuation gate provisions in Part IV. CROSS configures a continuation gate as the mechanism by which evidentiary pressure escalates across rounds of a program, and as the instrument by which agencies update their gate configuration on the basis of accumulated evidence. EFM's directive that agencies should not treat reporting as an end in itself but as a feedback mechanism into the next planning cycle is the same structural commitment, applied to the state sector reporting context.

The Poutama self-assessment model maps to CROSS's funder obligation maturation provisions in Part XI and to the funder configuration disclosure required at gate configuration. CROSS makes funder maturation visible to the field over time by requiring funders to record and version their gate configurations across rounds. The Poutama is a self-assessment instrument that produces the same visibility within an agency. Where a state sector agency uses CROSS for a grant program and the Poutama for its broader measurement practice, the two instruments are complementary: CROSS records the per-round configuration with public version history, and the Poutama provides the cross-cycle assessment of where the agency stands on its journey toward Puawaitanga.

The following table summarizes the core mappings.

| EFM Element | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Define EFM measurement and reporting priorities | Pre-round publication of gate configuration and plain-language summary (Part IV) |
| Universal and Maori-specific indicator selection | Indicator specification (Part V) and Public Benefit Mechanism and Access Condition declaration (Part IV) |
| Whanau-level measurement; engagement with Maori service users | Beneficiary engagement and beneficiary validation provisions (Part II) |
| Tikanga Maori in data collection; data as taonga | Evidence scope, evidence strength, and format agnosticism (Parts I and IV) |
| Bilingual reporting; kanohi ki te kanohi reporting back | Attestation Corpus and funder-signed gate determinations (Parts III, IV) |
| Utilise EFM information for performance improvement | Continuation gate provisions (Part IV) |
| Poutama self-assessment of measurement practice | Funder obligation maturation and configuration version history (Part XI) |
| Engagement with Maori as overarching principle | Beneficiary engagement primitive (Primitives Foundation, Layer 3) |
| State Sector Collaboration as overarching principle | Concurrent funding disclosure and portfolio context statement (Parts II, IV) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements ensure that intake fields collecting Effectiveness for Maori data are measurement instruments before any Maori participant or grantee encounters them. EFM directs that engagement with Maori begin early in the measurement cycle and that data collected from Maori be treated as taonga. WALKRI operationalizes this by requiring that the construct, metric, unit, baseline, and evidence threshold be specified for every field before the field is presented to anyone. A field that asks Maori service users about whanau wellbeing is not a measurement instrument unless it specifies what whanau wellbeing means in measurable terms, whose wellbeing is being asked about, and what evidence threshold a given response represents. Without WALKRI's pre-publication audit, the field collects narrative but not measurement, which is the failure mode EFM's planning phase is designed to prevent.

WALKRI's applicant identity instrument provisions (Section 3.7) intersect with EFM's engagement-with-Maori principle in a specific way. EFM requires that agencies engaging Maori in measurement design understand whose voice they are hearing: a service user, an iwi authority, a hapu representative, an individual Maori participant, or a Maori provider organization. WALKRI's identity instrument requires that the entity speaking through any field be declared and that the relationship between speaker and the population the measurement concerns be visible. A measurement design that conflates individual Maori service user feedback with iwi authority endorsement fails both EFM's engagement principle and WALKRI's identity precision requirement.

WALKRI's instrument also ensures that bilingual reporting commitments under EFM produce bilingual measurement, not only bilingual narration. A reporting commitment to publish in te reo Maori and English requires that the underlying indicators, fields, and gate criteria be specified in both languages before the round opens. WALKRI's pre-publication audit catches the failure mode in which a program publishes its final report bilingually but designed its measurement architecture monolingually, producing a translated artifact that has not been measurement-tested against the population whose language it claims to honour.

For Te Puni Kokiri's monitoring role under Section 5(b) of the Ministry of Maori Development Act 1991, WALKRI-audited intake produces the kind of independently reviewable measurement record that allows the Ministry to assess whether an agency's services are adequate for Maori. WALKRI's record of field specifications and their operational definitions is a procedural evidence base that supports the monitoring function independent of any particular outcome.

## Distinct Lineage and Compatibility Posture

CROSS+WALKRI's coverage of the Effectiveness for Maori Framework is offered in the spirit of compatibility, not assimilation. The framework was developed by Te Puni Kokiri to express the Crown's Treaty obligations as operational measurement practice across the New Zealand state sector. Its authority derives from the Treaty of Waitangi and from Te Puni Kokiri's statutory role, not from any technical standard external to that context.

A CROSS+WALKRI-conformant grant program operating within New Zealand and funding Maori-targeted activity satisfies EFM when the program's gate configuration, indicator specifications, beneficiary engagement provisions, and reporting practices align with the EFM cycle and overarching principles as described above. A funder adopting CROSS+WALKRI does not displace EFM; the funder's program operates within the Crown-Maori accountability structure EFM expresses, and CROSS+WALKRI provides the gate architecture, the field-level precision, and the Attestation Corpus record that EFM's planning, collection, reporting, and utilisation phases require.

The distinction from settler-state development frameworks is preserved by this posture. EFM is not absorbed into a universal evaluation logic in which Maori appear as a population subset. It remains a Crown-Maori results framework whose grounding in the Treaty of Waitangi and in Te Puni Kokiri's statutory monitoring role is foundational rather than incidental. CROSS+WALKRI's compatibility with EFM is a structural compatibility between two independently authored frameworks, expressed in the terms each framework uses.
