# Ford Foundation Office of Strategy and Learning Model Compatibility - CROSS+WALKRI

## Source and Scope Note

Ford's Office of Strategy and Learning (OSL) model is not a published standard document in the same sense as the other references in this compatibility series. This statement documents structural alignment with Ford's evaluation architecture as described in published case studies, the Evaluation Roundtable case study (2025), and public Ford Foundation materials. It does not represent formal conformance with a codified policy. The alignment described here is analytical: if a funder or intermediary implements CROSS+WALKRI, the resulting practices correspond structurally to the OSL model's key features. Ford Foundation has not reviewed or endorsed this statement.

## The Ford OSL Model: Key Features

Ford operates on 10-year strategy lifecycles, with 5-year strategy reviews and external evaluation at the midpoint. The OSL works with program teams to develop Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (MEL) plans that include indicators of progress and learning questions that explicitly test the assumptions underlying each program strategy.

A central feature of the OSL model is its three-sphere evaluation ontology. The Sphere of Control covers direct grantmaking actions: what the Foundation and its grantees do. The Sphere of Influence covers program strategy outcomes involving multiple actors, where the Foundation's role is contributory rather than causal. The Sphere of Impact covers long-term systemic change, where the Foundation's work is one of many forces operating over extended time horizons.

External evaluations in the OSL model focus at the portfolio and strategy level, not on individual grantees. This reflects a deliberate choice to reduce grantee reporting burden while improving the quality of evidence at the level where strategic decisions are actually made. Ford has publicly documented a reduction in average grantee reporting burden from 75 hours in 2012 to 50 hours in 2024.

"Learning-oriented accountability" is the OSL's framing for the relationship between accountability and learning: accountability is not for outputs delivered but for understanding how change happens collectively. "Contribution over attribution" is an explicit principle, formally applied to how Ford's program strategies are assessed.

## CROSS Alignment in Detail

Ford's three-sphere ontology maps directly to CROSS's theory-of-change (ToC) hierarchy. The Sphere of Control corresponds to the activities and outputs level in CROSS, where programs specify what they will do and what direct results they will produce, and where attribution claims are most defensible. The Sphere of Influence corresponds to the outcomes level, where CROSS programs operating under Change or Retroactive obligations must declare whether they are claiming attribution or contribution, since outcomes in this sphere involve actors and dynamics beyond any single program's control. The Sphere of Impact corresponds to the goal level in CROSS, where the long-term systemic change that motivates the program is named but held at a distance from direct obligation claims.

This mapping is not an exact translation; CROSS's ToC hierarchy is a gate-level structure, not an organizational evaluation ontology. But the underlying logic is the same: obligation verification is strongest and most tractable closest to the Sphere of Control, and weakest at the level of systemic impact. CROSS makes this gradation operational by attaching different evidence requirements and causality stance requirements at different levels of the ToC.

CROSS's causality stance field explicitly implements Ford's "contribution over attribution" principle. At each gate, programs using CROSS must declare whether they are claiming that their activities caused the observed change (attribution) or that their activities contributed to a change with multiple causes (contribution). This is not a narrative softening of the obligation direction; it is a structured declaration that affects how evidence is evaluated at the gate. A program that claims attribution must provide evidence that supports a causal claim. A program that claims contribution must provide evidence that supports a role-in-context claim. The distinction is operational, not rhetorical.

CROSS's pre-round obligation specification reduces grantee burden in the same direction Ford's OSL model has pursued. When grantees know before applying what evidence they must produce at each milestone gate, the reporting burden is shifted to the round design process, where it belongs, and away from individual grantees who would otherwise be responding to post-award information requests. The grantee who has read the round specification before applying is not surprised by what the milestone gate requires; they have planned for it.

## WALKRI Alignment in Detail

WALKRI's pre-publication audit reduces extractive burden on grantees by a different mechanism than CROSS. Where CROSS shifts burden by publishing gate conditions before applications open, WALKRI shifts burden by specifying what evidence is required before grantees are asked to provide it. An update template that has passed WALKRI's pre-publication audit contains fields that are already defined as measurement instruments. Grantees responding to those fields are not constructing a case from scratch; they are providing evidence against a pre-declared standard. The cost of interpretation falls on the round designer at instrument creation time, not on the grantee at reporting time.

WALKRI's field-level evidence type declarations also support the OSL's learning questions function. MEL plans that test underlying assumptions require that the measurement instruments be designed around those assumptions, not around whatever data happens to be available. WALKRI's requirement that each field declare its construct and metric before data collection begins applies this design discipline.

## Partial Nature of This Statement

Ford's OSL model, as a 10-year strategy lifecycle framework with internal MEL processes and portfolio-level external evaluations, is more architecturally complex than any single implementation of CROSS+WALKRI can cover. This statement documents the structural correspondences that exist; it does not claim that CROSS+WALKRI replicates the full OSL model. In particular, CROSS+WALKRI does not address the internal foundation governance functions of the OSL, the portfolio-level evaluation commissioning process, or the 5-year strategy review cycle. Those remain outside the scope of what CROSS+WALKRI specifies.
