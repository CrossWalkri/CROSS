# MacArthur Foundation Strategy-Level Evaluation Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The MacArthur Foundation's strategy-level evaluation approach focuses evaluation at the portfolio and strategy level rather than the individual grant level, using four evaluation focus areas (landscape, feedback, outcomes, and impact) and explicit principles including contribution over attribution, non-punitive evaluation, and Board-level strategic decision integration. A CROSS+WALKRI-conformant program satisfies the structural requirements of this approach as a consequence of conformance: the gate architecture addresses all four focus areas, the causality stance field implements the contribution-over-attribution principle, and the Attestation Corpus enables cross-round pattern analysis at the portfolio level.

This statement documents alignment with MacArthur's publicly described evaluation practices. MacArthur does not publish a formal evaluation policy document; this statement is based on materials published on macfound.org and in public case studies.

---

## The MacArthur Foundation's Evaluation Approach

MacArthur's evaluation architecture is explicitly positioned at the strategy and portfolio level: "Rather than evaluating individual grants, we focus our efforts on the portfolio and strategy as a whole." The four evaluation focus areas are:

**Landscape:** What is the context for the program area and how is it changing? This requires understanding the field before and during grantmaking, not only at the end.

**Feedback:** What is working and where can we improve? This requires ongoing monitoring data collected with sufficient consistency to surface patterns.

**Outcomes:** What progress is evident and with what consequences? This requires pre-specified outcome indicators with baselines and targets.

**Impact:** How is the work contributing to meaningful change? This requires a causal theory connecting the program's activities to longer-term change, with an explicit attribution stance.

MacArthur's evaluation principles include: be ambitious yet realistic; be rigorous and apply logic while staying flexible; prioritize learning; identify both successes and failures; never be punitive; stay focused on contribution over attribution. External evaluation partners work at the strategy level with the purpose of challenging assumptions and providing Board-ready analysis for decisions on whether to "pivot, exit, or deepen a commitment."

## How CROSS Satisfies the MacArthur Evaluation Approach

CROSS's ToC architecture and gate design address all four MacArthur evaluation focus areas structurally.

**Landscape** is addressed by CROSS's coherence disclosure requirement, which requires programs to declare how their program relates to other known interventions in the same problem area. This structures the landscape context that MacArthur's evaluators need to assess relevance and field positioning before and during grantmaking.

**Feedback** is addressed by CROSS's progress verification gate architecture. Progress gates produce structured determination records at defined points during implementation. These records are consistent across the cohort because they are assessed against criteria that existed before applications opened, making cross-grant pattern analysis possible.

**Outcomes** are addressed by CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification. Fields 1 through 5 (name, definition, unit, baseline, target) produce pre-specified outcome indicators with baselines and targets as a structural output of the entry specification gate. MacArthur evaluators working with CROSS-conformant grantees find outcome indicators that were specified before work began, not reconstructed from grantee narratives after the fact.

**Impact and attribution stance** are addressed by CROSS's causality stance field. This field requires programs to declare, at each gate, whether they are claiming attribution (the intervention caused the change) or contribution (the intervention contributed to a change with multiple causes). This is the structural expression of MacArthur's "contribution over attribution" principle: the stance is declared in the gate configuration before any grantee applies, not asserted post-hoc in a final report.

| MacArthur Evaluation Focus | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| Landscape: field context before and during grantmaking | Coherence disclosure requirement (how program relates to other interventions in same area) |
| Feedback: ongoing monitoring with consistent data | Progress verification gate records (consistent across cohort because pre-specified) |
| Outcomes: pre-specified indicators with baselines and targets | Eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1-5: name, definition, unit, baseline, target) |
| Impact: causal theory with explicit attribution stance | ToC architecture + causality stance field (attribution vs contribution declared pre-round) |
| "Never be punitive" | Entry specification gate information is published before applicants submit; no post-hoc standard changes |
| Board-level strategic decisions (pivot/exit/deepen) | Continuation gate: pre-defined evidence for advancing to subsequent rounds |
| Portfolio and strategy level, not individual grant level | Attestation Corpus: cross-round pattern analysis across grantee population |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

MacArthur's focus on non-extractive evaluation requires that grantees not bear a disproportionate data burden. WALKRI's pre-publication audit reduces this burden structurally: by specifying what each intake field measures and what evidence is required before any applicant sees the form, WALKRI eliminates the ambiguity that forces grantees to over-produce data. A grantee responding to a WALKRI-conformant intake form knows exactly what evidence is required and what the reviewer will accept. The data production burden is minimized because the specification is clear.
