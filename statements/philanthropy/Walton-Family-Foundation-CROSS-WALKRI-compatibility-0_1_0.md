# Walton Family Foundation Strategy, Learning and Evaluation (SLED) Approach Compatibility - CROSS+WALKRI

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

This statement documents structural alignment between the Walton Family Foundation's Strategy, Learning and Evaluation (SLED) approach and the CROSS+WALKRI specification standards. The Walton Family Foundation requires grantees to draft their own performance measures as part of proposal development, which are then reviewed and approved by the Foundation's SLED Department before any funding is issued. This SLED pre-approval gate for grantee-developed performance measures is structurally distinctive among major US foundations: it is the only major philanthropic framework in CROSS+WALKRI's coverage that makes pre-approval of grantee-drafted performance measures a required condition of disbursement, as opposed to requiring grantees to report against funder-prescribed indicators. CROSS's pre-award indicator confirmation window implements the same architecture; WALKRI ensures that the initial performance measure proposals grantees draft are operationally specified measurement instruments from the outset.

---

## The Walton Family Foundation SLED Approach

The Walton Family Foundation is one of the largest private foundations in the United States, with focus areas in environment (rivers and oceans conservation, freshwater systems), education (K-12 educational opportunity and school choice), and community development in the Arkansas delta region where the Walton family has deep roots. The Foundation's total grantmaking exceeds $600 million annually. The SLED Department, whose work is described at waltonfamilyfoundation.org/learning, is responsible for strategy development, learning agenda design, and evaluation oversight across all program areas.

The defining procedural standard in the Walton SLED approach is the grantee performance measure drafting and approval requirement. Grantees are expected to develop their own performance measures during the proposal development process. Those measures are reviewed collaboratively between the grantee and the SLED Department. Funding is not issued until SLED has reviewed and approved the performance measures. This review is collaborative, not merely a conformance check: SLED works with grantees to refine measures that are not adequately specified, rather than simply accepting or rejecting measures as submitted.

This procedural architecture is structurally different from how other major foundations handle performance measures. The Gates Foundation, Hewlett Foundation, Packard Foundation, Ford Foundation, and MacArthur Foundation all specify evaluation requirements for grantees, but they typically do not require pre-approval of grantee-developed measures as a gate condition before disbursement. In those frameworks, grantees receive guidance on what to measure and report against, and evaluation requirements are applied through reporting review rather than through a pre-disbursement approval gate. The Walton SLED approval gate makes performance measure quality a condition of funding release rather than a condition of reporting conformance, which creates a fundamentally different incentive structure for grantees drafting their measures.

The SLED approach also emphasizes learning at the portfolio level. The Foundation maintains a learning agenda across program areas, and individual grant evaluations are designed, where possible, to contribute to portfolio-level learning questions. This means the performance measures approved through the SLED process are not only obligation verification records for individual grants; they are also data collection instruments for portfolio-level learning.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Walton SLED Framework

CROSS's pre-award indicator confirmation window is the primary structural response to the Walton SLED approval gate. The architecture of these two mechanisms is the same: both create a post-entry, pre-disbursement window in which performance measure specifications are reviewed and confirmed before funding is released.

CROSS's general framework establishes the entry specification gate as the primary pre-award commitment mechanism: round specifications, including indicator specifications, Theory of Change declarations, and evidence type requirements, must be complete before applications open. This is the standard CROSS pre-award commitment. However, CROSS Part IV provides an optional pre-award indicator confirmation window, applicable in contexts where grantees have latitude to propose their own performance measures within a funder-defined framework. In this window, grantee-proposed indicator specifications are submitted after the entry gate but before disbursement; the funder reviews and confirms the specifications (or returns them for revision) within a specified timeframe; and silence after that timeframe constitutes confirmation. The Walton SLED approval process is a required version of this same architecture: the grantee drafts measures, SLED reviews them collaboratively, and disbursement is withheld until SLED confirms the measures are adequately specified.

CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification, Fields 1 through 11 in the CROSS indicator specification standard, provides the structural framework that SLED-reviewed performance measures must satisfy to be operationally adequate. Fields 1 through 5 cover the basic measurement instrument specification: indicator name, operational definition, unit of measurement, baseline value, and target value. Fields 6 through 11 cover the more demanding elements of outcome measurement: measurement timing, data source, data collection method, comparison group specification, causality stance, and evidence threshold. A performance measure submitted to SLED that does not include operational definitions for all applicable fields is not yet a measurement instrument; it is an intention to measure. CROSS provides the field structure that the collaborative SLED review process is implicitly working toward, whether or not CROSS vocabulary is in use.

The portfolio-level learning agenda in the Walton SLED approach corresponds to CROSS's coherence disclosure. CROSS requires that every round specification declare how the program's outcomes relate to the broader condition the funder is tracking at the portfolio level. For Walton-funded programs in freshwater conservation, for example, the coherence disclosure specifies how the specific program outcomes (river miles protected, flow regimes restored, named-party coalitions built) relate to the portfolio-level learning question about freshwater system health. This connection must be declared before the round opens; it cannot be inferred retroactively from grant reports.

| Walton SLED Requirement | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Grantee-drafted performance measures reviewed before disbursement | CROSS Part IV pre-award indicator confirmation window (required version of optional window) |
| Collaborative refinement of performance measures with SLED | CROSS eleven-field indicator specification as the standard SLED review works toward |
| Pre-disbursement approval gate for measure adequacy | CROSS confirmation or return-for-revision mechanism in the pre-award window |
| Portfolio-level learning agenda | CROSS coherence disclosure: program outcomes situated within funder's portfolio-level learning question |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements ensure that the performance measure proposals grantees submit to SLED are operationally specified measurement instruments from the outset of the drafting process, reducing the collaborative revision burden that the SLED approval process otherwise requires.

Without WALKRI, grantees drafting performance measures typically produce measures that are conceptually oriented but operationally incomplete. A measure like "increase in student reading proficiency" names a construct but does not specify what assessment instrument will be used, what baseline is established, what gain constitutes the target, over what time period measurement will occur, or what comparison group is available. SLED must then work through those operational questions in the collaborative review process. WALKRI's five requirements, applied to grantee-facing drafting guidance, ensure that grantees address these questions before they submit measures for SLED review: construct, metric, unit, baseline, target, and evidence threshold must be specified in the initial submission.

This shifts the SLED collaborative review from a process of surfacing missing operational elements to a process of verifying the quality of already-specified elements. It makes the review faster, more consistent across program officers, and more useful to grantees who receive clearer guidance about what "an adequately specified measure" actually requires. WALKRI also ensures that the measures submitted across a grantee cohort are specified in a comparable format, which is a precondition for portfolio-level analysis: if measures are not comparably specified, portfolio-level learning is impeded because the data structures are not commensurable.

For Walton's environment program specifically, WALKRI's pre-publication requirements applied to grantee drafting guidance would ensure that freshwater conservation outcome measures reference specific hydrological data sources, specify what flow regime threshold constitutes a restored river reach, and identify what monitoring protocol will be used to assess habitat condition. These operational specifications are the foundation of portfolio-level learning; without them, the portfolio data collected across individual grants cannot be aggregated or compared to answer the Foundation's portfolio-level questions about freshwater system trajectories.
