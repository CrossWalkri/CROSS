# Rockefeller Foundation Strategic Learning Blueprint Compatibility - CROSS+WALKRI

## Summary

This statement documents structural alignment between the Rockefeller Foundation's 2024 Strategic Learning Blueprint and the CROSS+WALKRI specification standards. The Blueprint, published alongside the 2024 Impact Report, articulates the Foundation's operating principles for measurement, learning, and evidence sharing across its grantmaking portfolio. The two primary sources are the Blueprint perspective piece (rockefellerfoundation.org/perspective/deepening-impact-through-strategic-learning/) and the 2024 Impact Report.

CROSS v0.4.1 and WALKRI v0.1.6 are both published under CC0 at github.com/CrossWalkri.

## The Rockefeller Strategic Learning Blueprint: Core Principles

The Blueprint organizes its learning approach around five commitments. The first is a shift from having answers to asking good questions: programs should formulate explicit learning questions attuned to the complexity of the systems they operate in, rather than entering grant relationships with predetermined conclusions to validate.

The second is learning with grantees, not just from them. The Blueprint distinguishes collaborative evaluation from extractive evaluation; the latter mines grantees for data while the former treats grantees as co-investigators in understanding what is and is not working.

The third is triangulation across qualitative and quantitative evidence, with explicit attention to equity. No single metric type is treated as definitive; programs are expected to hold multiple evidence streams in tension.

The fourth is external transparency: sharing insights internally and externally to build trust. The 2024 Impact Report frames this as a norm rather than a case-by-case decision.

The fifth is institutional humility, captured in the Blueprint's statement that it is acceptable to be wrong. This includes an explicit preference for surfacing what did not work alongside what did.

The 2024 Impact Report adds two learning principles from field practice: "Learn first, join second, build last" and "stand behind, not in front of." Both formulations position the Foundation as a learning participant in systems it does not control.

## CROSS Alignment in Detail

CROSS addresses the "learning with grantees" principle at a structural level. The entry specification gate requires that all gate criteria, evidence type requirements, and milestone conditions be published before any application is submitted. A grantee applying to a CROSS-compliant round knows, before they invest time in an application, exactly what they will be required to demonstrate at each gate. They are not encountering evaluation criteria for the first time when they receive a mid-grant reporting request. This is the structural opposite of extractive evaluation: the burden of specifying what must be shown falls on the funder at round design time, not on the grantee at reporting time.

CROSS's causality stance field implements the "asking good questions" and "humility" principles by requiring programs to declare at each gate whether they are claiming attribution (this outcome was caused by our work) or contribution (this outcome occurred in a context to which our work contributed). This distinction is not cosmetic. A program that declares a contribution stance has formally acknowledged that outcomes have multiple causes and that the evaluation question is how and whether the program played a role, not whether it single-handedly produced the result. CROSS makes this declaration a gate condition, not a reflection included in a narrative report at the end of the grant period.

CROSS's Attestation Corpus implements the "share insights externally" principle by making prior round specifications and gate decision records persistently accessible. Past evaluation findings are not locked in program-team files or grant management systems; they are structured records in a publicly accessible corpus that informs future round design, applicant preparation, and external learning.

The CROSS completion gate's required recording of what did not meet the milestone threshold, alongside what did, instantiates the Foundation's humility principle in operational form.

## WALKRI Alignment in Detail

WALKRI's pre-publication audit implements "sharing intentions" at the instrument level. Before any grantee sees an intake form or update template, every field in that form must be specified: what construct it measures, by what metric, against what evidence type. This is a form of transparency that precedes data collection, rather than following it. Grantees encounter a measurement instrument, not an open-ended request whose evaluation criteria are internal to the funder.

The pre-publication audit also supports equity considerations directly. When field specifications are published before grantees respond, the criteria are the same for all applicants. Interpretation of what counts as satisfactory evidence is not made field-by-field during review; it is declared in the instrument specification. This reduces the space for implicit criteria to operate differently across applicants.

WALKRI's requirement that evidence types be declared before collection (quantitative, qualitative, or both) implements the triangulation principle at the instrument design level. A round specification that declares both evidence types for a given milestone has structurally committed to triangulation; a round that declares only one has documented that commitment to a single stream and must justify it in the specification.

## Scope and Limitations

The Rockefeller Foundation has not reviewed or endorsed this statement. This document describes structural alignment between the Blueprint's stated principles and the CROSS+WALKRI specification; it does not constitute certification that any specific program or portfolio meets the Foundation's learning standards. The Blueprint is a principles document, not a compliance framework; alignment is assessed at the level of structural correspondence, not rule satisfaction.
