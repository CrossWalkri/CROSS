# EU GECES Social Impact Measurement Report (2014) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The European Commission GECES sub-group report "Proposed Approaches to Social Impact Measurement" (June 2014) establishes the EU's own social impact measurement standard, distinct from SROI and developed specifically for European Social Entrepreneurship Funds (EuSEF) regulation and the Employment and Social Innovation programme (EaSI). Its defining contribution is a four-component adjustment framework: attribution, deadweight, displacement, and drop-off. CROSS satisfies each of the four adjustment components through named structural provisions. WALKRI ensures that the intake fields producing baseline and population scope data meet the measurement quality required for each adjustment to be calculable rather than rhetorical.

The GECES report applies to any organization seeking EuSEF qualification or EaSI funding, and it functions as the European Commission's baseline standard for what constitutes verifiable social impact measurement in EU-regulated contexts. Funders operating in EU jurisdictions who use CROSS+WALKRI produce documentation that satisfies the GECES adjustment framework as a structural consequence of conformance.

---

## The GECES Framework

GECES defines social impact as "social effect (change), both long-term and short-term, achieved for target populations, adjusting for attribution, deadweight, displacement and drop-off." The four adjustment terms are:

Attribution: the portion of the observed change that is attributable to the program rather than to other actors or interventions operating in the same space.

Deadweight: the portion of the observed change that would have occurred anyway, even without the program's intervention.

Displacement: the degree to which the program's outputs may have shifted a problem or burden from one population or geography to another, rather than reducing it in aggregate.

Drop-off: the degree to which the program's impact diminishes over time after the intervention ends, reducing the long-term social value actually delivered.

These four adjustments together determine the net social impact figure that EuSEF-qualified social entrepreneurship funds and EaSI-funded programs must report. They are not optional refinements; they are mandatory components of the social impact calculation under EU regulation. The GECES report provides worked examples and guidance on calculating each adjustment, but leaves the measurement design to organizations and their funders.

## How CROSS Satisfies the GECES Framework

CROSS addresses each of the four GECES adjustments through distinct structural provisions. The correspondence is not analogical: each adjustment requires a specific piece of evidence or a specific design choice, and CROSS requires that piece of evidence or that design choice as a gate condition.

| GECES Adjustment | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Attribution | Causality stance field (Indicator Field 11): programs declare attribution stance or contribution stance for each indicator; attribution stance requires additionality argument and counterfactual evidence; contribution stance requires disclosure of co-factors | Full satisfaction. CROSS Field 11 is the structural location of the attribution adjustment. The additionality argument required under attribution stance produces exactly the documentation the GECES attribution adjustment requires: a reasoned claim that the observed change would not have occurred, or would have occurred to a lesser degree, without the program. |
| Deadweight | Counterfactual baseline requirement at entry specification gate: programs must declare what the target population's condition would be without the intervention, as a precondition for specifying change indicators | Full satisfaction. CROSS's entry specification gate requires counterfactual baseline declaration before data collection begins. The deadweight adjustment is a comparison of observed outcomes against the declared counterfactual; CROSS makes the counterfactual declaration a gate condition rather than a retrospective estimate. |
| Displacement | Coherence disclosure requirement: programs declare how their intervention relates to other interventions operating in the same problem area, including known substitution effects and population overlap | Full satisfaction. CROSS's coherence disclosure requirement is the structural mechanism for the displacement adjustment. Programs must declare whether their outputs compete with, substitute for, or complement other interventions serving the same population. Where substitution effects are known, they must be disclosed. This disclosure is the evidence layer for the displacement calculation. |
| Drop-off | Population scope field applied at continuation gate: the continuation gate's sustainability stance (sustained, conditional, dependent) requires a judgment about whether outcomes persist, under what conditions, and for how long | Full satisfaction. CROSS's continuation gate directly addresses drop-off. The three-position sustainability stance requires programs to assess whether their outcomes are sustained independently after funding ends, sustained conditionally on ongoing support, or dependent on continued direct intervention. This assessment is the structural equivalent of the GECES drop-off adjustment: it quantifies the degree to which impact diminishes after the intervention period. |

**Result:** CROSS satisfies all four GECES adjustment components as structural consequences of conformance. Programs that pass CROSS's entry specification gate and continuation gate have produced the evidence infrastructure the GECES framework requires for a verifiable social impact calculation.

## Application to EuSEF and EaSI Contexts

The GECES framework applies to two EU regulatory contexts: European Social Entrepreneurship Funds (EuSEF), which must demonstrate social impact to retail investors under ESMA regulation, and the Employment and Social Innovation programme (EaSI), which requires social impact measurement as a condition of EU grant funding. Both contexts require organizations to show not only what outcomes they achieved but how they adjusted for attribution, deadweight, displacement, and drop-off in arriving at their net impact figure.

CROSS-conformant programs in these contexts produce the required evidence at each adjustment point through the gate architecture rather than through post-hoc calculation. The counterfactual baseline (deadweight) is declared at the entry gate. The causality stance (attribution) is declared at the indicator level before data collection. The coherence disclosure (displacement) is part of the entry specification record. The sustainability stance (drop-off) is recorded at the continuation gate. A GECES-conformant impact report can be assembled from CROSS gate records without additional data collection.

For EuSEF qualification specifically, CROSS's obligation record functions as the measurement design document that fund managers must maintain for portfolio companies. WALKRI's pre-publication audit of intake instruments provides the methodological quality documentation that EuSEF due diligence requires.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements apply directly to the intake fields that produce the data needed for each GECES adjustment. The deadweight adjustment depends on a valid counterfactual baseline: WALKRI's operational definition requirement ensures that the baseline field has an explicit, testable definition before any program applies data to it. The attribution adjustment depends on a causality stance field that measures what it claims to measure: WALKRI's criterion intent requirement ensures that the causality stance field's purpose is stated and that the evidence form specified for it is independent of the grantee's self-report.

For displacement and drop-off, WALKRI's conformance threshold requirement is particularly relevant. Both adjustments involve threshold judgments: at what level of displacement does it become material enough to reduce the net impact figure, and at what rate does drop-off diminish the long-term value calculation? WALKRI requires that conformance thresholds be explicit and declared before data collection begins, which means the displacement and drop-off thresholds are documented design choices rather than post-hoc judgment calls. This is the difference between a GECES-conformant measurement design and a measurement narrative.
