# FSG Collective Impact Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

CROSS and WALKRI together address the FSG Guide to Evaluating Collective Impact (2014, with Aspen Institute) at the shared measurement and backbone aggregation layer. Collective impact's shared measurement requirement is structural: it requires that multiple programs in a portfolio measure the same outcomes with the same instruments so that backbone organizations can aggregate data across the initiative. CROSS's Cohort Position assessment addresses the portfolio context requirement. CROSS's coherence disclosure structures the cross-program coordination data that backbone analysis depends on. WALKRI ensures that shared measurement fields across multiple programs use consistent instrument specifications, which is the structural precondition for backbone aggregation.

This compatibility is relevant for backbone organizations, their funders (including StriveTogether network members and United Way backbone programs), and for grant programs participating in collective impact initiatives who want a specification standard that satisfies shared measurement requirements.

---

## What the FSG Collective Impact Framework Is

Collective impact, as articulated in FSG's original 2011 article and elaborated in the 2014 Guide to Evaluating Collective Impact, is a structured approach to multi-organization collaboration around a common agenda for a complex social problem. The framework specifies five conditions for collective impact: common agenda, shared measurement systems, mutually reinforcing activities, continuous communication, and backbone support organization.

The 2014 guide, co-developed with the Aspen Institute Forum for Community Solutions, focuses on evaluation of collective impact initiatives and includes 27 backbone effectiveness indicators. It is used by StriveTogether, United Way backbone programs, the Strive Network, and funders such as the Kellogg Foundation and the Annie E. Casey Foundation. The guide addresses how to measure progress at the initiative level, how backbone organizations demonstrate their effectiveness, and what data systems are needed to support cross-program aggregation.

The shared measurement condition is the one most directly relevant to CROSS and WALKRI. Without consistent measurement instruments across programs, backbone aggregation is meaningless: you cannot sum participants counted by different definitions, or compare outcomes assessed by instruments with different response forms. The shared measurement problem is, at its root, a specification problem: it is solved or not solved at the instrument design stage, before data collection begins.

---

## Shared Measurement Requirement: WALKRI Instrument Consistency

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements are the instrument-specification mechanism that addresses the shared measurement problem directly. When multiple programs within a collective impact initiative apply WALKRI to their collection instruments, the result is a set of instruments whose criterion intent, operational definitions, response forms, evidence forms, and compliance thresholds are all explicitly documented and comparable across programs.

The operational definition requirement is particularly significant for shared measurement. Shared measurement fails most often not because programs use different indicator names but because they use the same indicator name with different operational definitions. "Student chronically absent" can mean absent more than 10% of days in one program and absent more than 15% in another; without explicit operational definitions, the same label produces incomparable data. WALKRI's operational definition requirement forces each program to state its definition explicitly and document it before collection begins. When a backbone organization reviews operational definitions across programs, divergences are visible and can be resolved before data is collected, not discovered after aggregation produces implausible results.

The response form requirement addresses the collection method consistency dimension: two programs using different response forms (self-report versus administrative records, for example) produce data that is not directly comparable even if their operational definitions are identical. WALKRI requires each program to document its response form and justify the choice; a backbone organization can then identify inconsistencies in response form across programs and work with programs to align collection methods before the measurement cycle begins.

## Portfolio Context: CROSS Cohort Position Assessment

Collective impact requires each program to understand its role within the initiative's full portfolio: what contribution it makes to the common agenda, how its outputs and outcomes relate to other programs' outputs and outcomes, and where it sits in the initiative's theory of change. The FSG guide refers to this as the "contribution analysis" question for collective impact evaluation.

CROSS's Cohort Position assessment addresses this requirement at the specification layer. The Cohort Position assessment situates a program within the portfolio of programs sharing the same obligation structure: it documents what other programs are doing, how this program's outputs and outcomes relate to theirs, and what the expected interaction effects are. For collective impact initiatives using CROSS, the Cohort Position assessment is the structural artifact that answers the portfolio context question for each participating program, in a standardized format that backbone organizations can review and aggregate.

---

## Cross-Program Coordination: CROSS Coherence Disclosure

The mutually reinforcing activities condition in collective impact requires programs to coordinate their activities so that they complement rather than duplicate or undermine each other. CROSS's coherence disclosure provision requires programs to document their coordination relationships and any formal arrangements with other programs in the same cohort. This provision produces the structured cross-program coordination data that backbone analysis depends on when assessing the mutually reinforcing activities condition.

Field 10 (institutional framework alignment) can record the collective impact initiative's shared framework as the formal alignment reference for each participating program, establishing a documented connection between the program's indicator specification and the initiative's common agenda.

| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Shared measurement systems | Coherence disclosure; Cohort Position situates program in portfolio context | Five pre-publication field requirements ensure instrument consistency across programs | Structural: WALKRI is the specification-layer mechanism for shared measurement |
| Common agenda | ToC architecture with named causal links; Field 10 records initiative framework alignment | No direct provision | Partial: ToC alignment documented, agenda governance outside scope |
| Mutually reinforcing activities | Coherence disclosure structures cross-program coordination data | No direct provision | Structural: coordination relationships formally documented |
