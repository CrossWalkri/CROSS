# PHINEO Social Impact Navigator (Kursbuch Wirkung) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

PHINEO, in partnership with Bertelsmann Stiftung, published the Social Impact Navigator (German: Kursbuch Wirkung), the de facto reference guide for impact-oriented grantmaking across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, cited by German foundation networks as the sector standard. The Navigator is organized around the IOOI model: Input, Output, Outcome, Impact. CROSS's Theory of Change architecture covers all four IOOI levels as structural components of the program specification, with named provisions at each level. WALKRI applies at the needs assessment and target group definition stages, ensuring that the data collected at those stages meets the measurement quality the Navigator describes as best practice.

The Social Impact Navigator's influence extends well beyond the DACH region. Its combination of practical guidance, theory of change methodology, and indicator design instruction makes it a reference for any impact-oriented funder seeking a German-language or European alternative to Anglo-American frameworks. CROSS+WALKRI compatibility with the Navigator means that programs conforming to CROSS are also producing documentation that satisfies the Navigator's requirements for needs assessment, theory of change development, and indicator design.

---

## The Social Impact Navigator and IOOI Model

The Social Impact Navigator addresses five substantive areas of impact-oriented practice:

Needs assessment: the process of identifying what problem exists, how large it is, who is affected, and what is known about its causes and dynamics. The Navigator treats needs assessment as the foundation of credible impact work; without it, the theory of change is speculative.

Target group definition: the precise identification of who the intervention is designed to serve, including demographic and situational characteristics, size, and geographic scope. The Navigator emphasizes that vague target group definitions are the most common source of unmeasurable outcomes.

Theory of change development: the explicit causal model linking Inputs through Outputs and Outcomes to Impact. The Navigator uses the IOOI model as its organizing framework and requires that causal assumptions be made explicit rather than left implicit.

Indicator design: the specification of how change at each IOOI level will be measured, including indicator type (output, outcome, or impact), data source, frequency, and responsibility. The Navigator provides detailed guidance on indicator quality, including the distinction between output indicators (counting activities) and outcome indicators (measuring actual change in the target group).

Fundraising justification: the translation of the theory of change and indicator design into a funder-facing argument. The Navigator treats this as a downstream consequence of good measurement design, not a separate exercise.

## How CROSS Satisfies the IOOI Model

CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy covers all four IOOI levels with named structural components at each level. The correspondence is not a post-hoc mapping; CROSS's ToC architecture was designed to accommodate the four-level logic that IOOI represents.

| IOOI Level | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Input | Concurrent funding disclosure and resource declaration at entry specification gate: programs declare what resources (financial, human, infrastructure) are committed to the intervention | Full satisfaction. CROSS requires resource declaration as part of the entry specification gate. Inputs are not an implicit backdrop; they are declared fields in the obligation record. |
| Output | Completion gate deliverable specification: the precise outputs the program will produce, stated as gate conditions that must be met for milestone passage | Full satisfaction. CROSS treats outputs as gate conditions, not narrative claims. The completion gate deliverable specification corresponds exactly to the IOOI Output level: what the program actually produces and delivers. |
| Outcome | Change obligation target condition: the condition the target population is expected to be in after the program's outputs have been delivered and absorbed | Full satisfaction. CROSS's change obligation target condition is the IOOI Outcome level. It requires programs to specify what change in the target group's situation, behavior, or capacity is expected as a result of outputs, distinguished from the outputs themselves. |
| Impact | Goal-level Theory of Change declaration: the broader social change the program is contributing to, stated at the top of the ToC hierarchy | Full satisfaction. CROSS requires a goal-level declaration that situates the program's Outcomes within a larger causal argument about social change. This is the IOOI Impact level: the contribution to systemic or population-level change that Outcomes are expected to produce. |

**Result:** CROSS satisfies all four IOOI levels as structural components of the entry specification gate and ToC hierarchy. A CROSS-conformant program specification is an IOOI model in structured form.

## Indicator Design Alignment

The Social Impact Navigator's guidance on indicator design is one of its most detailed and practically useful sections. The Navigator distinguishes between output indicators (measuring what the program produces), outcome indicators (measuring what changes in the target group), and impact indicators (measuring contribution to systemic change). It recommends that programs specify at least one indicator at each level, with explicit data source, collection frequency, and responsibility.

CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification is the structural implementation of this guidance. Field 6 (population) anchors the indicator to a defined target group. Field 7 (metric type) distinguishes output from outcome from impact indicators using the same conceptual distinction the Navigator draws. Field 8 (data source) specifies where the evidence comes from. Field 9 (frequency) specifies when. Field 10 (responsible party and institutional framework alignment) specifies who is accountable for collection. Field 11 (causality stance) specifies whether the indicator is claimed as attributable to the program or as a contribution alongside other factors.

Together, Fields 6 through 11 implement precisely the indicator quality requirements the Social Impact Navigator describes as best practice. Programs using CROSS produce indicator specifications that satisfy the Navigator's standards without additional documentation.

## Needs Assessment and Target Group Definition

The Social Impact Navigator treats needs assessment and target group definition as specification-layer activities that precede indicator design and theory of change development. The quality of the entire downstream framework depends on the precision and evidence base of these two activities. The Navigator observes that the most common failure mode in impact measurement is an imprecise target group definition that makes outcome indicators unmeasurable in practice.

CROSS's population scope field, applied at the program level and at the indicator level, is the structural mechanism for the target group definition the Navigator requires. CROSS requires that population scope be declared as a field with an operational definition, not a narrative description. The specificity of the population scope declaration determines whether outcome indicators are measurable; a population scope that admits any applicant who self-identifies cannot anchor outcome change claims.

WALKRI addresses this requirement directly. WALKRI's operational definition requirement ensures that the population scope field has explicit inclusion and exclusion criteria before any applicant encounters it. WALKRI's conformance threshold requirement ensures that the standard for what counts as a qualifying member of the target group is stated in advance. These two WALKRI requirements implement the Navigator's target group definition guidance at the instrument design level.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's most significant contribution to Social Impact Navigator alignment is at the target group definition stage. The Navigator's warning about imprecise target group definitions is an instrument design warning: the field collecting target group information must be designed to elicit precise, verifiable data, not self-assessments that resist external validation.

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements, applied to the population scope and needs evidence fields, ensure that these fields function as measurement instruments. The criterion intent requirement states what the field is measuring and why. The operational definition requirement gives the field explicit boundaries. The response form justification requirement ensures that the data collection method (categorical, quantitative, open text, structured attestation) is appropriate to the measurement goal. The evidence form requirement ensures that target group membership can be verified independently. The conformance threshold requirement states the minimum standard for what counts as a qualifying response.

This is the instrument-level implementation of the Social Impact Navigator's needs assessment and target group definition best practices. Programs that have passed WALKRI's pre-publication audit before activating CROSS's entry specification gate are operating at the measurement quality level the Navigator recommends as foundational to credible impact work.
