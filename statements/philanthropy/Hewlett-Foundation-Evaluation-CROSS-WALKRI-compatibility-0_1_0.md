# Hewlett Foundation Evaluation Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Hewlett Foundation's "Evaluation Principles and Practices" (Second Edition, 2019) is the most widely cited peer-funder evaluation reference in US philanthropy. Published both as internal guidance for Hewlett program officers and explicitly as a normative resource for peer funders, it shapes evaluation design across a large share of US foundations. A CROSS+WALKRI-conformant program satisfies the five core principles of that guide as a structural consequence of conformance, not through supplementary documentation.

This statement maps each of the five Hewlett principles to the CROSS and WALKRI provisions that produce it. It also notes the peer-funder implication: any funder that uses the Hewlett guide as a reference implicitly requires what CROSS+WALKRI provides.

---

## The Five Hewlett Evaluation Principles

The Hewlett Foundation's evaluation framework is organized around five core principles:

**Outcome Orientation**: evaluations should be linked to a theory of change, with clear definitions of intended outcomes before evaluation design begins.

**Rigor**: methods must match the evaluation question; measurement instruments must actually measure what the question requires.

**Credibility**: evaluation data must be collected independently and transparently, with clear documentation of methods and sources.

**Utility**: evaluation findings must be structured to inform decisions rather than produce reports that are not acted upon.

**Learning Culture**: evaluations should generate usable knowledge that enables mid-course corrections and institutional learning, not only end-of-grant accountability.

Grantees are explicitly required to cooperate in evaluations and provide information as necessary. This data provision requirement is structural, not discretionary.

---

## Principle 1: Outcome Orientation

Hewlett's Outcome Orientation principle requires that evaluations be linked to a theory of change before evaluation design begins, with intended outcomes defined clearly enough to be measured.

CROSS addresses this principle directly. Every CROSS-conformant round includes a declared program-level Theory of Change before applicants apply. The Theory of Change is structured as a goal/outcome/output/activity hierarchy with named causal links, using CROSS's ToC architecture. Outcome indicators are specified at the entry specification gate using CROSS's 11-field indicator format. This means the theory of change is not a narrative document produced after the fact; it is a structured obligation architecture that precedes and structures the entire program.

For Hewlett program officers working with CROSS-conformant grantees, the Outcome Orientation requirement is satisfied before the grant agreement is signed. The theory of change exists in structured form, the outcome indicators are specified, and the causal links between activities and outcomes are documented. The evaluation can begin with an already-structured obligation architecture rather than constructing one from the program narrative.

---

## Principle 2: Rigor

Hewlett's Rigor principle requires that evaluation methods match the evaluation question, and that measurement instruments actually measure what the question requires rather than what is convenient to measure.

WALKRI addresses this principle at the field specification level. Every WALKRI-conformant intake criterion requires an operational definition: the precise boundary conditions of the criterion, stated before data collection begins. An operational definition is exactly what distinguishes a criterion that measures what the question requires from one that measures a convenient proxy. Without an operational definition, two evaluators assessing the same contribution can reach different conclusions because they are implicitly applying different boundaries. WALKRI makes those boundaries explicit and fixed before evaluation opens.

WALKRI's evidence form requirement adds a second layer of rigor: every criterion must specify the measurement instrument and the artifact access path. This ensures that the data collection method is matched to the criterion before any data is collected, which is the core requirement of Hewlett's Rigor principle.

CROSS reinforces rigor at the gate level. CROSS's causality stance field requires programs to declare, at each gate, whether evidence is being assessed at attribution stance (the intervention caused the change) or contribution stance (the intervention contributed to the change, with co-factors acknowledged). This distinction, which Hewlett's principles address under the question "does the method match the question," is made explicit in the gate configuration rather than left to evaluator discretion.

---

## Principle 3: Credibility

Hewlett's Credibility principle requires that evaluation data be collected independently and transparently, with methods and sources documented clearly enough for external reviewers to assess their validity.

WALKRI's integrity standard (data quality standard 5.2) requires that evidence collection be separated from the entity that benefits from favorable outcomes. This is the structural definition of independent data collection: the measurement instrument must have an access path that does not depend on the grantee's own reporting. For programs using OSO, blockchain data, or other independently accessible sources, this is structurally satisfied. For programs using survey or self-report instruments, WALKRI's integrity standard requires the access path to document how independence is maintained.

CROSS's pre-commitment requirement reinforces credibility at the program level. Evaluation criteria are published before applications open, which means criteria cannot be adjusted after the funder has seen the results. This is the structural guarantee of transparency that Hewlett's Credibility principle requires: the standard against which contributions are assessed was set before contributions were evaluated, not after.

CROSS's coherence disclosure requirement, which requires programs to declare how the program relates to other interventions addressing the same problem area, addresses the transparency dimension of credibility: the evaluation is situated in context rather than presented as if the program were the only relevant actor.

---

## Principle 4: Utility

Hewlett's Utility principle requires that evaluation findings be structured to inform decisions, not to produce reports that sit unread. Evaluation design should begin with the question "who will use this finding, and how," before specifying methods.

CROSS's gate architecture is designed around utility. Each gate produces a determination (funded, not funded, conditional) and a structured record. These are decision outputs, not reports. The four-gate sequence produces findings at the moments when decisions are required: entry (who qualifies), milestone/progress verification (is the work on track), completion (was the obligation fulfilled), continuation (should further support be granted). Each finding is matched to a decision point in the grant lifecycle.

WALKRI's criterion intent requirement, the first of the five pre-publication field requirements, requires each criterion to state why it is being assessed and what decision it informs. This makes the utility design explicit at the field level. A WALKRI-conformant criterion cannot be designed without first specifying the decision it is meant to support.

Together, CROSS and WALKRI produce evaluations that are structured around decision points rather than reporting cycles, which is exactly the orientation Hewlett's Utility principle requires.

---

## Principle 5: Learning Culture

Hewlett's Learning Culture principle requires that evaluations generate usable knowledge enabling mid-course corrections and institutional learning. Evaluation should not be a terminal obligation-fulfillment exercise; it should produce findings that change practice.

CROSS's developmental gate character supports this principle. Progress verification gates in CROSS are configured as developmental rather than summative: they identify what needs to change before the next gate, not only whether the grantee has met a binary threshold. This produces the mid-course learning that Hewlett's principle requires.

The eight obligation dimensions in CROSS, which describe the nature of the obligation being assessed at each gate, provide a structured vocabulary for the kind of comparative learning that Hewlett identifies as institutionally valuable. Programs can compare obligations across grantees, across rounds, and across cohorts using the same structured categories, which is the data architecture required for institutional learning rather than project-level anecdote.

CROSS's Attestation Corpus, which aggregates prior declarations and determinations associated with a grantee's on-chain identity anchor, is a persistent institutional memory that accumulates across grant rounds. This is the structural equivalent of the learning culture Hewlett describes: each round's determinations inform the next round's design.

## The Data Provision Requirement

Hewlett's guide states that grantees are required to cooperate in evaluations and provide information as necessary. This requirement is structurally satisfied by CROSS+WALKRI-conformant programs before it is invoked.

CROSS+WALKRI-conformant programs produce structured, comparable data as a consequence of conformance. The 11-field indicator specification produces documentation in a consistent format. The WALKRI evidence form requirement produces documented access paths to evidence. The gate architecture produces determinations at fixed points in the grant lifecycle. Hewlett evaluators working with CROSS+WALKRI-conformant grantees do not need to make bespoke data requests: the structured data produced by the program's intake and gate architecture is the evaluation data.

This is the strongest argument for the compatibility between CROSS+WALKRI and the Hewlett guide. CROSS+WALKRI does not add an evaluation layer on top of the grant program; it builds the evaluation data structure into the grant program from the beginning.

## The Peer-Funder Implication

The Hewlett guide is published as a normative resource for peer funders, not only as Hewlett's own internal policy. A large share of US foundations use the guide as a reference when designing grant conditions and evaluation requirements. Any funder that uses the Hewlett guide as a reference implicitly requires, for each of its five principles, what CROSS+WALKRI provides structurally.

This is a market positioning claim, not a conformance claim. CROSS+WALKRI is not certified by Hewlett, and Hewlett has not endorsed CROSS+WALKRI. The claim is simply that the obligation architecture CROSS+WALKRI provides is the architecture that Hewlett-aligned funders are already requiring, expressed in a different vocabulary. CROSS+WALKRI makes that architecture explicit, structured, and reusable across programs and funders.

## Outcome Focused Grantmaking (OFG) Framework Alignment

The Hewlett Foundation's Outcome Focused Grantmaking framework (March 2012, archived as a formal program but foundational as a sector reference) defines 10 elements of effective grantmaking organized between a strategic plan and an implementation plan. This framework is the more directly actionable CROSS alignment document because it specifies what grant programs must produce before awards are made, not only how evaluations should be designed.

The OFG's 10 elements and their CROSS mappings:

| OFG Element | CROSS Provision | Scope |
|:--|:--|:--|
| 1. Goals (specific, achievable, measurable outcomes) | CROSS ToC goal level; entry specification gate requires measurable FROM state, TO state, defined population | Full alignment |
| 2. Logic model and theory of change | CROSS Part IX-B ToC architecture (goal/outcome/output/activity with named causal links and confidence tiers) | Full alignment |
| 3. Capacity scan (field and grantee capacity) | CROSS Cohort Position assessment places each applicant against the full cohort; CROSS organizational identity declaration (governance, history, stage) | Partial alignment |
| 4. Metrics and targets | CROSS eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1-5: name, definition, unit, baseline, target) | Full alignment |
| 5. Expected return (ROI estimation) | Outside CROSS scope; CROSS does not specify investment return calculations | Out of scope |
| 6-7. Financial/personnel budget and partner plan | Outside CROSS scope; CROSS covers obligation architecture, not financial planning | Out of scope |
| 8. Due diligence and capacity building plan | CROSS organizational identity declaration, sufficiency position assessment, and disclosure requirements | Partial alignment |
| 9. Monitoring and evaluation plan | CROSS four-gate architecture (entry, progress, completion, continuation) with published evidence requirements per gate | Full alignment |
| 10. Phasing and exit plan | CROSS continuation gate specification: what a grantee must demonstrate to enter subsequent rounds | Partial alignment |

Elements 5, 6, and 7 are financial planning and ROI estimation functions outside the grant specification layer that CROSS covers. All other elements have direct CROSS equivalents. A program using CROSS to govern its obligation architecture produces OFG Elements 1, 2, 4, and 9 as structural outputs of a conformant round, before the first application is submitted.

The OFG framework is archived by Hewlett as a named program but remains widely cited and actively used across the US philanthropy sector as a reference model for outcome-oriented grantmaking. Its core vocabulary (theory of change, metrics and targets, monitoring plan) is the vocabulary that CROSS formalizes at the specification layer.
