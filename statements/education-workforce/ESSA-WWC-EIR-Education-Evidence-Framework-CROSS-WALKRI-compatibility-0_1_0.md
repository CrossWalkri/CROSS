# US Federal Education Evidence Framework (ESSA, WWC, and EIR)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The US federal education evidence framework consists of three interlocking instruments: the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) statutory evidence tiers, the What Works Clearinghouse (WWC) methodology for rating studies against those tiers, and the Education Innovation and Research (EIR) program, which operationalizes the ESSA tiers within a competitive grant program with escalating evidence requirements across three funding levels. These instruments are not independent: ESSA sets the statutory definitions, WWC provides the rating methodology, and EIR applies both within a program architecture that requires progressively stronger evidence to advance from Early-Phase to Mid-Phase to Expansion funding. Together they constitute the most fully articulated tiered evidence obligation architecture in US domestic grant-making. CROSS+WALKRI aligns with this framework at the structural level: ESSA Tier 4's logic model requirement corresponds to CROSS's entry specification gate, ESSA Tiers 1-3 correspond to CROSS's causality stance field and evidence strength taxonomy, EIR's tiered escalation corresponds to CROSS's continuation gate architecture, and WALKRI's pre-publication requirements address the specification problem that underdefined "ongoing evaluation" commitments create at the Tier 4 entry point.

## The US Federal Education Evidence Framework

The US federal education evidence framework is codified at 20 USC 7801 (the ESSA definitions title), operationalized at 34 CFR 77.1 (the Department of Education's definitions regulation applicable to all ED discretionary grants, not only ESSA programs), reviewed against published methodology by the What Works Clearinghouse at IES (ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc), and applied within a competitive grant program through the Education Innovation and Research program. These three instruments are designed to function together, and they cannot be understood in isolation from one another.

**ESSA Evidence Standards.** ESSA's four statutory evidence tiers are defined at 20 USC 7801(21). Tier 1 (Strong Evidence) requires findings from a well-designed and well-implemented randomized controlled trial. Tier 2 (Moderate Evidence) requires findings from a well-designed and well-implemented quasi-experimental study. Tier 3 (Promising Evidence) requires findings from a well-designed and well-implemented correlational study with a comparison group. Tier 4 (Demonstrates a Rationale) requires that an intervention be based on a well-specified logic model or theory of change supported by research or evaluation, with an ongoing high-quality study committed to. Under 34 CFR 77.1, these tier definitions apply to all US Department of Education discretionary grants subject to the evidence-based intervention requirement, not only programs operating under ESSA titles.

**What Works Clearinghouse.** The WWC is published by the Institute of Education Sciences and maintains the primary federal evidence quality methodology for education research: the Standards Handbook Version 4.0. The Standards Handbook specifies study design requirements for each tier, attrition thresholds for randomized and quasi-experimental studies, outcome measure quality requirements, and statistical analysis and reporting standards. When a grant competition requires Tier 1 evidence, it is WWC ratings that determine whether a specific study qualifies. WWC and ESSA are interlocking by design: ESSA sets the statutory thresholds, and WWC provides the methodology for rating studies against those thresholds.

**Education Innovation and Research Program.** EIR is a competitive grant program operated by the Department of Education with three funding tiers carrying escalating evidence requirements. Early-Phase grants require that the proposed intervention demonstrate promising evidence at ESSA Tier 3 or above from prior work. Mid-Phase grants require moderate evidence at ESSA Tier 2 from prior work and require the grantee to implement a rigorous independent evaluation meeting WWC standards during the grant period. Expansion grants require that the prior mid-phase grant demonstrated sizable, significant impacts from a well-designed evaluation meeting WWC standards. EIR publishes annual performance measures for all grantees, including the percentage reaching target student numbers, the percentage maintaining well-designed independent evaluations, the percentage implementing cost-effectiveness evaluations, and the cost per student served. These published performance measures are a funder-side transparency obligation that the EIR program commits to regardless of grantee findings.

## How CROSS Satisfies the US Federal Education Evidence Framework

CROSS addresses the ESSA tier architecture, the WWC methodology requirements, and the EIR tiered escalation at the structural level.

**ESSA Tier 4 and the entry specification gate.** ESSA Tier 4 requires that an intervention be based on a well-specified logic model or theory of change, with an ongoing high-quality study committed to. This is the exact requirement CROSS's entry specification gate implements. CROSS requires that the logic model or theory of change be declared before any application opens, including the FROM state (the baseline condition), the TO state (the target outcome), the population scope, and the causal pathway connecting activities to outcomes. CROSS additionally requires that a high-quality study commitment take the form of a fully specified indicator set, with all eleven fields complete at the entry gate, before any grantee is selected. A CROSS-conformant program operating at the Tier 4 evidence level satisfies the logic model and ongoing study commitment requirements as a structural consequence of gate conformance.

**ESSA Tiers 1-3 and the causality stance field.** ESSA Tiers 1 through 3 distinguish between study designs in descending causal strength: RCT, quasi-experimental, and correlational with comparison group. CROSS's causality stance field requires every program to declare at the entry gate whether it claims attribution (causal) or contribution (associational) and to specify the counterfactual baseline against which change is measured. The causality stance field maps directly to the ESSA tier structure: a program claiming attribution with an RCT design is operating at Tier 1; a program claiming attribution with a quasi-experimental design is operating at Tier 2; a program claiming contribution with a comparison group is operating at Tier 3. The declaration is made at the entry gate, before applications open, and is locked by the disaggregation ratchet and the gate architecture, which prevents retroactive weakening of causal claims after data collection has begun.

**EIR tiered escalation and the continuation gate.** EIR's three-tier escalation architecture, requiring progressively stronger evidence to advance from Early-Phase to Mid-Phase to Expansion, is a program-level operationalization of what CROSS's continuation specification gate requires. CROSS's continuation gate specifies what a grantee must demonstrate at the end of one funding period to qualify for the next, including the evidence type, strength, and comparison group required. An EIR program using CROSS configures the continuation gate to require: for Early-Phase to Mid-Phase progression, evidence meeting WWC quasi-experimental standards (ESSA Tier 2); for Mid-Phase to Expansion progression, sizable and significant impact findings from the required independent evaluation. The escalating evidence standards are built into the continuation gate specification, not left to reviewer discretion at each stage transition.

**WWC attrition thresholds and evidence strength taxonomy.** CROSS's evidence strength taxonomy, which covers output evidence, usage evidence, outcome evidence, and impact evidence in escalating strength, maps to the WWC's study design requirements across the ESSA tiers. WWC attrition thresholds for RCT studies (low attrition defined as under 20% overall and under 7% differential) correspond to CROSS's completion gate evidence review, which assesses whether the evidence submitted at completion matches the design committed to at entry. A program that specified an RCT design at the entry gate but experienced attrition exceeding WWC thresholds must disclose this in its completion gate record; the causality stance field and evidence strength declaration constrain how the results may be characterized.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

ESSA Tier 4's ongoing study commitment is the most commonly underspecified requirement in the federal education evidence framework. Programs declare an ongoing evaluation in their applications without specifying what the evaluation will measure, how it will measure it, who will conduct it, or what evidence standard the results must meet. The commitment is procedurally present but substantively empty. WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements applied to EIR intake fields resolve this problem at the source: before any grantee submits a proposal, the intake field collecting the ongoing evaluation commitment must specify criterion intent (what the evaluation must assess), operational definition (what counts as a qualifying study design), response form (how the applicant documents the evaluation design), evidence form (what documentation of the evaluation plan is required), and compliance threshold (what study design and evidence standard the committed evaluation must meet). A WALKRI-conformant EIR intake field for the ongoing evaluation commitment produces evaluation commitments that are substantively preregistered, not merely declared.

The EIR Mid-Phase requirement that grantees implement a rigorous independent evaluation meeting WWC standards creates a secondary WALKRI problem: reviewers must assess whether a proposed evaluation design meets WWC standards at the application stage, before any evaluation has been conducted. Without a WALKRI-conformant reviewer guidance document specifying what "meets WWC standards" means for each study type at the proposal stage, inter-reviewer variability is high and applicant uncertainty about the required standard is structural rather than incidental. WALKRI's compliance threshold requirement, applied to the reviewer-facing guidance for the Mid-Phase evaluation requirement, forces the program to specify, before any applications are reviewed, exactly what design elements an independent evaluation must include to satisfy the WWC standards threshold.
