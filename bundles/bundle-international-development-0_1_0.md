# CROSS+WALKRI Program Bundle: International Development

---

## Overview

This bundle covers grant and funding programs that distribute resources from bilateral aid agencies, multilateral development banks, humanitarian response mechanisms, and private development philanthropy. The ecosystem includes bilateral programs (USAID, DFAT, JICA, AFD, GIZ, Global Affairs Canada, SDC, Sida, Norad, Danida, IDRC), multilateral development bank grants (World Bank, ADB, AfDB, IDB, EBRD, GCF, GEF), humanitarian response grants (OCHA CBPF, programs operating under the Core Humanitarian Standard), and private development philanthropy operating in development contexts (Gates, Ford, Packard, MacArthur, Rockefeller).

Four program sub-types operate in this space with materially different obligation structures. This bundle addresses all four and notes where provisions diverge.

**Sub-type A: Bilateral aid programs** distribute resources from national aid agencies against development objectives that must satisfy both the donor government's strategic priorities and the OECD DAC five criteria: relevance, effectiveness, efficiency, coherence, and impact (plus sustainability). These programs almost universally operate under a Change obligation mode.

**Sub-type B: Multilateral development bank grants** distribute resources from shared institutional pools (World Bank trust funds, ADB grants, AfDB grants, GCF and GEF allocations) against development outcomes defined in project appraisal documents. Results frameworks are mandatory and must be submitted before the project approval gate.

**Sub-type C: Humanitarian response grants** distribute resources under emergency conditions through mechanisms like OCHA Country-Based Pooled Funds and must satisfy the Core Humanitarian Standard's nine commitments, including Commitment 6 on coordination and coherence. Time constraints compress the specification cycle but do not eliminate the pre-commitment requirement.

**Sub-type D: Private development philanthropy** distributes resources from endowments and donor-advised funds in development contexts. These programs have the most flexibility in obligation mode selection and often choose a mixed architecture: Build mode for capacity-building components and Change mode for population-level outcome components.

## Part 1: CROSS Runbook

### Obligation Mode Selection

**Sub-type A (bilateral aid programs):** Use Change obligation mode. Bilateral aid programs are designed to produce measurable changes in population conditions: health outcomes, educational attainment, income levels, governance quality, or resilience indicators. Build mode is permissible for components that produce specific infrastructure or policy outputs, but the primary obligation of a bilateral grant must be stated in Change terms. The Theory of Change hierarchy (activities, outputs, outcomes, goals) must be declared before any application cycle opens; the hierarchy is the structural pre-commitment from which all indicators flow.

**Sub-type B (multilateral development bank grants):** Use Change obligation mode for the primary obligation. MDB project appraisal documents require a results framework before board approval; this framework is the entry specification gate document. Build mode applies to project components that produce specified physical or institutional outputs (infrastructure, legal frameworks, government systems), but the project-level obligation remains Change: what condition in the target population or system changes as a result.

**Sub-type C (humanitarian response grants):** Use Build obligation mode for life-saving response activities where the deliverable is the service itself (food packets distributed, shelter kits delivered, protection cases managed). Use Change obligation mode when the program design extends beyond immediate response into recovery or resilience, where measurable condition change in the affected population is the stated objective. The time-compressed nature of humanitarian programming requires that obligation mode be selected at the sector cluster coordination level before individual grants are allocated.

**Sub-type D (private development philanthropy):** Use Build or Change obligation mode depending on the grant's primary purpose. For organizational capacity grants: Build mode (the deliverable is enhanced organizational capability). For programmatic grants aimed at community outcomes: Change mode. For research grants within development philanthropy: see the Research Grants bundle. For multi-year general operating support: consider the Retroactive obligation mode for periodic recognition of demonstrated organizational contribution.

### Entry Specification Gate

Before any application window opens, the following must be committed to in writing and published in a verifiable location (the funder's official program documentation, a government gazette, or a publicly accessible repository):

**Program specification:**
- Obligation mode (Build, Change, or mixed, by component)
- Total funding envelope and currency
- Eligible geographic scope and target population
- Development objective and Theory of Change hierarchy (activities through goals)
- OECD DAC criteria as the evaluation anchors, with the funder's interpretation of each criterion for this program
- Indicators at each level of the Theory of Change, specified to eleven fields before the application window opens
- Attribution or contribution stance: does the program claim to cause the outcome (attribution) or to contribute to an outcome alongside other actors (contribution)? This must be declared before applications open because it determines the counterfactual baseline requirement
- Disaggregation requirements: minimum disaggregation categories for all indicators (gender at minimum; age, disability, and geographic disaggregation as required by the specific program)
- IATI compliance requirement: where machine-readable activity data is required by the donor government's IATI commitments, this must be stated as a compliance condition before applications open

**Concurrent funding and co-financing:**
International development programs routinely involve co-financing and parallel funding from multiple donors. All of the following must be disclosed at application:
- Co-financing commitments from other bilateral donors for the same project or scope
- Parallel funding streams from multilateral agencies covering the same geographic area and sector
- Government counterpart contributions (in-kind and financial)
- Private sector contributions in public-private partnership structures
- Any funding from the same bilateral donor through a separate mechanism for overlapping scope

**MEAL plan reference:** Where the funder requires a Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability, and Learning plan, the MEAL plan structure and minimum requirements must be published as part of the entry specification gate documentation. The MEAL plan is the operational document for the completion gate.

### Application Gate

**Theory of Change indicator specification:** Each indicator in the results framework must be specified to all eleven fields. Fields 1 through 5 cover the indicator's identity: name, definition, unit, baseline, and target. Fields 6 through 11 cover its measurement architecture: timing (when measurement occurs relative to program activities), data source (which institution or system produces the underlying data), collection method (survey, administrative record, direct observation, or remote sensing), comparison group (for contribution stance: what the situation would have been without the intervention), causality stance (attribution or contribution, specific to this indicator), and evidence threshold (the minimum evidence quality required to accept the indicator as satisfied at the completion gate).

**Beneficiary population specification:** The application must declare the target population with the disaggregation categories required by the program's entry specification. Where gender disaggregation is required, the application must specify the gender-disaggregated baseline for the primary indicators and the gender-disaggregated targets. Where geographic disaggregation is required (sub-national, district, or community level), the application must specify the geographic scope of activities and outcomes at that resolution.

**Local partner and implementation structure:** The application must name the implementing organization(s), specify the role and responsibilities of each, and declare whether implementation is direct (the applying organization implements) or through local partners (sub-grantees, contracted service providers, government counterparts). Where local partners are primary implementers, the accountability structure for partner performance against the results framework must be stated.

**Attribution or contribution declaration:** The application must confirm the causality stance declared in the entry specification gate and provide the counterfactual baseline: what condition exists in the target population without this intervention, and how that baseline was established. For contribution stance claims, the application must name the other actors contributing to the same outcome and describe the theory by which this program's contribution is distinguishable.

### Completion Gate

**Evidence against the results framework:** The completion gate assessment must compare final indicator values against the pre-specified targets using the data sources and collection methods declared at the entry specification gate. Substituting an alternative data source without prior disclosure constitutes a specification deviation. If the primary data source becomes unavailable during the program period, the substitution must be disclosed and justified before the final reporting date.

**Disaggregation at completion:** All indicators specified with disaggregation requirements must be reported at the disaggregated level at completion. A completion report that presents only aggregate figures where disaggregated figures were required does not satisfy the completion gate. Where sample sizes in disaggregation categories are too small for reliable estimates, this limitation must be disclosed with the best available estimate and its confidence interval.

**Attribution or contribution evidence:** For programs operating under attribution stance: the completion evidence must include the counterfactual comparison (comparison group data) specified at the entry gate. For programs operating under contribution stance: the completion evidence must include a contribution analysis that distinguishes the program's share of the observed outcome from other contributing factors, referencing the comparison group declaration in the entry specification.

**Additionality declaration:** Where additionality was declared as part of the program's public benefit claim (what this grant produces beyond what would have occurred without it), the completion report must demonstrate additionality: what evidence shows that the outcomes achieved would not have been achieved, or would have been achieved more slowly or at lower quality, without this program's specific contribution.

**IATI submission (Sub-types A and B):** Where IATI compliance was stated as a condition at the entry gate, machine-readable activity data must be published to the IATI Registry before the completion gate assessment. The IATI publication constitutes the machine-readable attestation record for the program.

### Attestation Gate and Corpus

The Attestation Corpus for international development grants must be structured to satisfy both the funder's accountability requirements and the host-country government's program coordination requirements:

- Program documentation: the entry specification gate document, including the results framework with all eleven indicator fields, stored in the funder's official program repository
- Application record: the approved application including the Theory of Change, indicator specifications, beneficiary population declaration, and concurrent funding disclosure
- Mid-term review (where required): independent review findings against pre-specified indicators, stored alongside the application record
- Completion report: final indicator values against targets, with disaggregated data and contribution or attribution analysis
- Independent evaluation (where required): third-party evaluation findings, stored in the funder's evaluation repository and, where public, accessible to the OECD DAC Evaluation Registry
- IATI publication: machine-readable activity record published to the IATI Registry (for Sub-types A and B with IATI requirements)

Where the host-country government operates a Development Cooperation Information Management System or equivalent national aid coordination platform, the program record must be registered before activities begin.

### Continuation Gate

For programs that extend into subsequent phases or that fund recurring grantees:

A continuing program must demonstrate: delivery of all prior phase commitments against the results framework, resolution of all concurrent funding disclosures (co-financing commitments fulfilled or disclosed as changed), and an updated Theory of Change incorporating lessons from the prior phase. Where an independent mid-term review identified recommendations, the continuation application must address each recommendation and state whether it has been incorporated into the next phase design.

A continuing program operator must demonstrate: the entry specification gate for the new phase is published before the previous phase closes, disaggregation requirements are maintained or strengthened (the disaggregation ratchet: disaggregation may be extended but not contracted without justification), and the attestation corpus for the previous phase is complete and accessible to the host-country government and any co-financing donors.

## Part 2: WALKRI Field Specifications

The following field specifications cover the intake fields most commonly required in international development grant programs. Each specification satisfies WALKRI's five pre-publication requirements: criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, compliance threshold.

---

### Field: Beneficiary Population Scope and Demographic Disaggregation

**Criterion intent:** Establishes who the program is designed to serve, with the demographic detail required to assess equity of access and to satisfy the funder's disaggregation requirements. The population scope declaration is the reference point against which all outcome indicators are anchored.

**Operational definition:** The beneficiary population is the defined group whose conditions the program is designed to change. Scope has four dimensions: geographic (the area within which beneficiaries reside or operate), demographic (the characteristics that define eligibility or targeting), quantitative (the number of individuals or households to be served), and temporal (the period during which individuals are considered active beneficiaries). Demographic disaggregation is the decomposition of the population by characteristics relevant to equity: gender, age cohort, disability status, geographic sub-unit, income quintile, or ethnicity as required by the program's entry specification.

**Response form:** A population scope statement specifying: the geographic boundary (country, region, district, or community), the total estimated beneficiary count (with basis for the estimate), and the disaggregation breakdown for each required category. For gender disaggregation: the estimated percentage of direct beneficiaries who are women and girls. For geographic disaggregation: the list of sub-national units covered with estimated beneficiary counts per unit.

**Evidence form:** For baseline disaggregation: the data source for demographic estimates (national census, household survey, administrative registry, or community mapping). For direct count targets: the targeting methodology that explains how the stated number will be reached. For indirect or systems-level beneficiaries: the theory by which the population count is estimated, with the assumptions made explicit.

**Compliance threshold:** A population scope declaration that specifies all required disaggregation categories with baseline estimates. A declaration that states only total beneficiary counts without the required gender or geographic breakdown does not satisfy this field. Estimates without a stated data source do not satisfy the evidence form requirement.

---

### Field: Theory of Change Indicator Ladder

**Criterion intent:** Establishes the causal chain from program activities through outputs and outcomes to the development goal, with indicators specified at each level before applications open, so that the results framework is a pre-commitment rather than a post-hoc description.

**Operational definition:** The Theory of Change hierarchy has four levels: activities (what the program does), outputs (the direct products of activities, within the program's control), outcomes (the changes in behavior, knowledge, access, or condition produced by outputs, partially within the program's influence), and goals (the broader development change to which the program contributes, beyond the program's direct influence). An indicator ladder maps at least one indicator per level for each primary results chain. Each indicator must be specified to all eleven fields before applications open.

**Response form:** A results framework table presenting the indicator ladder. For each indicator: Fields 1 through 5 (name, definition, unit, baseline, target) presented in the table; Fields 6 through 11 (timing, data source, collection method, comparison group, causality stance, evidence threshold) presented as supplementary indicator specification sheets. The table must show the logical linkage between levels: how achieving the output indicators is expected to produce the outcome indicators, and how the outcome contributes to the goal.

**Evidence form:** The baseline values for output and outcome indicators must be sourced from an identified data source cited in Field 7. Where no existing data source covers the baseline, the program must describe the data collection activity that will establish the baseline before the first implementation reporting period. For goal-level indicators: the data source is typically a national or international statistical system (DHS, MICS, World Bank WDI, or equivalent); the program should reference the most recent available estimate.

**Compliance threshold:** A results framework that covers all four levels of the Theory of Change hierarchy, with at least one indicator per level specified to all eleven fields. A framework that specifies activities and outputs but presents outcomes only narratively without indicators does not satisfy this field. A framework whose indicators were specified after the application window opened does not satisfy the entry specification gate requirement.

---

### Field: Attribution or Contribution Stance with Counterfactual Baseline

**Criterion intent:** Declares whether the program claims to cause the measured outcome (attribution) or to contribute to it alongside other actors (contribution), and establishes the counterfactual baseline against which the causal claim can be assessed at completion.

**Operational definition:** Attribution is the claim that the measured change in the outcome indicator was produced by this program's activities and would not have occurred without them. It requires a comparison group that experienced no program exposure. Contribution is the claim that this program's activities contributed to a measured change that also reflects the influence of other actors and enabling conditions. It requires a contribution analysis that identifies the other actors and estimates the program's share. The counterfactual baseline is the estimate of what the outcome indicator would have been in the absence of the program; it must be established before activities begin.

**Response form:** A declaration of the causality stance (attribution or contribution) for each primary outcome indicator, with the comparison group or contribution analysis approach specified. For attribution claims: identification of the comparison group (geographic area, matched cohort, or randomized control condition) and how it was selected. For contribution claims: identification of the other actors contributing to the same outcome and the method for isolating the program's contribution (most significant change analysis, contribution analysis, process tracing, or equivalent).

**Evidence form:** For attribution stance: documentation of the comparison group selection process, including the data source for baseline similarity assessment. For contribution stance: the contribution analysis framework, naming the other actors and their estimated contribution shares. For both: the counterfactual baseline value for each primary outcome indicator, with the data source and collection date.

**Compliance threshold:** A causality stance declaration that covers all primary outcome indicators, with a counterfactual baseline established from a named data source before activities begin. A completion report that claims attribution without a comparison group, or that claims contribution without a contribution analysis, does not satisfy the completion gate evidence requirement. Programs that change their causality stance during implementation (shifting from attribution to contribution or vice versa) must file a specification change before the new stance applies.

---

### Field: Gender and Equity Disaggregation Plan

**Criterion intent:** Establishes how the program will collect, report, and use gender-disaggregated and equity-disaggregated data throughout the program cycle, satisfying both the funder's reporting requirements and the program's own commitment to equity of access and outcome.

**Operational definition:** Gender disaggregation is the collection and reporting of indicator values separately for women and men (and, where applicable, other gender categories recognized in the target context). Equity disaggregation extends this to other dimensions of inequality: age cohort, disability status, income quintile, geographic sub-unit, ethnicity, or any other dimension the program has identified as a source of differential access or outcome. The disaggregation plan specifies which indicators will be disaggregated, by which categories, at which data collection points, using which data collection instruments.

**Response form:** A disaggregation plan table identifying: each indicator that will be disaggregated, the disaggregation categories applied to that indicator, the data collection instrument that captures the required categories (survey form, administrative registry field, or direct observation protocol), the data collection timing, and the reporting format (whether disaggregated data will appear in quarterly reports, annual reviews, and the final evaluation).

**Evidence form:** The data collection instruments (survey questionnaires, monitoring forms, registration systems) must include the fields required to capture the disaggregation categories stated in the plan. Instruments that do not include these fields do not satisfy the evidence form requirement, even if the plan states the intent to disaggregate. Where instruments are under development, the specification of required fields constitutes provisional compliance pending instrument finalization.

**Compliance threshold:** A disaggregation plan that covers all indicators subject to the funder's disaggregation requirements, with data collection instruments that include the required category fields. The ratchet applies: the disaggregation plan may add categories beyond the minimum required but may not reduce below the minimum. A program that reports only aggregate figures at completion when disaggregated figures were required does not satisfy the completion gate.

---

### Field: Co-financing and Concurrent Donor Funding Disclosure

**Criterion intent:** Documents all parallel and complementary funding from other sources covering the same geographic scope, sector, or target population, enabling assessment of additionality and preventing double-counting of the same activities across multiple donor reports.

**Operational definition:** Co-financing is funding from another source that is formally linked to this program and contributes to the same results framework. Parallel funding is funding from another source that covers the same geographic area and sector without a formal link to this program's results framework. Both constitute concurrent funding for CROSS purposes. The disclosure covers all such funding active during the program period, including government counterpart contributions, other bilateral grants, multilateral allocations, private sector contributions in partnership structures, and any other resources the implementing organization is deploying in the same scope during the same period.

**Response form:** A disclosure table listing each concurrent funding source with: source name (donor government, multilateral agency, foundation, or private entity), funding amount or in-kind value, currency, sector and geographic coverage, program period, the results chain or activities that overlap with this application's scope, and the formal relationship (co-financing under a joint results framework, parallel funding, or government counterpart contribution). If no concurrent funding sources exist: an explicit declaration to that effect.

**Evidence form:** For co-financing: the co-financing agreement or letter of commitment. For parallel funding: the funder's program documentation establishing the geographic and sectoral overlap. For government counterpart contributions: the government budget allocation or official letter of commitment. The disclosure table is the applicant's declaration; the cited documents are the supporting evidence. Non-disclosure discovered after award is treated as a material omission requiring a corrective action process.

**Compliance threshold:** A disclosure that covers all concurrent funding sources active during the program period. A table that omits a co-financing arrangement the applicant is party to does not satisfy this field. "None" is a compliant response only when the implementing organization has no other funding covering the declared scope during the declared period.

## Part 3: Compatibility Map

This section maps the international development program type to the frameworks in the CROSS+WALKRI compatibility corpus that are most directly applicable. The map is organized by primitive.

---

### Pre-specification Primitive

The pre-specification primitive requires that indicators, criteria, and impact metrics be committed to before any applicant submits. International development programs that implement this primitive most rigorously:

- **USAID PIRS (Performance Indicator Reference Sheets):** indicator-level specification documents required before data collection begins; each PIRS covers definition, unit, disaggregation, data source, collection method, and frequency; the eleven CROSS indicator fields map directly onto PIRS structure
- **World Bank Results Framework:** project-level results chain required before board approval; indicators specified at PDO level and intermediate results level
- **UNDP IRRF (Integrated Results and Resources Framework):** integrated results framework required before project document approval
- **OECD DAC evaluation criteria:** five criteria (relevance, effectiveness, efficiency, coherence, impact) published as the evaluation anchor before any project begins; not project-specific pre-specification but sector-level commitment
- **CROSS itself:** entry specification gate as the canonical implementation of this primitive

*Compatibility statements:* USAID-PIRS, World-Bank-RF, UNDP-IRRF, OECD-DAC-Criteria, Global-Affairs-Canada-PMF.

---

### Theory of Change Hierarchy Primitive

The Theory of Change hierarchy (activities, outputs, outcomes, goals) is the structural pre-commitment from which all indicators flow in international development programming:

- **Kellogg Logic Model:** activities and inputs through outputs to outcomes and impact; widely referenced in NGO and foundation programming; compatible with CROSS hierarchy
- **USAID Results Framework:** outputs through intermediate results to the development objective; maps onto CROSS outcomes-to-goal level
- **Logframe/LFA (Logical Framework Approach):** activities, outputs, purpose (outcome), and goal; the standard tool of bilateral aid programming; Fields 1-5 of the CROSS indicator specification map directly onto the logframe indicator column
- **JICA ODA evaluation:** project purpose, outputs, activities, and inputs; comparable structure to the logframe; JICA evaluations reference OECD DAC criteria as the five-criterion anchor

*Compatibility statements:* Kellogg-Logic-Model, USAID-Results-Framework, LFA-Logframe, JICA-ODA-Evaluation, AFD-Evaluation-Policy.

---

### Attribution or Contribution Primitive and Causality Stance

Most international development programs claim contribution rather than attribution because attribution requires experimental or quasi-experimental designs that are often infeasible in complex development environments:

- **OECD DAC (contribution stance as the default):** DAC evaluation guidance recognizes contribution analysis as the standard approach for development programs; attribution is reserved for programs with experimental designs
- **Verra VCS (attribution stance):** voluntary carbon markets require attribution for credit issuance; this is a stricter standard that development programs using ecosystem services or REDD+ must satisfy
- **REDD+ (counterfactual baseline as the primary evidence requirement):** the reference level in REDD+ is the counterfactual baseline for deforestation; CROSS's counterfactual baseline field maps directly onto the REDD+ reference level concept
- **CGIAR MELIA (contribution analysis in agricultural research):** CGIAR's approach to evaluating research impact uses contribution analysis as the standard; multi-actor systems make attribution impractical at scale

*Compatibility statements:* OECD-DAC-Evaluation-Guidance, Verra-VCS-Standard, REDD-Plus-Reference-Level, CGIAR-MELIA.

---

### Disaggregation Primitive

The disaggregation ratchet applies at minimum at gender level in all bilateral development programs; many require additional dimensions:

- **ESF+ Common Indicators (European Social Fund+):** standardized disaggregation by gender, age, disability, educational attainment, and employment status for EU-funded social programs; these categories serve as a reference for European bilateral donors
- **PEPFAR MER (Monitoring, Evaluation, and Reporting):** the most extensively specified disaggregation system in development assistance; age and gender disaggregation required for all PEPFAR indicators; fine-grained age bands (0-4, 5-9, 10-14...) exceed standard development program requirements
- **HRSA Title V NPMs (National Performance Measures):** maternal and child health disaggregation by race and ethnicity in US domestic programs; a reference for private development philanthropy operating in US health equity contexts

*Compatibility statements:* ESF-Plus-Common-Indicators, PEPFAR-MER, HRSA-Title-V-NPMs, IDRC-Research-Quality-Plus.

---

### Independent Verification Primitive

International development programs have varied but often rigorous independent verification requirements:

- **CGIAR MELIA (Monitoring, Evaluation, Learning, and Impact Assessment):** systematic independent review process for CGIAR research programs; evaluations are public and feed into portfolio-level learning
- **DFAT Australia three-tier PAF (Performance Assessment Framework):** three-tier review structure at program, sector, and portfolio levels; independent evaluation at the program tier feeds into the sector-level assessment
- **UNTF (UN Trust Fund) independent review process:** independent review commissioned by the trust fund administrator; findings are published and accessible to all contributing donors

*Compatibility statements:* CGIAR-MELIA, DFAT-Three-Tier-PAF, UNTF-Independent-Review, Nordic-Bilateral-Evaluation-Standards.

---

### Portfolio Aggregation Primitive

International development funders aggregate across programs to assess portfolio-level impact:

- **ESIF performance framework (European Structural and Investment Funds):** portfolio-level performance indicators required for each EU funding period; individual project indicators must nest within the portfolio framework
- **MDB group portfolio reporting:** World Bank, ADB, AfDB, and IDB all require program-level results that aggregate to the country partnership framework or sector strategy level
- **Global Affairs Canada ODAAA/PMF (Official Development Assistance Accountability Act and Performance Measurement Framework):** Canada's statutory framework for ODA accountability; program indicators must feed into the PMF's country and thematic aggregations

*Compatibility statements:* ESIF-Performance-Framework, World-Bank-Country-Partnership-Framework, Global-Affairs-Canada-ODAAA.

---

### Coherence and Coordination Disclosure Primitive

Coherence, the fifth OECD DAC criterion, requires that programs not undermine the effects of other interventions:

- **OECD DAC coherence criterion:** requires programs to assess whether they reinforce or contradict the objectives of other development actors and host-country policies; CROSS's concurrent funding disclosure is a structural operationalization of this criterion
- **Core Humanitarian Standard (Commitment 6):** requires humanitarian organizations to coordinate with other actors to avoid duplication and gaps; a direct application of the coherence primitive in the humanitarian sub-type
- **IATI Standard (International Aid Transparency Initiative):** machine-readable activity data enables coherence assessment across the full portfolio of donors active in a country; IATI publication is the portfolio-level coherence disclosure mechanism

*Compatibility statements:* OECD-DAC-Coherence-Criterion, Core-Humanitarian-Standard-Commitment-6, IATI-Standard.

---

## New Primitives Relevant to International Development Programs

**IATI as the attestation corpus for portfolio coherence:** The IATI Standard requires machine-readable publication of activity data including funding amounts, implementing organizations, geographic coordinates, sector codes, and results data. For bilateral development programs, IATI publication constitutes the machine-readable layer of the attestation corpus. Programs building development grant management infrastructure should treat IATI publication as a first-class output of the completion gate, not an afterthought. The IATI Activity Standard's result element maps directly onto CROSS's completion gate evidence requirements.

**The disaggregation ratchet applied across a program lifecycle:** In development programming, disaggregation requirements typically increase rather than decrease as programs mature: a program might begin with gender disaggregation only, add geographic disaggregation at the mid-term review, and add disability disaggregation for the final phase. The CROSS disaggregation ratchet formalizes this norm: disaggregation may be extended but not contracted without a documented justification. Programs designing multi-phase development grants should build the ratchet into the continuation gate requirements explicitly.
