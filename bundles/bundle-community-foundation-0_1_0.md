# CROSS+WALKRI Program Bundle: Community Foundation

---

## Overview

This bundle covers grant and funding programs operated by community foundations distributing from donor-advised funds and endowments, trust-based and equity-focused grantmakers, place-based community development programs, and participatory grantmaking initiatives. The ecosystem includes community foundations distributing from donor-advised funds and endowments under the Community Foundation National Standards and the Council on Foundations, trust-based and equity-focused grantmakers following the Trust-Based Philanthropy Project principles and NCRP racial equity grantmaking criteria, place-based community development programs using CDBG, CDFI, and CSBG ROMA frameworks, and participatory grantmaking initiatives where community members function as decision-makers rather than only as beneficiaries.

Four program sub-types operate in this space with materially different obligation structures. This bundle addresses all four, with particular attention to the productive tension between Trust-Based Philanthropy's emphasis on unrestricted general operating support and CROSS's pre-specification requirement.

**Sub-type A: Community foundation grantmaking** distributes endowment income and donor-advised fund grants to nonprofit organizations serving a defined geographic community. These programs typically fund a mix of organizational capacity (Build mode) and community outcomes (Change mode). The geographic boundary of the community is the primary scope declaration.

**Sub-type B: Trust-based and equity-focused grantmaking** distributes multi-year unrestricted general operating support to organizations working on racial equity, social justice, and systemic change. Trust-Based Philanthropy emphasizes reducing burden, building trust, and sharing power with grantees. CROSS handles this sub-type through the Retroactive obligation mode for unrestricted recognition and the sufficiency position declaration for general operating support. The tension between TBP principles and pre-specification is real and is addressed directly in this bundle.

**Sub-type C: Place-based community development** distributes resources from CDBG (Community Development Block Grant), CDFI (Community Development Financial Institution) Fund grants, and community action agency programs using CSBG ROMA (Community Services Block Grant Results-Oriented Management and Accountability). These programs are governed by federal accountability frameworks that pre-specify the national outcome goals against which all local activity is measured.

**Sub-type D: Participatory grantmaking** designs the grant review and decision-making process so that community members with lived experience of the issues being addressed hold decision-making authority over grant awards, not merely advisory roles. The participation structure itself is a pre-specified component of the entry specification gate.

## Part 1: CROSS Runbook

### Obligation Mode Selection

**Sub-type A (community foundation grantmaking):** Use Build obligation mode for organizational capacity grants (the deliverable is a specified enhancement to the organization's capacity: a new program director, an updated strategic plan, a technology system). Use Change obligation mode for programmatic grants (the outcome is a measurable change in the condition of community members served). Many community foundation grants fund both simultaneously; the application must designate an obligation mode for each funded component and specify the indicator at each level.

**Sub-type B (trust-based and equity-focused grantmaking):** The Trust-Based Philanthropy Project's first principle is multi-year unrestricted general operating support. CROSS's entry specification gate requires pre-specification of indicators before awards are made. These two commitments can coexist through two routes. First: for programs that want to maintain TBP principles fully, use the Retroactive obligation mode. The funder recognizes prior organizational contribution against published criteria; the grantee's work continues under its own strategy without project-specific indicators attached. The entry specification gate specifies the criteria for recognizing organizational contribution, not project deliverables. Second: for programs that maintain unrestricted support but want to specify community-level outcomes, use the sufficiency position declaration. The funder declares that the grant is designed to support the organization's capacity to work toward specified community conditions; the community-level indicators are the funder's portfolio-level outcome frame, not the grantee's project indicators. The organization reports on its own terms; the funder assesses community-level change from its own portfolio-level data sources.

**Sub-type C (place-based community development):** Use Change obligation mode for CSBG ROMA programs. The Six National Goals are pre-specified by HHS and do not require local programs to develop their own outcome framework from scratch; the local program must map its activities to the applicable national goals and specify the National Performance Indicators that will be reported. Use Build obligation mode for CDBG infrastructure and housing components (the deliverable is units rehabbed, units constructed, or linear feet of infrastructure). Use Change mode for CDBG public service components (the outcome is changed conditions for low-and-moderate-income persons served).

**Sub-type D (participatory grantmaking):** Use Build or Change obligation mode for the grants that the participatory process awards. The participatory process itself constitutes the entry specification gate for those grants: community decision-makers specify the criteria, review applications, and determine awards. The entry specification gate for the participatory process is a meta-level commitment about how community voice will be structured, weighted, and protected. The obligation mode for the process itself is Build: the deliverable is a grantmaking process that meets the participation standards declared before the cycle opens.

### Entry Specification Gate

Before any application window opens, the following must be committed to in writing and published on the funder's website or in a publicly accessible grant guidelines document:

**Program specification:**
- Obligation mode (Build, Change, Retroactive, or mixed by component)
- Geographic scope: the community boundary within which eligible organizations operate and eligible beneficiaries reside
- Target population with the demographic disaggregation categories that will be required at application and completion (race, income, geography declared upfront; disability and age if required by the program)
- Community outcome frame: for community foundation programs with a portfolio-level outcome strategy, the community conditions the funder is working to change must be named and the indicators specified before grant cycles open
- For TBP programs: the criteria for organizational contribution recognition (mission alignment, track record, community rootedness, leadership demographics) must be specified before any organization is approached for a grant
- For participatory programs: the community decision-maker role, the selection process for community reviewers, the weighting of community voice in the award decision, and the process for managing conflicts of interest among community reviewers

**Racial equity disclosure:** For programs explicitly working on racial equity, the racial equity analysis approach must be declared before the grant cycle opens. This includes: which racial groups are the focus of the program's equity goals, what the baseline racial disparities are in the outcome domain, and how the program's theory of change connects its activities to reducing those disparities.

### Application Gate

**Community boundary and population scope:** The application must state the geographic area within which the proposed work will occur and the target population within that area. For programs requiring demographic disaggregation, the application must provide the baseline disaggregated population data for the relevant indicators: the current condition among the target population broken down by the required categories. Where the funder's program is explicit about race or income as targeting criteria, the application must state the demographic composition of the target population.

**Power-building or equity outcome indicators:** For programs using the NCRP racial equity grantmaking criteria or equivalent equity frameworks, the application must specify the power-building or equity outcome indicators to which the grant will contribute. Power-building indicators may include: number of community members engaged in civic action, number of organizations that include program participants in leadership, number of policy changes influenced by the community's advocacy, or measures of community organizational capacity developed through the program. These must be specified with the relevant indicator fields before the application is reviewed.

**Organizational capacity indicators (for capacity-building grants):** Where the grant is explicitly for organizational capacity, the application must specify what capacity will be built and how that capacity will be measured. Acceptable capacity indicators include: staff positions filled, staff retention rates, board composition and diversity, financial reserves as a percentage of annual budget, technology systems implemented, or program delivery capacity measured by service volume. The capacity indicator must be specified to at minimum Fields 1 through 5 (name, definition, unit, baseline, target).

**General operating support use declaration (for unrestricted grants):** For Sub-type B programs providing unrestricted support, the application must include an organizational identity and governance disclosure: the organization's mission, its geographic scope and primary community served, its governance structure (board composition, leadership demographics), its financial position (most recent audited financials or Form 990), and a narrative describing how the general operating support will be used. This disclosure is not a project indicator specification; it is the accountability frame for unrestricted recognition.

### Completion Gate

**Community outcome evidence:** For Change-mode grants, the completion gate assessment must compare final indicator values against the pre-specified targets using the data sources declared at the application gate. Where the organization's activities are one of several contributing factors to a community-level condition change, the completion report must acknowledge the contribution analysis challenge and provide the best available evidence of the organization's contribution: program participation data, beneficiary outcomes measured by the organization, or community-level trend data with a narrative analysis of the organization's role.

**Disaggregation at completion:** All indicators specified with disaggregation requirements must be reported at the disaggregated level. For racial equity programs: the completion report must present outcome data by race for the primary indicators. A completion report that presents only aggregate figures when race-disaggregated data was required does not satisfy the completion gate. Where sample sizes within racial categories are too small for reliable estimates, the limitation must be disclosed with the best available estimate.

**Participatory process completion evidence:** For Sub-type D programs, the completion gate for the grantmaking process includes: documentation that community reviewers with the declared characteristics participated in the review, documentation of the weight applied to community voice in the final award decisions, and a process evaluation assessing whether the participation structure operated as designed. Grants awarded through a process that deviated from the pre-specified participation structure require a disclosure of the deviation.

**Organizational performance for unrestricted grants:** For TBP-aligned programs, the completion gate for unrestricted grants is a periodic check-in rather than an indicator report. The completion assessment should confirm: the organization is still operating within its declared mission and geographic scope, its governance and leadership are consistent with the representation declared at application, and no material changes to the organization's capacity or fiscal health have occurred without disclosure.

### Attestation Gate and Corpus

The Attestation Corpus for community foundation grants should be structured for transparency to the community served as well as to the donor base:

- Grant guidelines: the entry specification gate document published before each cycle opens, archived in the funder's public grant database
- Application record: the approved application with the population scope, indicator specifications, and concurrent funding disclosure, retained in the funder's grant management system
- Progress reports: mid-grant check-ins with indicator updates, retained in the grant file
- Completion report: final indicator values against targets, with disaggregated data; for participatory programs, the process evaluation
- 360Giving data publication: where the funder publishes to the 360Giving Data Standard, the machine-readable grant record constitutes the public attestation corpus
- Community Foundation National Standards accreditation: for Sub-type A programs, accreditation status is itself an attestation that the program meets the national standards for community foundation grantmaking practice

### Continuation Gate

For multi-year grants and for programs that fund organizations in recurring cycles:

A continuing grant must demonstrate: delivery of prior-period commitments against the results framework, updated population scope and demographic disaggregation data (the disaggregation ratchet applies: a continuing grant may not reduce its disaggregation below the prior period without justification), and disclosure of any changes to the organization's governance, leadership, or financial position since the prior application.

A continuing program must demonstrate: the geographic scope and community outcome frame for the new cycle are published before the previous cycle closes, racial equity analysis is updated with the most recent available baseline data, and the participation structure for Sub-type D programs is reviewed and affirmed or modified with community input before the new cycle opens.

## Part 2: WALKRI Field Specifications

The following field specifications cover the intake fields most commonly required in community foundation grant programs. Each specification satisfies WALKRI's five pre-publication requirements: criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, conformance threshold.

---

### Field: Geographic Scope and Community Boundary

**Criterion intent:** Establishes the place-based scope within which the program will operate and within which beneficiaries must reside or be served, so that the funder can assess whether the organization's work is within the program's geographic mandate and can aggregate outcomes across grantees operating in the same community.

**Operational definition:** The geographic scope is the spatial boundary that defines eligibility for service and for organizational operations. At the broadest level: a metropolitan area, county, or multi-county region. At the intermediate level: a city, town, or municipality. At the most specific level: defined neighborhoods, census tracts, or zip codes. The community boundary is the funder's pre-declared service territory; it determines which organizations are eligible to apply and which populations count as beneficiaries for the program's outcome framework. Sub-county geographic scope is required when the program targets areas of concentrated poverty, racially segregated neighborhoods, or specific community geographies where conditions differ substantially from the county average.

**Response form:** A geographic scope declaration specifying: the primary geographic boundary (city, county, zip code, or named neighborhood), the administrative unit (census tract, school district, planning area) if applicable, and a map or GIS boundary file if the scope is not defined by a standard administrative boundary. For organizations operating across multiple geographies: the scope must identify which communities are within the funder's geographic mandate and which are outside it.

**Evidence form:** For standard administrative boundaries (city, county): the boundary is self-defining and no additional evidence is required. For sub-county geographies: a map with labeled boundaries or a census tract list. For organizations serving a mobile or dispersed population (e.g., people experiencing homelessness): a description of the service catchment area and the basis for the population count estimate within the funder's geographic mandate.

**Conformance threshold:** A geographic scope declaration that is specific enough for the funder to determine whether the organization's work falls within the program's geographic mandate. A declaration of "the greater metropolitan region" when the funder's mandate is the city limits does not satisfy this field. A declaration that names specific neighborhoods or zip codes satisfies this field even where the organization also works outside those areas, as long as the in-scope and out-of-scope activities are distinguished.

---

### Field: Target Population with Demographic Disaggregation

**Criterion intent:** Establishes who the program serves, with the demographic detail required to assess equity of access and outcomes, and to satisfy the program's required disaggregation categories.

**Operational definition:** The target population is the defined group within the community boundary whose conditions the program is designed to change or whose organizational capacity the grant will support. Demographic disaggregation is the decomposition of the population by characteristics that are relevant to equity: race and ethnicity, income level (as a percentage of area median income or federal poverty level), age cohort, disability status, or housing status. The specific disaggregation categories required are those declared in the program's entry specification gate; the application must provide the baseline composition of the target population on those dimensions.

**Response form:** A target population description specifying: the total number of direct beneficiaries expected during the grant period, the primary characteristic or circumstances that define eligibility (income level, geographic location, identity, or experience), and the disaggregated composition of the target population by each required category. For racial equity programs: the racial and ethnic composition of the target population, with the baseline prevalence of the outcome condition by racial group. For income-targeted programs: the percentage of participants at or below 100% of the federal poverty level, or within the applicable AMI threshold.

**Evidence form:** The baseline demographic data must come from an identified source: the organization's most recent program year data, a community needs assessment, census data (American Community Survey or Decennial Census), or a local data intermediary report. The data source and date must be cited. Where the organization's own program data is the source: the data collection instrument (intake form, registration system, or equivalent) must capture the required demographic fields.

**Conformance threshold:** A target population declaration that specifies all required disaggregation categories with baseline data from a named source. A declaration that states only total beneficiary counts without the required racial or income breakdown does not satisfy this field. A declaration that states demographic targets without baseline data (stating a target without knowing the current composition) does not satisfy the baseline requirement.

---

### Field: Power-Building or Equity Outcome Indicators

**Criterion intent:** Establishes the specific measurable changes in community power, civic participation, or racial equity outcomes that the program is designed to produce, satisfying the NCRP racial equity grantmaking criteria and equivalent equity frameworks that require equity-specific indicators beyond standard service delivery metrics.

**Operational definition:** A power-building indicator measures a change in the capacity of a community, particularly communities that have been excluded from power, to influence decisions that affect their lives. Power-building outcomes include: increases in civic participation (voting, advocacy, organizational membership), increases in community leadership (residents in leadership roles within organizations and institutions), changes in policies or institutional practices resulting from community-led advocacy, and increases in community organizational capacity and resources under community control. An equity outcome indicator measures a reduction in the disparity between a marginalized group and the overall population (or a reference group) on a specific condition: income, health, educational attainment, housing stability, or civic representation.

**Response form:** One or more power-building or equity outcome indicators, each specified with: a name (what the indicator measures), a definition (how it is operationalized), a unit (count, percentage, or ratio), a baseline (the current value for the target community or the current disparity between the target group and the reference group), and a target (the value the program expects to achieve by the end of the grant period). For equity indicators: the baseline disparity must be stated as the ratio of the outcome rate for the target racial group to the overall rate or the reference group rate.

**Evidence form:** For the baseline: the data source for the current power-building or equity measure (local civic participation survey, organizational membership records, voter registration data, leadership composition audit, or administrative data on the outcome condition by race). For the target: a rationale for the target value referencing either prior program performance, peer organization benchmarks, or a community-defined goal. For policy change indicators: documentation of the policy target being pursued and the advocacy activities that will produce it.

**Conformance threshold:** At least one power-building or equity outcome indicator specified to Fields 1 through 5, with a baseline from a named data source and a target with a stated rationale. Indicators that measure only service delivery outputs (number of workshops held, number of participants attending) without any indicator of changed community capacity or reduced disparity do not satisfy this field for programs explicitly operating in the racial equity or power-building domain.

---

### Field: Community Decision-Maker Role and Weighting in Review Process

**Criterion intent:** Establishes the specific role, selection process, and decision authority of community members in the grantmaking process, satisfying the pre-specification requirement for participatory grantmaking programs and creating accountability for whether the stated participation structure was actually implemented.

**Operational definition:** A community decision-maker is a person with lived experience of the conditions the grant program addresses who holds formal decision-making authority in the grant review process. Authority means a vote or veto that materially affects grant awards, not merely an advisory or consultative role. The weighting of community voice is the fraction of the decision authority held by community decision-makers relative to foundation staff, board members, or external experts. Selection of community reviewers must follow a process that is declared before the grant cycle opens.

**Response form:** A participation structure declaration specifying: the composition of the review body (number of community reviewers, number of foundation staff, number of external experts), the selection process for community reviewers (application, community nomination, demographic criteria), the decision rule (majority vote, consensus, or weighted scoring), the weight assigned to community reviewer scores relative to other reviewer scores, the process for managing conflicts of interest among community reviewers, and the compensation or support provided to community reviewers (stipends, childcare, transportation, language interpretation).

**Evidence form:** The review panel composition with reviewer names or demographic profiles, the signed agreements or commitments from community reviewers, the scoring rubric showing how community reviewer scores are weighted in the final award calculation, and for completed cycles, the record of reviewer participation and the grant award outcomes. Claimed participation structures that are not documented with panel compositions and scoring rubrics do not satisfy the operational definition of community decision-making authority.

**Conformance threshold:** A participation structure declaration that names the specific decision authority held by community reviewers (not advisory roles), the weighting in the scoring process, and the selection criteria. A program that consults with community members during program design but retains all award authority with foundation staff does not satisfy this field for programs claiming participatory grantmaking. The weighting must be quantitatively stated; "community voice is central to our process" without a stated percentage or decision rule does not satisfy the operational definition.

### Field: Racial Equity Analysis

**Criterion intent:** Establishes how the program identifies and addresses racial disparities in the outcome domain, and how the program's theory of change connects its activities to reducing those disparities, satisfying the NCRP criteria requiring racial equity analysis as a component of grantmaking practice.

**Operational definition:** A racial equity analysis is a systematic assessment of: which racial and ethnic groups experience worse outcomes in the program's domain and by how much (the disparity gap), what structural or institutional factors produce or maintain those disparities, how the program's theory of change addresses those structural factors, and how the program will measure whether racial disparities are narrowing as a result of its activities. The analysis is not a statement of intent but an evidence-grounded assessment of the current conditions and the proposed pathway to change them.

**Response form:** A racial equity analysis covering four elements: (1) the disparity baseline, stating the outcome rate or condition for each racial group in the target community with the gap between the lowest-outcome group and the overall or reference group rate; (2) the structural analysis, identifying the institutional, policy, or historical factors that produce the disparity; (3) the theory of change, explaining how the program's activities address the structural factors and through what mechanism disparity reduction is expected; (4) the equity indicators, specifying how the program will track disparity reduction over time (the equity indicators from the power-building field, applied specifically to measuring the gap over the grant period).

**Evidence form:** For the disparity baseline: administrative data, census data (ACS), or a local needs assessment with race-disaggregated figures, cited with source and date. For the structural analysis: a literature citation, a community-generated analysis, or documentation from a prior assessment. For the theory of change: a logic model or narrative that traces the connection from program activities to structural change. The analysis may be co-produced with community members and partner organizations; co-production should be noted.

**Conformance threshold:** A racial equity analysis that provides a quantified disparity baseline from a named data source, identifies at least one structural factor that the program is designed to address, and specifies at least one equity indicator that will be tracked during the grant period. An analysis that states "we serve communities of color" without quantifying disparities or naming structural factors does not satisfy this field. An analysis that names racial disparities but presents only service delivery outputs as the pathway to addressing them (without a theory connecting services to structural change) does not satisfy the theory of change element.

## Part 3: Compatibility Map

This section maps the community foundation program type to the frameworks in the CROSS+WALKRI compatibility corpus that are most directly applicable. The map is organized by primitive.

---

### Pre-specification Primitive

Community foundation programs have a less uniform pre-specification culture than research or bilateral development programs. The frameworks that have moved farthest toward pre-specification:

- **Community Foundation National Standards Board:** the National Standards for U.S. Community Foundations require a community assessment process, a grantmaking strategy that responds to community needs, and grant guidelines published before the grant cycle opens; the published guidelines are the entry specification gate document
- **CSBG ROMA (Six National Goals):** the Six National Goals for community action agencies are pre-specified at the federal level before any local program opens; local programs must map their activities to these goals and report using the National Performance Indicators; this is the strongest pre-specification structure in the community foundation sub-type
- **CROSS itself:** entry specification gate as the canonical implementation of this primitive

*Compatibility statements:* Community-Foundation-National-Standards, CSBG-ROMA-Six-National-Goals, Council-on-Foundations-Ethical-Principles.

---

### Theory of Change Hierarchy Primitive

Community foundation programs use a range of theory of change frameworks; the most commonly referenced:

- **Kellogg Logic Model:** activities and inputs through outputs to outcomes and impact; widely used in foundation grantmaking and community development; the most common theory of change tool in this program type
- **Annie E. Casey Foundation Results-Based Accountability (RBA):** distinguishes population accountability (what is the condition of the whole population?) from performance accountability (how well is the program doing?); this distinction is structurally equivalent to the CROSS distinction between goal-level indicators (population) and outcome-level indicators (program); RBA is widely used in community foundation and United Way programming

*Compatibility statements:* Kellogg-Logic-Model, Annie-E-Casey-RBA, United-Way-Global-Results-Framework.

---

### Disaggregation Primitive

The disaggregation ratchet in community foundation programs applies primarily to race and geography:

- **NCRP racial equity grantmaking criteria:** NCRP's criteria require that foundations disaggregate their grantmaking and their grantee outcome data by race; this applies both to who receives grants and to who benefits from program activities; the CROSS disaggregation ratchet formalizes the norm that race disaggregation, once adopted, is not reversible without justification
- **Equitable Evaluation Initiative:** promotes equity-centered evaluation practices including demographic disaggregation, community participation in evaluation design, and attention to who holds power in the evaluation process; the EEI framework addresses the WALKRI evidence form question from a power analysis perspective
- **SNAP-Ed equity lens:** USDA's SNAP-Ed program applies an equity lens to nutrition education programming, requiring disaggregation by race, income, and geography; a reference for community foundation programs working in food access and nutrition

*Compatibility statements:* NCRP-Racial-Equity-Criteria, Equitable-Evaluation-Initiative, SNAP-Ed-Equity-Lens.

---

### Independent Verification Primitive

Community foundation programs have weaker independent verification requirements than research or bilateral development programs, but meaningful standards exist:

- **360Giving (data transparency as a form of external verification):** 360Giving's open data standard for UK grant data enables third-party analysis of who receives grants, for what purpose, and in which geographies; public data publication is a transparency mechanism that functions as a distributed form of verification
- **Community Foundation National Standards accreditation:** the accreditation process includes a peer review of the foundation's grantmaking practices against the National Standards; accreditation status is a third-party verification of process quality

*Compatibility statements:* 360Giving-Data-Standard, Community-Foundation-National-Standards-Accreditation, Equitable-Evaluation-Initiative.

---

### Portfolio Aggregation Primitive

Community foundation programs aggregate at the portfolio level to assess whether the foundation's collective grantmaking is producing community-level change:

- **United Way Global Results Framework (network-level reporting):** United Way affiliates report against a common results framework that enables aggregation across the network; individual agency performance contributes to community-level indicators; this is the portfolio aggregation primitive applied at network scale
- **CSBG ROMA (cross-CAA comparability):** ROMA's National Performance Indicators enable aggregation across community action agencies in a state or nationally; the same indicator definitions applied consistently across programs make portfolio aggregation meaningful; CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification produces the same cross-program comparability

*Compatibility statements:* United-Way-Global-Results-Framework, CSBG-ROMA-National-Performance-Indicators, FSG-Collective-Impact.

---

### Participatory Grantmaking Primitive

Participatory grantmaking is a distinctive feature of Sub-type D programs; two external frameworks define the operational standard:

- **Participatory Budgeting 15 Key Metrics:** the participatory budgeting field has developed fifteen metrics for assessing the quality of participatory processes: representation (are decision-makers demographically representative?), power (do participants have real decision authority?), scale, and deliberation quality; these metrics provide an operational definition for the participation structure declaration in the WALKRI field
- **OECD Citizen Participation Guidelines:** the OECD's guidelines for citizen participation in public decision-making establish a spectrum from consultation through engagement to empowerment; the CROSS participation structure declaration requires a position on this spectrum to be declared before the grant cycle opens

*Compatibility statements:* Participatory-Budgeting-15-Metrics, OECD-Citizen-Participation-Guidelines, Trust-Based-Philanthropy-Project.

---

### Coherence and Power-Building Primitive

The NCRP's coherence standard for equity-focused grantmaking requires that programs assess whether their activities reinforce or undermine systemic change:

- **NCRP criteria (power-building in context of systemic change):** NCRP distinguishes service delivery (helping people navigate an inequitable system) from power-building (changing the system); programs claiming to advance racial equity through power-building must demonstrate that their theory of change connects to systemic change, not only individual-level improvement; this is the coherence criterion applied to equity strategy
- **FSG Collective Impact:** collective impact frameworks require that multiple organizations working in the same community coordinate around a common agenda, shared measurement, and mutually reinforcing activities; the CROSS concurrent funding disclosure requirement covers the resource dimension of collective impact; the FSG framework covers the strategic coordination dimension

*Compatibility statements:* NCRP-Power-Building-Criteria, FSG-Collective-Impact, Trust-Based-Philanthropy-Project.

## New Primitives Relevant to Community Foundation Programs

**The Trust-Based Philanthropy tension with pre-specification, resolved:** Trust-Based Philanthropy and CROSS's entry specification gate appear to be in conflict: TBP emphasizes reducing grantee burden and providing unrestricted support, while CROSS requires pre-specified indicators before awards are made. This bundle resolves the tension by distinguishing the level at which pre-specification applies. For TBP-aligned programs, the pre-specification is at the funder's portfolio level (what community conditions is the foundation working to change?), not at the grantee's project level (what will your specific program produce?). Unrestricted grants are recognized through the Retroactive obligation mode based on organizational contribution to those community conditions. The entry specification gate specifies the criteria for organizational recognition; the grantee is not asked to commit to project-specific indicators. This resolution is novel in the corpus and represents a genuine extension of CROSS's obligation mode architecture into the trust-based grantmaking context.

**Geographic boundary as the primary scope anchor for community foundations:** Community foundations are distinctive among grantmaking organizations in that their geographic scope is constitutive of their identity; a community foundation exists to serve a specific place. The geographic boundary is not just a targeting parameter; it is the primary definition of the accountability subject. CROSS's population scope declaration, when applied to community foundation programs, should begin with the geographic boundary as the primary anchor and derive all other scope declarations from it. This means that the geographic scope must be specified with enough precision to determine which organizations and which populations fall within the mandate, not merely stated as a region name.
